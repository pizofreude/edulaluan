## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is Bank Negara Malaysia?**

Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) is Malaysia's central bank.

It does not serve regular customers.

What it does:

* Controls how much money is in the economy
* Sets the interest rate that affects your loan and savings rates
* Makes sure banks, insurers, and money changers follow the rules
* Protects you if a licensed financial company treats you unfairly

Think of BNM as the referee of Malaysia's entire financial system.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Bank Negara Malaysia was established in 1959 under the Central Bank of Malaysia Act.

Its core mandates:

* Monetary stability — keeping inflation and the ringgit stable
* Financial system stability — ensuring banks and insurers remain solvent
* Consumer protection — enforcing fair treatment in financial services
* Payment system oversight — governing how money moves (including e-wallets and interbank transfers)

BNM does not manage the stock market. That falls under the **Securities Commission (SC)**.

## Part 1: What BNM Does

### Key Functions at a Glance

| Function                        | What It Means for You                                          |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sets Overnight Policy Rate (OPR)| Influences your loan interest rates and fixed deposit returns  |
| Issues banking licences         | Only licensed banks can legally accept deposits                |
| Supervises insurers and takaful | Licensed operators must meet solvency and conduct standards    |
| Regulates money services        | Money changers, remittance providers, e-wallet operators       |
| Manages foreign exchange        | Controls currency flows, monitors ringgit exchange rate        |
| Consumer financial education    | Runs BNMLINK, BNM TELELINK, and financial literacy initiatives |

### Institutions Under BNM Supervision

| Category                      | Examples                                              |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Commercial banks              | Maybank, CIMB, Public Bank, RHB, Hong Leong, HSBC MY  |
| Islamic banks                 | Bank Islam, Bank Muamalat, Maybank Islamic            |
| Investment banks              | CIMB Investment, Kenanga, RHB Investment              |
| Insurance companies           | AIA, Allianz, Great Eastern, Etiqa                    |
| Takaful operators             | Takaful Malaysia, Etiqa Takaful, Prudential BSN Takaful |
| Development financial institutions | Bank Rakyat, BSN, EXIM Bank, SME Bank            |
| Money services businesses     | Licensed money changers, remittance agents, e-wallet operators |

### What BNM Does NOT Regulate

| Entity                        | Regulator Instead               |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Bursa Malaysia (stock exchange) | Securities Commission (SC)    |
| Unit trust companies          | Securities Commission (SC)      |
| Cooperatives (koperasi)       | Suruhanjaya Koperasi Malaysia   |
| Moneylenders (lesen Akta Pinjaman Wang) | Ministry of Urban Wellbeing / state authorities |
| Unlicensed investment schemes | PDRM / SC / BNM (joint action)  |

## Part 2: Licensed vs Unlicensed Financial Institutions

### Why This Matters

Using an unlicensed financial provider means:

* No legal protection if things go wrong
* No avenue for formal complaint
* Possible exposure to fraud or predatory terms

### How to Verify a Licence

BNM maintains a public register of all licensed entities.

Check via: **BNM's Financial Consumer Alert list and Licensed Institutions list**

Website: **bnm.gov.my → Consumer → Check Licence**

Before engaging any:

* Bank or financial product
* Insurance or takaful plan
* Money changer or remittance service
* Investment scheme claiming BNM backing

**Always verify first.**

### Red Flags of Unlicensed Operators

| Warning Sign                                      | What It Suggests                    |
| ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Guaranteed high returns with no risk              | Likely unlicensed investment scheme |
| Cannot show a BNM or SC licence number            | Not regulated                       |
| Pressures you to recruit others                   | Possible pyramid or Ponzi structure |
| Operates only via WhatsApp or Telegram            | No formal business registration     |
| Claims to be "partnered with BNM"                 | BNM does not partner with investment operators |

BNM's **Financial Consumer Alert (FCA) list** names entities flagged as unauthorised or suspicious.

Check: **bnm.gov.my/financial-consumer-alert**

## Part 3: Consumer Rights and Complaints

### Your Rights as a Financial Consumer

Under BNM's Financial Services Act 2013 and related frameworks, licensed institutions must:

* Disclose all fees, charges, and terms clearly
* Handle complaints fairly and within set timeframes
* Not engage in misleading or unfair practices
* Protect your personal financial data

### Complaint Pathway (Summary)

For full step-by-step escalation, see **Section 9.8.2 — Financial Services Termination and Complaints**.

Quick reference:

```
1 Raise with institution's complaints unit (not general customer service)
2 If unresolved → escalate to Ombudsman for Financial Services (OFS)
3 For systemic issues or misconduct → BNM TELELINK
```

| Contact             | Purpose                                              |
| ------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| BNM TELELINK        | Financial consumer enquiries and complaints          |
| Email               | bnmtelelink@bnm.gov.my                               |
| Phone               | 1-300-88-5465                                        |
| Ombudsman for Financial Services (OFS) | Independent dispute resolution for consumers |
| OFS Website         | ofs.org.my                                           |

**Important:** BNM TELELINK handles enquiries and escalation. It does not replace the institution's own complaints unit as the first step.

## Part 4: Exchange Rates and Monetary Policy Basics

### The Overnight Policy Rate (OPR)

The OPR is the benchmark interest rate set by BNM's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC).

It meets approximately 6 times per year.

How it affects you:

| OPR Movement     | Effect on Borrowers             | Effect on Savers               |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| OPR rises        | Loan instalments increase       | Fixed deposit and savings rates improve |
| OPR falls        | Loan instalments decrease       | Savings returns fall           |
| OPR held steady  | Rates remain unchanged          | Rates remain unchanged         |

Variable rate loans (most home loans in Malaysia use Base Rate + Spread) are directly affected by OPR changes.

### The Ringgit (MYR)

BNM manages the ringgit but does not peg it to a fixed rate.

MYR is a managed float — its value is influenced by:

* Trade balances
* Foreign investment flows
* Global commodity prices (particularly oil)
* US dollar and regional currency movements

BNM intervenes in foreign exchange markets when ringgit volatility is excessive.

### Foreign Currency Controls

Malaysia has selective foreign exchange controls under the Financial Services Act 2013.

Key rules affecting individuals and businesses:

| Situation                              | Rule                                            |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Sending money abroad (individuals)     | Generally allowed; large amounts may require declaration |
| Business foreign currency accounts     | Permitted with conditions for resident companies |
| Borrowing in foreign currency          | Limits apply for residents                      |
| Converting large amounts               | May require documentation of underlying transaction |

For current thresholds: **bnm.gov.my → Foreign Exchange Policy**

## Practical Habits for Financial Consumers

Low-risk financial behaviour includes:

* Verifying any financial institution or scheme before engaging
* Reading terms and conditions on loans, insurance, and investments
* Understanding whether your complaint should go to the institution, OFS, or BNM TELELINK
* Checking the OPR direction before locking into fixed or variable rate products
* Not assuming "licensed by BNM" applies to all financial apps or platforms — verify individually

---

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Unlicensed investment scheme**

A retiree invested savings into a scheme claiming BNM-licensed status. BNM does not license investment operators — only banks and insurers. The scheme collapsed. Because it was unlicensed, there was no regulatory protection or formal avenue for recovery.

**Scenario 2: OPR rise impact on home loan**

A homeowner on a variable rate loan saw instalments increase twice in one year following consecutive OPR hikes. Understanding how OPR affects Base Rate would have helped them evaluate fixed vs variable at the point of application.

**Scenario 3: Complaint escalated correctly**

A customer's insurance claim was declined. Instead of calling general customer service, they submitted a formal complaint to the insurer's complaints unit. When unsatisfied, they escalated to OFS. The dispute was resolved in the customer's favour within 90 days.

**Step 1:** Contact the institution's dedicated complaints unit (not general customer service).

**Step 2:** If unresolved within a reasonable period → escalate to OFS at ofs.org.my.

**Step 3:** For systemic misconduct or if OFS is not applicable → contact BNM TELELINK.

Your loan rate is typically: **Base Rate (BR) + Spread**

Check your loan agreement for your current BR and spread.

Monitor BNM MPC announcements (6 per year) to anticipate changes.

## FAQ

**Q: Is BNM the same as a commercial bank?**
No. BNM is the central bank — it regulates commercial banks but does not offer retail banking services to the public.

**Q: Does BNM regulate e-wallets like TNG or GrabPay?**
Yes, for their money services functions. E-wallet operators must hold a BNM licence under the Financial Services Act 2013.

**Q: If my bank goes bankrupt, is my money protected?**
Deposits in licensed banks are protected up to RM 250,000 per depositor per bank by **PIDM (Perbadanan Insurans Deposit Malaysia)**.

**Q: Does BNM regulate unit trusts or stocks?**
No. Those fall under the Securities Commission (SC).

**Q: How do I know if an investment scheme is legitimate?**
Check both the BNM Financial Consumer Alert list and the SC's Investor Alert list. Legitimate investment operators are licensed by the SC, not BNM.
