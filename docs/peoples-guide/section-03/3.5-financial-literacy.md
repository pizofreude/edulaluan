## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**Why does managing money matter?**

Money you earn only helps if you control where it goes.

Think of it this way:

* **Budgeting** = knowing how much comes in and where it goes out
* **Emergency fund** = money set aside for surprises (job loss, hospital, car repair)
* **Debt** = borrowed money that costs more over time if you ignore it
* **Saving and investing** = growing money so it works for you

You do not need to be rich to start.

Small habits compound into big results.

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Personal financial literacy is the ability to:

* Understand income and expenses
* Make informed decisions about saving and spending
* Avoid debt traps
* Build long-term financial resilience

Malaysia's economic landscape includes:

* Wide income disparity across B40, M40, and T20 groups
* High household debt relative to income
* Strong government-linked savings infrastructure (EPF, Tabung Haji, Amanah Saham)
* Growing fintech tools accessible to all income groups

This page gives you a practical starting framework.

## Part 1: Budgeting Basics

### What Is a Budget?

A budget is a plan for your money before the month begins.

Without one, spending tends to fill available income.

### Common Budgeting Methods

| Method         | How It Works                                                          | Best For                            |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| 50/30/20 Rule  | 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings or debt repayment                  | Steady income earners               |
| Zero-Based     | Every ringgit is assigned a job. Income minus all allocations = 0    | Detail-oriented or irregular income |
| Pay Yourself First | Move savings out first, spend the rest                            | Beginners who struggle to save      |
| Envelope Method | Divide cash into physical or digital envelopes per category         | Overspenders who need hard limits   |

### Malaysian Context

Malaysian ringgit stretches differently depending on:

* Location (Klang Valley vs East Malaysia vs smaller towns)
* Family obligations (contributing to parents, siblings)
* Commute costs (car ownership vs public transport)

There is no single budget ratio that works for everyone.

**The goal: spend intentionally, not by default.**

## Part 2: Emergency Fund

### Why It Matters

An emergency fund is not a luxury. It is a buffer.

Without it, one unexpected event forces you to:

* Borrow from family
* Take personal loans at high interest
* Use credit cards and accumulate debt

### How Much Should You Save?

| Situation                          | Recommended Target         |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Single, no dependants              | 3 months of expenses       |
| Married, dual income               | 3–4 months of expenses     |
| Single income household            | 6 months of expenses       |
| Freelancer or irregular income     | 6–9 months of expenses     |
| Caregiver or health conditions     | 9–12 months of expenses    |

### Where to Keep It

Emergency funds should be:

* Liquid (accessible within 1–3 days)
* Separate from your spending account
* Not invested in volatile assets

| Option                  | Provider Examples                         | Notes                                  |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| High-yield savings      | CIMB FastSaver, MAE Savings               | Better than standard savings rate      |
| ASB / ASN               | Amanah Saham Bumiputera, Amanah Saham Nasional | Stable dividend, liquid withdrawal |
| Money Market Fund       | Versa, StashAway Simple                   | Higher returns, still liquid           |
| Regular savings account | Any local bank                            | Convenient but lowest return           |

**Do not use Tabung Haji or fixed deposits for emergency funds** — withdrawals are restricted or penalised.

## Part 3: Debt Management

### Types of Debt in Malaysia

| Debt Type         | Examples                                | Risk Level   |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------ |
| Secured debt      | Home loan, car loan                     | Moderate     |
| Unsecured debt    | Personal loan, credit card balance      | High         |
| Study loan        | PTPTN                                   | Low interest, manageable |
| Informal debt     | Ah Long (illegal lenders), family loans | Very high risk |

### Warning Signs of Debt Trouble

* Using one credit card to pay another
* Minimum payment only on credit cards for months
* Borrowing to cover everyday expenses
* Monthly debt repayment exceeds 40% of income (CCRIS risk threshold)

### Debt Repayment Strategies

| Strategy        | How It Works                                              | Best For                         |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Avalanche       | Pay highest interest debt first                           | Minimising total interest paid   |
| Snowball        | Pay smallest balance first for quick wins                 | Motivation and behaviour change  |
| Consolidation   | Combine multiple debts into one lower-rate loan           | Managing multiple high-rate debts |

### Malaysian Debt Relief Resources

| Resource      | What It Offers                                                |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| AKPK (Credit Counselling and Debt Management Agency) | Free counselling, debt restructuring programmes |
| PTPTN rescheduling | Options for borrowers facing repayment difficulty        |
| Bank Negara TELELINK | Guidance on financial hardship with licensed institutions |

AKPK is free, government-backed, and confidential. There is no shame in using it.

## Part 4: Savings and Investment Intro

### The Savings Ladder

Think of building wealth in stages:

```
Stage 1: Emergency fund (3–6 months)
Stage 2: Settle high-interest debt
Stage 3: Contribute to EPF (voluntary top-up via i-Saraan or i-Lindung if applicable)
Stage 4: Low-risk savings products (ASB, Tabung Haji)
Stage 5: Diversified investments (unit trusts, ETFs, stocks)
```

Do not skip stages.

### EPF Voluntary Contributions

| Scheme        | Who It Is For                              | Notes                                        |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| i-Saraan      | Self-employed, freelancers, gig workers    | Government incentive included                |
| i-Lindung     | Self-employed (includes basic insurance)   | EPF + SOCSO combination                     |
| Voluntary top-up | Salaried workers wanting to contribute more | Above mandatory deduction             |

EPF dividends are historically 5–6% annually, tax-exempt, and compounding.

### Low-Risk Savings Products

| Product            | Provider                | Open To          | Notes                                         |
| ------------------ | ----------------------- | ---------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| ASB                | Amanah Saham Bumiputera | Bumiputera only  | Stable dividend, principal guaranteed         |
| ASN / AS 1Malaysia | Amanah Saham Nasional   | All Malaysians   | Similar structure to ASB                      |
| Tabung Haji        | Lembaga Tabung Haji     | Muslims only     | Savings for Haj, consistent returns           |
| Fixed Deposit      | All commercial banks    | All Malaysians   | Guaranteed return, locked period              |

### Starting Points for Investing

For Malaysians new to investing:

| Platform      | Type                   | Minimum    | Notes                                       |
| ------------- | ---------------------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Versa         | Money market / bonds   | RM 1       | Good starting point, low volatility         |
| StashAway     | Robo-advisor           | RM 0       | Diversified ETF portfolios, auto-rebalancing |
| Wahed Invest  | Islamic robo-advisor   | RM 10      | Shariah-compliant portfolios                |
| MyTHEO        | Robo-advisor           | RM 0       | Japan-backed, globally diversified          |
| Bursa Malaysia (via broker) | Stocks / ETFs | Varies | Direct market access, higher involvement required |

**Investing carries risk.** Start only after you have an emergency fund and managed high-interest debt.

## Practical Financial Habits

Financially stable Malaysians typically:

* Review income and expenses monthly
* Keep emergency fund in a separate account
* Avoid carrying a credit card balance month to month
* Contribute to EPF beyond the mandatory minimum when possible
* Ignore lifestyle inflation after income increases

Financial health is a habit, not a milestone.

---

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: No emergency fund**

A factory worker lost his job unexpectedly and had no savings buffer. He took a personal loan at 18% interest to cover rent, deepening his financial difficulty. Three months of emergency savings would have changed his options.

**Scenario 2: Credit card minimum payments**

A graduate paid only the minimum on a RM 5,000 credit card balance for two years. By the time she cleared it, she had paid almost RM 2,000 in interest alone.

**Scenario 3: Early EPF top-up**

A freelance designer contributed RM 200/month voluntarily via i-Saraan starting at 28. By 40, she had significantly more in EPF than peers who started at 35, due to compounding.

## Full Wizard Content (Modal / Sidebar)

### Path A: I have never budgeted before

**Step 1 of 3: Track for one month**

Do not change spending yet.

Record every transaction for 30 days.

Use bank statements or a free app (MAE, Money Lover).

**Step 2 of 3: Categorise**

Group spending into:

* Fixed (rent, loan repayment, utilities)
* Variable essentials (groceries, transport)
* Discretionary (eating out, subscriptions, shopping)

**Step 3 of 3: Assign limits**

Use 50/30/20 as a starting point.

Adjust based on your actual life.

## FAQ

**Q: I earn minimum wage. Is financial planning still relevant?**
Yes. Low income makes financial planning more important, not less. AKPK and EPF i-Saraan are specifically designed for lower-income groups.

**Q: Is ASB only for Bumiputera?**
Yes. Non-Bumiputera can use ASN, AS 1Malaysia, or similar Amanah Saham products instead.

**Q: Is robo-investing safe?**
Licensed platforms like StashAway and Wahed are regulated by the Securities Commission. They carry market risk, not fraud risk.

**Q: Should I invest or pay off debt first?**
Clear high-interest debt first (credit cards, personal loans). Low-interest debt (PTPTN, home loan) can coexist with investing.

**Q: Can I withdraw ASB or ASN any time?**
Yes, withdrawals are generally flexible. Tabung Haji allows withdrawals but has rules around Haj-related savings.
