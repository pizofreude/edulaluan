## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is PDRM?**
PDRM stands for Polis Diraja Malaysia, or the Royal Malaysian Police. They are the people in blue uniforms who keep us safe. They stop crime, help during accidents, and make sure everyone follows the rules.

**When should you call the police?**
- Someone is in danger or being hurt.
- You see a crime happening.
- There has been an accident.
- Something has been stolen from you.
- You feel unsafe and need help.

**What number do you call?**
Call **999**. It is free and available 24 hours a day.

**What do you say?**
Tell them:
1. Your name.
2. Where you are (full address or nearby landmark).
3. What is happening.
4. How many people are involved.

**Stay calm and wait for the police to arrive.**
Do not try to stop a criminal yourself. Your safety comes first.

### 🧑 Detailed

**Overview**

The Royal Malaysian Police (Polis Diraja Malaysia) operates under the Ministry of Home Affairs. PDRM is responsible for crime prevention and investigation, traffic enforcement, public order, border security, and community policing. It operates through several divisions including the Criminal Investigation Department (CID), the Commercial Crime Investigation Department (CCID), the Narcotics Crime Investigation Department (NCID), and the Traffic Investigation and Enforcement Department (TIED).

**Full Scope of PDRM Services:**
- Emergency response: crimes in progress, accidents, public disturbances.
- Crime reporting and investigation: theft, assault, fraud, cybercrime.
- Police reports: for insurance, employment, travel, and legal purposes.
- Traffic enforcement: summons, accident investigation, roadblocks.
- Permits and licensing: firearms, public events, demonstrations.
- Community policing: Skim Rondaan Sukarela (SRS), Community Policing.
- Immigration enforcement: working alongside Jabatan Imigresen.

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Ahmad's stolen motorcycle**
Ahmad returned to the parking lot and found his motorcycle missing. He was unsure whether to file a report online or walk in to the station. Because vehicle theft requires a physical inspection reference number for insurance, he needed to walk in. The report was processed in under 30 minutes. Without the report, his insurance claim would have been rejected.

**Scenario 2: Minor accident on the LDP**
Siti and another driver had a minor collision. Both cars had damage but no injuries. Siti assumed they could settle privately. However, her insurance company later required an official police report to process the claim. She had to file a report three days later, which complicated the investigation timeline. Always make a report immediately after any accident, no matter how minor.

**Scenario 3: Event organiser applying for a police permit**
A community organisation in Petaling Jaya wanted to hold an outdoor charity event with an expected crowd of 500 people. Under the Peaceful Assembly Act 2012, organisers must notify the police at least 5 days before the event. They submitted the notification to the nearest OCPD (Officer in Charge of Police District) and received acknowledgement without issues.

**Scenario 4: Online fraud report**
Hafizah received a fake job offer and was asked to pay RM300 for a "registration kit." She paid, and the company disappeared. This falls under commercial crime. She filed an online report via sso.rmp.gov.my and was given a report number the same day. The case was referred to CCID for follow-up.

**Q: What do you need help with?**
- There is a crime happening right now

- I need a police report for insurance or other purposes
- I want to check or pay a traffic summons

---

**Path A: There is a crime happening right now**

Step 1 of 3: Call 999 immediately.
- State your location clearly with the nearest landmark.
- Describe what is happening and how many people are involved.
- Stay on the line if it is safe to do so.

Step 2 of 3: Do not intervene physically.
- Your safety comes first.
- If possible, observe and remember details: clothing, vehicle plate numbers, physical descriptions.

Step 3 of 3: Stay at the scene until police arrive.
- Unless staying puts you in danger.
- You may be asked to give a witness statement.

---

**Path B: I want to report a crime that already happened**

Step 1 of 3: Determine if you can report online.
- Eligible cases for online reporting include: lost items (no suspect), online fraud, and nuisance calls.
- Online report portal: sso.rmp.gov.my.
- Cases involving suspects, violence, or insurance claims require a walk-in report.

Step 2 of 3: For walk-in reports, go to the nearest police station.
- Bring your MyKad or valid ID.
- Bring any evidence: screenshots, receipts, photos, videos.
- The report is free and typically processed within 30 to 60 minutes.

Step 3 of 3: Keep your report number safe.
- You will need this for insurance claims, legal proceedings, or follow-up enquiries.
- You can follow up on your case status at the same station or via the PDRM portal.

---

**Path C: I was in a road accident**

Step 1 of 4: Check for injuries first.
- Call 999 if anyone is injured.
- Call 999 or 03-2112 2222 if the accident is blocking traffic.

Step 2 of 4: Do not move the vehicles until police arrive, if possible.
- Take photos of the scene, vehicle positions, and damage from multiple angles.
- Note the other driver's name, IC number, vehicle plate, and insurance details.

Step 3 of 4: File a police report within 24 hours.
- Walk-in to the nearest police station.
- Both drivers should file separate reports.
- Bring your driving licence, IC, and vehicle registration card (geran).

Step 4 of 4: Submit the report number to your insurance company.
- Most insurers require the report within 24 to 48 hours of the accident.
- Failure to report promptly may affect your claim eligibility.

---

**Path D: I need a police report for insurance or other purposes**

Step 1 of 2: Walk in to the nearest police station with your IC.
- Common reasons: lost MyKad, lost passport, lost documents, employment background check.
- For lost documents, you will be asked to make a statutory declaration (akuan bersumpah).

Step 2 of 2: Collect your certified copy of the report.
- Some institutions require a certified true copy stamped by the station.
- Ask for this at the counter when filing the report.

---

**Path E: I want to check or pay a traffic summons**

Step 1 of 1: Use one of these official channels.
- MyBayar app or portal: mybayar.hasil.gov.my
- Pos Malaysia counter: walk-in with IC and vehicle plate number.
- JPJ counter: for summons issued by JPJ specifically.
- PDRM summons can also be paid at the police station directly.
- Discounts on traffic summons are offered periodically. Check the PDRM or MyBayar portal for active campaigns.

---

**Path F: I need a permit for a public event**

Step 1 of 2: Submit a notification to the nearest OCPD at least 5 days before the event.
- Required under the Peaceful Assembly Act 2012.
- Include: event details, expected attendance, location, organiser contact.

Step 2 of 2: For other permits such as firearms or special event licences, visit rmp.gov.my for the specific application form and requirements.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I file a police report online for any type of case?**
No. Online reporting via sso.rmp.gov.my is limited to selected cases such as lost items with no known suspect, online fraud, and nuisance communications. Cases involving physical crimes, suspects, accidents, or insurance claims require a walk-in report at a police station.

**Q: What if the police do not act on my report?**
Your report creates an official record regardless of immediate action. If you feel your case is not being addressed, you can escalate to the OCS (Officer in Charge of Station), the OCPD, or the PDRM Integrity and Standards Compliance Department (JIPS) at 03-2266 4272. You may also contact Suhakam (Human Rights Commission of Malaysia) if your rights are involved.

**Q: How long does a walk-in police report take?**
A straightforward report typically takes 20 to 45 minutes. Bring your IC and all relevant evidence to avoid delays. Complex cases may require a follow-up visit for a full statement.

**Q: Do I need a lawyer when making a police report?**
No. Making a police report does not require legal representation. However, if the case proceeds to prosecution or civil action, consulting a lawyer is advisable. Legal aid is available through Biro Bantuan Guaman (BBG) for those who qualify.

**Q: Can a foreigner or non-citizen make a police report in Malaysia?**
Yes. Anyone in Malaysia has the right to make a police report regardless of nationality. Bring your passport or valid identification document.
