## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is an ambulance for?**
An ambulance is a special vehicle that brings medical help to you fast. The people inside are trained to give you medical care before you reach the hospital.

**When should you call an ambulance?**
- Someone has fainted or is unconscious.
- Someone cannot breathe properly.
- Someone is having a heart attack or stroke.
- Someone is badly injured from an accident.
- Someone is in severe pain and cannot be moved safely.

**What number do you call?**
Call **999**. It is free. It works 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

**What do you say?**
Tell them:
1. Your name.
2. Where you are (full address or nearest landmark).
3. What happened and how the person looks right now.
4. Whether the person is conscious and breathing.

**While waiting for the ambulance:**
- Keep the person still and calm.
- Do not give food or water.
- Do not move them unless there is immediate danger.
- Stay on the line with the operator if possible.

### 🧑 Detailed

**Overview**

Ambulance services in Malaysia are provided by a mix of government, non-governmental, and private operators. The primary emergency number is 999, which dispatches the nearest available ambulance. Government ambulance services are free of charge and funded by public taxes. Non-governmental organisations (NGOs) such as St. John Ambulance of Malaysia (SJAM) and the Malaysian Red Crescent (MRC) supplement government capacity, especially during mass casualty events and disasters. Private ambulance providers handle non-emergency transfers and outstation trips.

**When to use government vs private ambulance:**

| Situation | Recommended Service |
|---|---|
| Life-threatening emergency | Call 999. Government or SJAM/MRC dispatched. |
| Non-emergency hospital transfer | Private ambulance (SJAM, First Ambulance, MMR) |
| Outstation medical transport | Private ambulance with advance booking |
| Mass casualty or disaster | 999 or 991 (Civil Defence coordination) |
| Event medical standby | SJAM or MRC on prior arrangement |

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Encik Subramaniam collapses at home**
Encik Subramaniam, 67, collapsed in his living room. His wife panicked and considered driving him to the hospital herself. His son stopped her and called 999 instead. The operator guided them through basic first aid while the ambulance was dispatched. Moving a stroke or cardiac patient incorrectly can cause further damage. The ambulance arrived in 12 minutes and the paramedics stabilised him en route to the hospital.

**Scenario 2: Road accident on the North-South Expressway**
A multi-vehicle accident occurred near Rawang. A witness called 999 and specified multiple injured persons. The operator coordinated PDRM, BOMBA, and ambulance dispatch simultaneously. Bystanders were advised not to move the injured until paramedics arrived. This single call triggered the full emergency response chain.

**Scenario 3: Non-emergency transfer for Puan Halimah**
Puan Halimah needed to be transferred from a government hospital in Ipoh to a specialist centre in KL for a planned procedure. Her family arranged a private ambulance through First Ambulance with 48 hours advance notice. The cost was approximately RM420 for the night transfer, inclusive of paramedic escort and medical equipment.

**Scenario 4: Charity run medical standby**
An NGO organising a 10km charity run in Putrajaya contacted SJAM two weeks in advance to request ambulance standby for the event. SJAM provided two ambulances and a first aid team at the venue. This service is available for public events at a scheduled rate.

**Path A: Someone needs emergency medical help right now**

Step 1 of 4: Call 999 immediately.
- Speak clearly. Do not hang up unless told to.
- State your full address and the nearest landmark.
- Describe the patient's condition: conscious or not, breathing or not, visible injuries.

Step 2 of 4: Follow the operator's instructions.
- You may be guided through CPR or basic first aid over the phone.
- Do not put the patient in a car and drive unless instructed and the hospital is closer than the ambulance ETA.

Step 3 of 4: Keep the patient still and comfortable.
- Loosen tight clothing.
- Do not give food, water, or medication unless instructed.
- If the patient is unconscious and breathing, place them in the recovery position (on their side).

Step 4 of 4: Send someone to meet the ambulance at the entrance.
- Unlock gates or barriers.
- Have the patient's IC, medication list, and any known medical history ready to hand to the paramedics.

Step 1 of 2: Contact a provider with advance notice of at least a few hours.

Step 2 of 2: Prepare the following information when calling:
- Patient's name, age, and condition.
- Pickup location and destination.
- Required equipment (oxygen, stretcher, wheelchair).
- Preferred time and date.
- Your contact number for confirmation.

---

**Path C: I need an outstation ambulance trip**

Step 1 of 1: Contact First Ambulance or SJAM at least 24 to 48 hours in advance.
- Outstation trips require advance booking and route confirmation.
- Costs vary by distance, time of day, and equipment needed.
- Request a written quotation before confirming.

---

**Path D: I want to arrange event medical standby**

Step 1 of 2: Contact SJAM or Malaysian Red Crescent at least 2 weeks before the event.
- Provide: event type, expected attendance, venue address, duration.
- Both organisations offer standby services for community and corporate events.

Step 2 of 2: Confirm equipment and personnel requirements.
- Minimum recommendation: one ambulance and two trained first aiders per 500 attendees.
- Higher risk events (sports, outdoor, large crowds) should request additional units.

## FAQ

**Q: Is calling an ambulance via 999 free?**
Yes. Government ambulance dispatch via 999 is free. You will not be charged for the ambulance ride to a government hospital. Private and NGO ambulances charge based on distance, time, and equipment used.

**Q: Should I drive the patient myself or wait for an ambulance?**
For serious conditions such as heart attack, stroke, severe bleeding, or loss of consciousness, always wait for the ambulance. Paramedics provide life-saving care en route. Driving an unstable patient yourself can worsen their condition and delay proper treatment.

**Q: What if the ambulance takes too long?**
If you feel the response is critically delayed, call 999 again and update the operator. You may also call SJAM or MRC directly as a backup. In rural areas, BOMBA may also provide emergency medical first response while the ambulance is in transit.

**Q: Can I request a specific hospital when calling 999?**
You can state your preference, but the dispatcher will direct the ambulance to the nearest appropriate facility based on the patient's condition and hospital capacity. In critical cases, the paramedic team makes the final call.

**Q: Are private ambulance costs covered by insurance?**
Some private medical insurance and takaful plans cover ambulance costs. Check your policy under "emergency transport" or "ambulance benefit." Government hospital ambulances are free and do not require an insurance claim.
