## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is a disaster?**
A disaster is something dangerous that happens suddenly and affects many people at once. In Malaysia, the most common disasters are floods, landslides, and sometimes earthquakes.

**What should you do if a disaster happens?**
- Listen to your parents or guardians.
- Go to a safe place away from water, mud, or damaged buildings.
- Do not go near flooded roads or rivers.
- Call 999 if someone is in danger.

**How do you prepare before a disaster?**
- Keep a small bag ready with important things: IC, medicine, water, a torch, and extra clothes.
- Know the address of your nearest relief centre (Pusat Pemindahan).
- Save emergency numbers in your phone.

**Important numbers:**
- Emergency: **999**
- Civil Defence: **991**
- NADMA: 03-8870 0150

### 🧑 Detailed

**Overview**

Malaysia is highly vulnerable to hydro-meteorological disasters, particularly floods and landslides during the two monsoon seasons (Northeast Monsoon from November to March, Southwest Monsoon from May to September). Earthquakes affect primarily Sabah and Sarawak due to proximity to the Pacific Ring of Fire. The National Disaster Management Agency (NADMA) under the Prime Minister's Department coordinates all national-level disaster response, working alongside state-level Majlis Keselamatan Negara (MKN) committees, JKM, JPAM, BOMBA, PDRM, and the Malaysian Armed Forces (ATM).

**Types of disasters covered in this page:**

| Disaster | Highest Risk Areas | Season |
|---|---|---|
| Monsoon floods | Kelantan, Terengganu, Pahang, Johor, Sabah | Nov to March |
| Flash floods | All urban areas, KL, Penang, JB | Year-round |
| Landslides | Cameron Highlands, Genting, Ampang, Sabah | During heavy rain |
| Earthquakes | Sabah (Ranau fault line), Sarawak | Year-round |
| Tsunamis | Penang, Kedah, Perlis coastline | Low frequency, high risk |

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Mak Cik Zainab and the Kelantan flood**
Mak Cik Zainab lives in Kuala Krai, Kelantan. Every year she experiences flooding. In 2021, floodwaters rose faster than expected. She had no go-bag prepared and wasted precious time gathering documents and medication. Her neighbour had a go-bag ready and evacuated within 10 minutes. The difference between a prepared and unprepared household during a flood can determine whether you leave safely or need to be rescued.

**Scenario 2: Landslide blocking a highland road**
A landslide blocked the main road to Cameron Highlands during heavy rain. Tourists were stranded. The Pahang State Disaster Management Committee (JPBN) coordinated with BOMBA, JKR, and the Malaysian Army to clear debris and evacuate those stranded. No single agency acts alone during disasters. Knowing who coordinates what helps you understand where to seek help.

**Scenario 3: Applying for Bantuan Bencana after a flood**
After the 2022 Klang Valley floods, thousands of households applied for Bantuan Bencana through JKM. Families who had their IC, land title or tenancy agreement, and damage photos ready processed their applications faster. Those without documentation faced delays. Keeping copies of important documents in a waterproof folder or cloud storage is critical.

**Scenario 4: Earthquake in Ranau, Sabah**
A 5.9 magnitude earthquake struck Ranau in 2015, killing 18 people on Mount Kinabalu. Residents who knew to shelter under sturdy furniture and stay away from windows reduced their injury risk. Aftershocks continued for days. Knowing the difference between a local tremor and a destructive earthquake helps avoid panic-driven decisions that cause more harm.

**Path A: I received a flood or disaster warning**

Step 1 of 4: Check the official warning level for your area.
- NADMA issues four alert levels: Awas (Alert), Berjaga (Standby), Amaran (Warning), and Bencana (Disaster).
- Monitor updates via MySejahtera, Maklum app, RTM Radio, and NADMA's official social media.

Step 2 of 4: Prepare your go-bag immediately.
- IC and important documents (in a waterproof folder or ziploc bag).
- At least 3 days of medication for anyone with chronic illness.
- Phone charger and power bank.
- Torch and extra batteries.
- Change of clothes and basic toiletries.
- Cash in small denominations.
- 3 litres of drinking water per person.

Step 3 of 4: Identify your nearest Pusat Pemindahan (relief centre).
- Contact your local MPKK (Village Community Management Council) or check with your local council website.
- Schools and community halls are typically activated as relief centres.

Step 4 of 4: Move early. Do not wait for water to enter your home.
- Inform your neighbours, especially elderly residents who may need help.
- Switch off gas, electricity, and water at the mains before leaving.

---

**Path B: I am currently in a flood or disaster situation**

Step 1 of 4: Call 999 if anyone is in immediate danger.
- Specify: flood rescue, trapped persons, landslide, or structural collapse.
- State your exact location and number of people needing rescue.

Step 2 of 4: Move to higher ground immediately.
- Do not attempt to cross flooded roads on foot or by vehicle.
- 15cm of fast-moving water can knock an adult off their feet.
- 30cm of water is enough to sweep a small car off the road.

Step 3 of 4: If you cannot evacuate, move to the highest floor of your building.
- Signal for help using a bright cloth, torch, or whistle from a window.
- Do not enter the attic unless you have a way to break through the roof.

Step 4 of 4: During an earthquake.
- Drop, cover, and hold on.
- Get under a sturdy table or desk. Cover your head and neck.
- Stay away from windows, shelves, and heavy objects.
- Do not run outside during shaking. Most injuries occur from falling debris when people try to flee.
- After shaking stops, evacuate calmly to an open area away from buildings.

---

**Path C: The disaster has passed and I need help**

Step 1 of 3: Do not return home until authorities declare it safe.
- Floodwater contains sewage, chemicals, and debris. It is a health hazard.
- Landslide-affected areas remain unstable for days after rain stops.

Step 2 of 3: Document all damage before cleaning up.
- Take photos and videos of every damaged item and structural damage.
- This is required for insurance claims and aid applications.

Step 3 of 3: Contact the following for immediate needs.
- Food, shelter, and basic necessities: nearest relief centre or JKM at 03-8000 8000.
- Medical assistance: nearest Klinik Kesihatan or government hospital.
- Emotional support: Talian Kasih 15999 (24-hour counselling hotline).

---

**Path D: I want to apply for disaster relief aid (Bantuan Bencana)**

Step 1 of 3: Confirm your eligibility.
- Your home must be officially affected and recorded by the district disaster management committee (JKDBN).
- You must be a Malaysian citizen.
- Aid is disbursed per household, not per individual.

Step 2 of 3: Prepare your documents.
- IC of all household members.
- Proof of address: utility bill, land title, or tenancy agreement.
- Damage photos (taken before cleaning up).
- Bank account number for direct disbursement.

Step 3 of 3: Submit your application.
- Walk-in to your nearest JKM office.
- Alternatively, JKM officers are typically deployed to major relief centres during active disasters.
- Aid types include: immediate cash assistance, household item replacement, and house repair grants.

---

**Path E: I want to prepare before a disaster happens**

Step 1 of 3: Build your household emergency kit.
- Go-bag contents listed in Path A above.
- Keep it near your front door or in an easy-to-grab location.

Step 2 of 3: Know your household emergency plan.
- Assign roles: who carries the go-bag, who helps elderly members, who switches off utilities.
- Agree on a meeting point outside your home if members are separated.
- Save all emergency numbers in every household member's phone.

Step 3 of 3: Register with your local MPKK or community leader (Ketua Kampung).
- Registration ensures rescue teams know your household exists and can prioritise those with mobility limitations, medical needs, or young children.

## FAQ

**Q: Who coordinates disaster response in Malaysia, BOMBA or NADMA?**
NADMA coordinates the national disaster management framework and activates the multi-agency response. BOMBA leads search and rescue operations on the ground. PDRM handles crowd control and evacuation enforcement. JKM manages relief centres and welfare. All agencies report upward to the district, state, and national disaster management committees.

**Q: Who qualifies for Bantuan Bencana?**
Malaysian citizens whose homes are officially recorded as affected by a declared disaster are eligible. The district disaster management committee (JKDBN) maintains the official affected household list. Renters and homeowners both qualify as long as they can prove residency.

**Q: Is RELA the same as JPAM (APM)?**
No. RELA (Ikatan Relawan Rakyat) is a civilian volunteer corps under KDN focused on community safety and public order. APM (Angkatan Pertahanan Awam Malaysia) is the Civil Defence Force with a specific mandate for disaster preparedness, emergency response, and civil protection. Both are covered in detail in Page 1.5. They often work together during large-scale disasters.

**Q: Can I get compensation for flood damage to my house or vehicle?**
If you have a comprehensive insurance or takaful policy that includes flood coverage, yes. Standard third-party vehicle insurance does not cover flood damage. Check your policy under "act of God" or "natural disaster" clauses. For uninsured households, Bantuan Bencana from JKM covers basic replacement of essential items and minor repairs.

**Q: What should I do if I experience earthquake tremors but no official warning is issued?**
Move away from windows and heavy objects. If shaking is strong, drop, cover, and hold on. After shaking stops, evacuate to an open area. Check NADMA and the Meteorological Department (MetMalaysia) social media for official updates. Do not re-enter buildings until they are declared structurally safe.
