## For Kids 👦👧

### Money Is Everywhere — Let's Understand It

You see money every day. Your parents use it at the supermarket. You might get some for your birthday or Hari Raya. You might buy something at the school canteen.

But what actually **is** money? And why does it matter so much?

Let's find out. 💰

---

### What Is Money?

Long ago, people traded things directly.

If you had rice and needed fish, you would find someone with fish who wanted rice — and swap. This is called **bartering**.

The problem? What if the person with fish did not want rice? Or what if you needed something from someone far away?

Money solved this problem.

**Money is something everyone agrees has value** — so you can use it to get anything you need without having to find the perfect swap.

In Malaysia, our money is called the **Ringgit (RM)**. The coins we use are called **sen**.

---

### Where Does Money Come From?

Money does not appear from nowhere. People **earn** it by doing work.

Your parents go to work and receive a **salary** — money paid for the work they do.

Some people run businesses and earn money by selling things or services.

Other ways people earn money:
* Working a job (salary or wages)
* Running a business
* Farming and selling crops
* Creating things and selling them (art, food, crafts)

**The important thing:** Money represents work and value that has already been done or created.

---

### What Can You Do With Money?

There are **four main things** you can do with money:

```
1. SPEND it — buy things you need or want right now
2. SAVE it — keep it for later
3. INVEST it — put it somewhere it can grow
4. GIVE it — donate to help others
```

Most people do all four — but in different amounts at different times of their life.

---

### Needs vs Wants 🍚 vs 🍦

Here is one of the most useful money ideas you will ever learn:

**Needs** are things you must have to live and be safe:
* Food 🍚
* Shelter (a place to sleep) 🏠
* Clothes 👕
* Medicine when you are sick 💊

**Wants** are things that are nice to have, but you would be okay without them:
* The newest phone 📱
* Ice cream 🍦
* A new toy 🎮
* Eating at a restaurant

Neither needs nor wants are bad. But **needs come first**.

When money is limited, you take care of needs before wants.

---

### Pocket Money — A Mini Budget 💸

If you receive pocket money, you are already learning how to manage money!

Here is a simple way to think about your pocket money:

| Jar | How Much | What It Is For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 🟢 Spend | 70% | Things you need or small treats now |
| 🔵 Save | 20% | Something bigger you are saving towards |
| 🟡 Give | 10% | Helping others — donation, sadaqah, buka puasa fund |

**Example:** If you get RM 10 pocket money:
* RM 7 for spending
* RM 2 for saving
* RM 1 for giving

You do not have to use these exact numbers. The important thing is that some goes to saving and some goes to giving — not everything gets spent immediately.

---

### The Magic of Saving ✨

Here is something amazing about saving:

**Small amounts saved regularly add up to big amounts over time.**

Imagine you save RM 2 every day.

In one week: RM 14
In one month: RM 60
In one year: RM 730

That is enough for:
* A new bicycle 🚲
* A laptop for school 💻
* A family day out 🎉

The money did not appear by magic — you saved it, bit by bit.

---

### Banks — Where Money Lives Safely 🏦

A bank is a safe place to keep money.

When you put money in a bank:
* It is safe (not under the mattress where it could be lost or stolen)
* The bank keeps a record of how much you have
* You can take it out when you need it
* The bank may pay you a small extra amount for keeping it there (this is called **interest** or **dividen** for Islamic savings)

In Malaysia, many banks offer **savings accounts for children** — you can open one with a parent or guardian.

Some banks also offer **tabung** (savings boxes) for young savers.

---

### The Difference Between a Card and Cash

You might notice your parents sometimes pay with a card instead of cash.

| Cash | Card |
| --- | --- |
| Physical notes and coins | Plastic card linked to a bank account or credit |
| You see exactly how much you spend | Spending can feel less "real" |
| Runs out visibly | Easy to lose track of spending |
| Cannot be used online | Can be used online |

Both are money — the card just accesses money stored in a bank digitally.

**Debit card** = spending money you already have in the bank.
**Credit card** = borrowing money from the bank to spend now, and paying it back later (with extra if not paid on time).

### A Story to Help You Remember

**Hana's Birthday RM 50**

On her 10th birthday, Hana received RM 50 from her grandparents.

She really wanted to buy a new art set (RM 35) AND a book she had been eyeing (RM 22).

But RM 35 + RM 22 = RM 57. She only had RM 50.

Her mum helped her think:

* **Need or want?** Both were wants — but the art set had more use for school.
* **Save some?** She decided to buy the art set (RM 35), save RM 10, and give RM 5 to the school donation box.

A month later, she had saved enough to buy the book she wanted too.

**She did not have to choose — she just had to wait a little.**

### Quick Activity — Try This! 🎯

Ask a parent or guardian:

1. "What is one thing you save money for?"
2. "What is one thing you spend money on every week?"
3. "What is one thing you give money to?"

Talking about money as a family helps everyone understand it better.

### For Parents — A Note 👨‍👩‍👧

**Why financial literacy starts young:**

Children who learn about money early — through pocket money, conversations, and simple decisions — develop healthier financial habits as adults. The concepts on this page (needs vs wants, save/spend/give, how banks work) form the foundation of financial wellbeing.

**Practical suggestions:**

* Give pocket money in physical cash where possible — children learn faster when they can see and count money
* Let children make small purchasing decisions and experience the consequences (spending all at once, then having nothing left)
* Visit a bank with your child to open a savings account — making the savings habit concrete
* Normalise talking about money in age-appropriate terms — financial avoidance at home leads to financial anxiety in adulthood
