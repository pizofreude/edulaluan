### Tax Is How We All Pay for Things We Share

Imagine your school decides to buy a new basketball for everyone to use. There are 30 students in the class.

If everyone gives RM 1, the class has RM 30 тАФ enough for a good basketball that everyone can enjoy.

**That's a bit like how tax works.**

**Tax** is money that people and businesses pay to the government. The government collects all that money and uses it to pay for things that everyone in the country shares and benefits from.

### Who Pays Tax?

In Malaysia, different types of tax are paid by different people and businesses:

**1. Income Tax**

Adults who earn money from working pay a portion of their earnings to the government. This is called **income tax**.

For example: if someone earns RM 5,000 a month, they might pay a small percentage of that to the government.

Not everyone pays the same amount тАФ people who earn more, pay more.

**2. Sales Tax and Service Tax (SST)**

When you buy certain things at a shop or pay for certain services, a small percentage is added to the price. This goes to the government.

You might have seen "SST included" on a restaurant bill or receipt тАФ that is Sales and Service Tax.

**3. Road Tax (Cukai Jalan)**

Cars need to pay a yearly fee called road tax. This helps pay for the upkeep of roads.

### A Simple Way to Understand It

Imagine Malaysia is one big household with millions of people in it.

**The government is like the household manager.** Their job is to make sure everyone has what they need тАФ hospitals, schools, roads, safety.

**Taxes are everyone's contribution** to that shared household budget.

Just like in a family, everyone contributes something so the household runs properly.

---

### Is Tax Collected Fairly?

In Malaysia, income tax works on a **progressive** system тАФ which means:

* People who earn **less** pay a **lower percentage**
* People who earn **more** pay a **higher percentage**

This is designed to be fair тАФ those with more help carry more of the shared cost.

There are also many **reliefs** тАФ if you spend money on things the government wants to encourage (like buying books, going to the doctor, or saving in EPF), you pay a little less tax.

---

### What Would Happen Without Tax?

Imagine if no one paid any tax at all.

* No money to pay teachers тЖТ schools close
* No money for hospitals тЖТ sick people cannot get help
* No money for police тЖТ less safety
* No money for roads тЖТ hard to travel

Countries with good tax systems and good use of tax money tend to have better hospitals, schools, and roads.

---

### Does The Government Always Use Tax Money Well?

This is a great question тАФ and an important one.

Governments are responsible for using tax money carefully and honestly. When they do not, it is called **corruption** тАФ using public money for personal benefit instead of the public good.

In Malaysia, agencies like:
* **SPRM (Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission / MACC)** тАФ investigate corruption
* **Parliament** тАФ oversees how the government spends money
* **Auditor General** тАФ checks whether money is being used properly

Citizens have a right to know how their tax money is spent. This is why government budgets are made public.

Asking "where does the money go?" is a smart and responsible thing to do.

### Zakat тАФ A Different Kind of Giving ЁЯМЩ

For Muslim Malaysians, there is also **Zakat** тАФ a religious obligation to give a portion of your wealth to those in need.

Zakat is different from tax:
* Tax goes to the **government** for everyone
* Zakat goes through **religious authorities** to help specific groups (the poor, students, new Muslims, travellers in need)

Both tax and zakat are ways of sharing wealth тАФ but through different systems, for different purposes.

### A Story to Help You Remember

**The Class Canteen**

Ms. Faridah's class had a problem. Their classroom's ceiling fan was broken and it was really hot.

"We can fix it," said Ms. Faridah. "But we need RM 50 for the repair. There are 25 of you. If each of you contributes RM 2, we have exactly what we need."

Some students had more pocket money than others. So they agreed: students with more would contribute RM 3, students with less would contribute RM 1. It still worked out.

A week later, the fan was fixed. Everyone benefited тАФ even the students who contributed less.

"That," said Ms. Faridah, "is a little bit like how taxes work in a country."

Izzat, who sat at the back, raised his hand. "But what if someone refuses to pay?"

"Then the fan stays broken," said Ms. Faridah. "And everyone suffers тАФ including the person who didn't pay."
