## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**Why do we get vaccinated?**

Vaccines train your immune system to fight specific diseases — before you encounter them.

When enough people in a community are vaccinated, diseases cannot spread easily. This protects babies, elderly people, and others who cannot be vaccinated.

In Malaysia:

* Most childhood vaccines are **free** at Klinik Kesihatan
* They follow a schedule starting from birth
* Missing vaccines can usually be caught up later

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Malaysia's immunisation programme is administered by KKM and has been running since the 1950s. It is one of Southeast Asia's most comprehensive and has contributed to the elimination or near-elimination of diseases such as polio, diphtheria, and neonatal tetanus in Malaysia.

The programme covers:

* **National Immunisation Programme (NIP)** — free childhood vaccines on a fixed schedule
* **Catch-up vaccinations** — for children or adults who missed scheduled doses
* **Adult vaccinations** — recommended vaccines beyond childhood
* **Travel vaccinations** — required or recommended for specific destinations

### Tracking Your Child's Vaccination Record

* **Pink health booklet (Buku Rekod Kesihatan Kanak-Kanak)** — issued at birth; bring to every vaccination visit
* **MySejahtera app** — vaccination records are digitally logged and accessible
* If a booklet is lost, records can be retrieved from the Klinik Kesihatan where vaccinations were given

## Part 2: Catch-Up Vaccinations

Missing a scheduled vaccine does not mean starting over.

KKM follows a **catch-up schedule** that allows incomplete vaccination series to be completed regardless of age.

### Who Needs Catch-Up

* Children who missed doses due to illness, travel, or parental hesitancy
* Adults who were not vaccinated as children (e.g. those born before certain vaccines were introduced)
* New arrivals to Malaysia whose home country schedules differ from Malaysia's NIP
* Reverts to Islam who were not vaccinated in childhood for religious or cultural reasons

### How to Catch Up

1. Visit your nearest Klinik Kesihatan
2. Bring any existing vaccination records
3. The medical officer or nurse will assess which doses are still needed
4. A catch-up plan will be arranged — often across several visits

Catch-up NIP vaccines remain **free** at Klinik Kesihatan for eligible groups.

Several vaccines are recommended for adults beyond the childhood schedule.

### Vaccines for Pregnant Women

| Vaccine     | Timing                    | Purpose                                    |
| ----------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Tdap        | Each pregnancy (27–36 weeks) | Protects newborn before their own vaccination |
| Influenza   | Any trimester             | Reduces flu severity during pregnancy      |
| COVID-19    | Per KKM guidance          | As recommended                             |

These are offered at Klinik Kesihatan during antenatal visits at no cost.

## Part 4: Travel Vaccinations

Travel vaccinations are separate from the NIP and are tailored to your destination and itinerary.

### Common Travel Vaccines

| Vaccine          | Relevant Destinations                              | Notes                                    |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Typhoid          | South Asia, Africa, parts of Southeast Asia        | Oral or injection; booster every 3 years |
| Hepatitis A      | Most developing countries                          | 2-dose series                            |
| Yellow Fever     | Sub-Saharan Africa, tropical South America         | **Required** for entry to some countries; certificate needed |
| Meningococcal    | Saudi Arabia (Haj/Umrah — mandatory), sub-Saharan Africa | Tabung Haji coordinates for pilgrims   |
| Japanese Encephalitis | Rural Southeast Asia, South Asia               | For extended rural stays                 |
| Rabies           | Extended travel to endemic areas                   | Pre-exposure series                      |
| Cholera          | High-risk areas, humanitarian workers              | Oral vaccine                             |

### Yellow Fever Certificate

If travelling to a yellow fever endemic country or transiting through one, a **Yellow Fever Vaccination Certificate (Yellow Card)** is required.

Yellow fever vaccine is only available at **KKM-authorised Yellow Fever Vaccination Centres**.

List of authorised centres: **moh.gov.my → Travellers' Health**

### Haj and Umrah — Meningococcal Requirement

Saudi Arabia mandates proof of meningococcal vaccination (ACWY) for all pilgrims.

Tabung Haji coordinates vaccination for registered pilgrims. Individual pilgrims and Umrah travellers should arrange this through their Klinik Kesihatan or a private travel clinic.

### Where to Get Travel Vaccines

* **Klinik Kesihatan** — some travel vaccines available (Hepatitis A, typhoid, Td); limited at smaller clinics
* **KKM Travel Health Clinics** — at major hospitals and designated centres
* **Private travel clinics** — more comprehensive; faster appointment availability
* **Airport clinics** (KLIA, KL Sentral) — limited range, convenient for last-minute needs

**Plan ahead:** Some vaccines require multiple doses weeks apart. Consult a travel health clinic at least **4–6 weeks before departure**.

## Part 5: Vaccine Safety and Common Concerns

Vaccines used in Malaysia's NIP are approved by KKM's National Pharmaceutical Regulatory Agency (NPRA) and meet WHO standards. Each vaccine undergoes multiple rounds of safety evaluation before use.

### Common Side Effects (Normal)

Most post-vaccination reactions are mild and resolve within 1–3 days:

* Soreness or redness at injection site
* Low-grade fever
* Mild fussiness in infants

These are signs the immune system is responding — not signs of illness.

### Serious Reactions (Rare)

Serious adverse events following immunisation (AEFI) are rare but do occur. KKM monitors all reported cases through its AEFI surveillance system.

If you observe any of the following after vaccination, seek medical attention promptly:

* High fever (above 39°C)
* Difficulty breathing
* Rash spreading beyond the injection site
* Prolonged crying in infants (more than 3 hours)
* Seizures

Report suspected AEFI to your Klinik Kesihatan or via **npra.gov.my**

### On Hesitancy

Concerns about vaccine safety are acknowledged and taken seriously by KKM. The best source of information on any specific vaccine's ingredients, efficacy, and safety profile is your medical officer at Klinik Kesihatan — they can address questions based on your child's or your own health history.

For parents and individuals with concerns: an open conversation with a medical officer is more reliable than online sources, which vary widely in accuracy.

## Practical Habits for Staying Up to Date

Families and individuals who manage immunisation well tend to:

* Keep the pink health booklet safe from birth — it is the definitive physical record
* Note upcoming vaccine dates in their calendar or MySejahtera
* Attend scheduled Klinik Kesihatan antenatal visits where maternal vaccines are integrated
* Check travel vaccination requirements at least 6 weeks before any international trip
* Ask about catch-up vaccines for any family member whose history is incomplete

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Missed school vaccination**

A child missed his Year 2 HPV dose due to an extended absence from school. His parents assumed it could not be administered later. A Klinik Kesihatan nurse confirmed catch-up was possible and completed the series within the same year.

**Scenario 2: Unvaccinated adult revert**

A man who converted to Islam in his 30s had no childhood vaccination records. He visited Klinik Kesihatan, which reviewed his status and arranged catch-up doses for hepatitis B and MMR — all free.

**Scenario 3: Travel vaccination missed**

A couple travelling to East Africa for a safari did not realise yellow fever vaccination was mandatory for re-entry into Malaysia from their transit country. Last-minute vaccination at KLIA was available but stressful and costly. Planning 6 weeks ahead would have resolved this calmly.

## FAQ

**Q: Are NIP vaccines compulsory in Malaysia?**
Not legally mandated, but strongly recommended by KKM. Schools may request vaccination records as part of enrolment health checks.

**Q: Can my child receive NIP vaccines at a private clinic?**
Yes, private clinics can administer NIP vaccines but may charge a consultation or administration fee. The vaccines themselves are provided by KKM to private practitioners at no cost, but total billing varies by clinic.

**Q: What if my child has allergies — are vaccines safe?**
Inform the medical officer of all known allergies before vaccination. Severe allergic reactions to vaccines are extremely rare. Klinik Kesihatan is equipped to manage mild reactions on-site.

**Q: Does Malaysia require proof of vaccination for school enrolment?**
The pink health booklet is typically reviewed at school registration. Children without up-to-date vaccinations are usually referred for catch-up rather than refused enrolment.

**Q: Are there halal concerns with any NIP vaccines?**
KKM's Department of Islamic Development (JAKIM) has issued fatwas confirming the permissibility of vaccinations in Malaysia. Questions about specific vaccine components can be directed to JAKIM or your medical officer.
