## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is the civil service?**

The civil service (Perkhidmatan Awam) is the group of people who work for the government.

They run hospitals, schools, courts, police stations, and government offices.

If you work as a government doctor, teacher, police officer, or civil servant in a ministry — you are part of the civil service.

Jobs in the civil service offer:

* Stable salary
* Pension after retirement
* Medical benefits
* Job security

To apply, most people go through an agency called **SPA** (Suruhanjaya Perkhidmatan Awam).

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Malaysia's public sector workforce is one of the largest in the world relative to population. The **civil service (Perkhidmatan Awam)** encompasses federal ministries and departments, state civil services, local authorities, statutory bodies, and the security services.

It is managed by two main bodies:

* **JPA (Jabatan Perkhidmatan Awam)** — human capital planning, pay policy, scholarship
* **SPA (Suruhanjaya Perkhidmatan Awam)** — recruitment, examinations, appointment for federal positions

Understanding the structure helps you navigate where to apply, what to expect, and whether the civil service is the right fit for your career goals.

## Part 1: Structure of the Civil Service

### Three Levels

| Level | What It Covers | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Federal | Ministries, departments, federal agencies | LHDN, JPN, PDRM, Hospital Kerajaan |
| State | State government departments and offices | State Education Departments, State Land Offices |
| Local | Local councils and municipal authorities | DBKL, MBPJ, Majlis Bandaran |

Federal positions are under SPA's jurisdiction. State and local positions are managed by their respective State Public Service Commissions (SPA Negeri) or local authority HR.

### Service Schemes (Skim Perkhidmatan)

Civil service positions are organised into schemes based on qualification and function:

| Scheme | Group | Entry Qualification | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pengurusan dan Profesional (P&P) | Management and Professional | Degree | Medical officers, engineers, lawyers, administrators |
| Sokongan I | Support I | Diploma / STPM | Technicians, supervisors, enforcement officers |
| Sokongan II | Support II | SPM | Clerical staff, general workers, drivers |

Each scheme has its own salary grade (Gred), allowances, and promotion track.

### Security Services (Separate Commission)

Security forces are governed by separate commissions:

| Force | Commission |
| --- | --- |
| Royal Malaysian Police (PDRM) | Suruhanjaya Polis DiRaja Malaysia |
| Malaysian Armed Forces (ATM) | Angkatan Tentera Malaysia recruitment |
| Malaysian Prison Department | Under Home Ministry |
| Fire and Rescue (BOMBA) | Under Home Ministry |

These are not under SPA — they have their own recruitment processes.

## Part 2: How to Apply

### SPA — The Primary Gateway

Most federal civil service positions are filled through **SPA Online (spa.gov.my)**.

SPA manages:

* Job vacancy listings
* Online applications
* Written examinations (PTD, PTK, competency tests)
* Interview coordination
* Appointment and placement

### Application Process

```
1 Create SPA account at spa.gov.my
2 Browse vacancies or set up job alerts
3 Submit online application with documents
4 SPA written exam (if applicable to the position)
5 Interview (shortlisted candidates)
6 Medical examination (confirmed candidates)
7 Offer letter and appointment
8 Pre-service training (INTAN or agency-specific)
```

### Key SPA Examinations

| Exam | What It Is For | Who Takes It |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Peperiksaan PTD (Pegawai Tadbir dan Diplomatik) | Entry to the administrative and diplomatic service (M41) | Degree holders |
| Peperiksaan Am (PA) | General aptitude exam for various positions | Various grades |
| PTK (Penilaian Tahap Kecekapan) | Competency assessment for promotion | Serving civil servants |
| SPA8 / SPA9 forms | General application forms for open vacancies | Applicants for Sokongan posts |

### Typical Timeline

The civil service hiring process is notoriously slow by private sector standards:

| Stage | Typical Duration |
| --- | --- |
| Application to exam | 3–12 months |
| Exam to interview | 3–6 months |
| Interview to offer | 1–6 months |
| Offer to start date | 1–3 months |
| **Total: application to first day** | **12–24 months is common** |

This is not an exaggeration. Candidates who apply to the civil service should plan their finances and career accordingly — do not resign from current employment until a formal offer letter is received.

### Improving Your Application

| Factor | What Helps |
| --- | --- |
| Academic results | Minimum CGPA requirements vary by position; higher is better |
| Language | Strong BM is essential; English is valued for P&P grades |
| Interview preparation | Current affairs, public service motivation, policy awareness |
| Co-curriculum | Leadership roles and community service are noted |
| Relevant experience | Internship or work experience in related field |

## Part 3: Pay Scales and Benefits

### Sistem Saraan Malaysia (SSM)

Civil service pay follows the **SSM (Sistem Saraan Malaysia)** salary schedule, updated periodically by the government.

Pay is structured by **Gred (Grade)** within each scheme:

| Grade Range | Scheme | Monthly Salary (approx.) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gred 11–22 | Sokongan II | RM 1,200–RM 2,500 |
| Gred 26–40 | Sokongan I | RM 1,800–RM 4,500 |
| Gred 41–54 | P&P (entry to mid) | RM 2,800–RM 7,500 |
| Gred 52–58 | P&P (senior) | RM 5,000–RM 12,000 |
| Gred 54–VU | Top management | RM 8,000–RM 25,000+ |

**Note:** Civil service salaries are generally lower than private sector equivalents at entry level, particularly in professional fields (engineering, medicine, law, IT). The gap narrows with seniority and is partially offset by non-monetary benefits.

### Benefits Package

This is where the civil service offering becomes substantially more attractive than raw salary suggests:

| Benefit | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Pension (pencen) | Defined benefit pension after 10+ years service; full pension after 25+ years |
| Medical | Free treatment at all government hospitals and clinics |
| Housing loan subsidy | Reduced interest rate on home loans via government schemes |
| Car loan subsidy | Interest-free or low-interest car loans for eligible grades |
| Study loan | Government study loan for approved courses |
| Annual leave | 8–30 days depending on grade and service length |
| Medical leave | 15–30 days per year |
| Maternity leave | 90 days (increased from 60 days) |
| COLA (Cost of Living Allowance) | For selected locations (Sabah, Sarawak, rural postings) |
| BSSP (Bantuan Sara Hidup) | Special living assistance payments |

**The pension** is the most significant financial benefit. A private sector employee must build their own retirement fund (EPF + personal savings). A civil servant with 25+ years of service receives a monthly pension for life — a guaranteed income stream that has substantial actuarial value.

## Part 4: Career Progression and Promotions

### Promotion Pathways

| Route | How It Works |
| --- | --- |
| Time-based increment | Annual salary increments based on performance assessment (SKT) |
| PTK (competency exam) | Must pass PTK to be eligible for certain promotions |
| Merit promotion | Based on SKT performance rating + supervisor recommendation |
| Kenaikan Pangkat Secara Lantikan (KPL) | Direct appointment to higher grade via panel assessment |

### Annual Performance Assessment (SKT)

SKT (Sasaran Kerja Tahunan) is the annual performance appraisal system:

* Targets set at start of year
* Mid-year review
* End-of-year assessment by supervisor
* Rating determines annual increment and promotion eligibility

Ratings range from Cemerlang (Excellent) to Hadir Sahaja (Attendance Only). Only Cemerlang and Baik ratings qualify for increments and promotion consideration.

### Honest Assessment of Progression

Career progression in the civil service can be slow and politically influenced at senior levels:

* Promotions at lower grades (Sokongan) are relatively predictable and time-based
* At P&P grades (41 and above), promotions become more competitive and discretionary
* Top management positions (JUSA grades) are highly political and limited in number
* Lateral mobility across agencies or fields is possible but requires formal transfer application

The civil service rewards loyalty and consistency. It is less suited to those who expect rapid career advancement based on individual merit alone.

## Part 5: Rights and Obligations of Civil Servants

### Key Obligations

Civil servants operate under the **General Orders (Perintah Am)** and the **Public Officers (Conduct and Discipline) Regulations 1993**:

| Obligation | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| Neutrality | Civil servants must not engage in partisan political activities |
| Confidentiality | Official information must not be disclosed without authorisation |
| Loyalty | Duty of loyalty to the government and the Yang di-Pertuan Agong |
| Integrity | Anti-corruption obligations under MACC Act |
| Appearance and conduct | Dress code and professional conduct standards apply |
| Approval for outside activities | Moonlighting or outside business requires prior written approval |

### Key Rights

| Right | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Security of tenure | Cannot be dismissed without due process under General Orders |
| Appeal | Right to appeal against disciplinary action |
| Transfer rights | Can apply for transfer; compulsory transfers are part of service |
| Pension entitlement | Vested after minimum qualifying service |
| Grievance channel | JPA and Ombudsman (Suruhanjaya Integriti Agensi Kerajaan — SIAK) |

### Political Activity Restrictions

Civil servants face significant restrictions on political participation:

* Cannot publicly campaign for or against any political party
* Cannot hold executive positions in political parties
* Cannot make public statements that could be construed as politically partisan
* These restrictions apply during service and are a common source of tension for civically engaged civil servants

This is a legal and regulatory reality of civil service employment — not a matter of personal preference.

## Part 6: Civil Service vs GLCs and Statutory Bodies

Many Malaysians conflate government employment with civil service employment. They are related but distinct.

### Distinctions

| Employment Type | Examples | Under SPA? | Pension? | Pay Scale |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Federal civil service | KKM doctors, LHDN officers, JPN staff | Yes | Yes (defined benefit) | SSM |
| State civil service | State land office, state education dept | No (SPA Negeri) | Yes (state pension) | State SSM |
| Statutory body | EPF (as employer), SOCSO, BNM, MARA | No | Varies (EPF + gratuity) | Own scale |
| GLC | Petronas, Telekom, TNB, Khazanah | No | No (EPF only) | Market-linked |

### Why This Matters

* **Statutory body and GLC employees** are not civil servants — they do not receive a government pension and are not bound by General Orders
* Pay at GLCs and statutory bodies is generally higher than civil service equivalents
* Job security at GLCs is lower — GLC employees can be made redundant; civil servants cannot under normal circumstances
* Some statutory bodies (BNM, Securities Commission) offer compensation packages competitive with private sector while retaining more stability

For students choosing between a JPA scholarship (leading to civil service) and a Petronas or Khazanah scholarship (leading to GLC employment) — these are fundamentally different career tracks with different trade-offs.

## Practical Habits for Civil Service Applicants

Candidates who navigate the civil service application process well tend to:

* Apply early and broadly — SPA processes are slow, multiple applications increase chances
* Never resign from current employment until a formal appointment letter is received
* Prepare specifically for SPA's written exams — past papers are available and the format is predictable
* Understand the pension system before accepting an offer — it is the most undervalued part of the package for young applicants
* Read the General Orders before joining — obligations around moonlighting and outside business activities affect financial planning

---

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Two-year wait, not planned for**

A fresh graduate applied to the civil service immediately after completing his degree and waited for the offer. He assumed it would take a few months. Two years later, when the offer came, he had already built a career in the private sector and declined. Planning for the timeline would have allowed a different decision.

**Scenario 2: Pension value underestimated**

A nurse considered leaving the government hospital for a private hospital offering 30% more salary. A financial planner helped her calculate the actuarial value of her government pension — the lifetime guaranteed income after 25 years of service was worth substantially more than the salary gap compounded over a career. She stayed.

**Scenario 3: Outside business not declared**

A civil servant ran a small online business without seeking prior written approval from his department head as required under General Orders. When discovered during an audit, he faced a disciplinary proceeding. Understanding the moonlighting rules before starting the business would have prevented this.

### Path B: I want to understand my pay and benefits as a civil servant

Check your current **Gred (Grade)** on your appointment letter.

Look up your salary band under the current SSM schedule — available at **jpa.gov.my**.

Review your pension entitlement under the Pensions Act 1980 — vesting conditions and calculation method are explained on the JPA website.

For allowances (housing, car, COLA), check with your agency's HR.

---

### Path C: I want to understand my obligations around outside work

Read **General Orders Chapter D** — regulations on outside business and employment.

Any outside business, directorship, or paid activity requires **prior written approval** from your Head of Department.

Unpaid community or voluntary work generally does not require approval — confirm with your agency HR.

## FAQ

**Q: Can non-Bumiputera apply for all civil service positions?**
Yes — the civil service is open to all Malaysian citizens. In practice, ethnic composition varies significantly across agencies and grades, reflecting historical patterns of application and placement. There are no legal restrictions on non-Bumiputera applications for most positions.

**Q: Can I hold a civil service job and run a private business?**
Only with prior written approval from your Head of Department. The business must not conflict with your official duties or compromise your integrity. Approval is not guaranteed and may be revoked.

**Q: What happens if I resign before my pension vests?**
If you resign before completing the minimum qualifying service for pension (generally 10 years for partial pension, 25 years for full pension), you receive a gratuity payment instead — a lump sum calculated on years served. No monthly pension is payable.

**Q: Are contract civil servants (pekerja kontrak) entitled to the same benefits?**
No. Contract officers do not receive the defined benefit pension. They are typically on EPF-based retirement contributions and fixed-term contracts. Permanent appointment (tetap) is required for full pension entitlement.

**Q: Is the civil service still a good career option in 2025?**
It depends entirely on what you value. For job security, medical benefits, pension, and work-life balance — yes, particularly for those in professional schemes. For income maximisation or rapid career advancement — the private sector is generally better. Neither is objectively superior; the trade-offs are real on both sides.
