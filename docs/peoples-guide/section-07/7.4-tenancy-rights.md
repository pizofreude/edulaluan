## A Note on Malaysia's Legal Gap

Malaysia does not have a dedicated **Residential Tenancy Act**. Unlike many countries, tenant protections in Malaysia are not codified in a single law — they exist through:

* The terms of your tenancy agreement (contract law)
* The **Contracts Act 1950** (general contract principles)
* The **Distress Act 1951** (landlord rights to seize goods for unpaid rent)
* Common law principles on implied duties
* The **National Land Code** (for formal lease registrations)

A proposed Residential Tenancy Act has been discussed in parliament periodically but has not been enacted as of March 2026.

**What this means for renters:** Your rights are largely determined by what your agreement says. A well-drafted agreement with clear terms protects you. A poorly drafted or absent agreement leaves you exposed.

### 🧒 Simple

**What should you know before renting a home in Malaysia?**

Renting means you are paying to live in someone else's property under an agreement.

The most important things:

* Always have a **written tenancy agreement** before paying anything
* Always get a **receipt** for every payment — deposit, rent, utilities
* Read the agreement before signing — you are legally bound by what you sign
* Take photos of the unit **before you move in** and keep them
* Know what deposits you paid and what conditions allow the landlord to keep them

Malaysia does not have strong laws protecting tenants by default — your agreement is your main protection.

## Overview

Renting is the primary housing arrangement for millions of Malaysians — particularly young adults, students, and workers in urban centres. Yet tenancy rights are poorly understood and weakly codified in Malaysian law.

This page gives renters a practical framework: what to check before signing, what rights you have during the tenancy, how to recover deposits, and how to handle disputes.

## Part 1: Tenancy Agreement — What to Check Before Signing

### Why the Agreement Is Everything

In the absence of a Residential Tenancy Act, the tenancy agreement is the primary document governing your rights. Courts will generally enforce what the agreement says — including terms that may be unfavourable to you if you signed them.

**Never sign an agreement you have not read in full.** Take 24 hours to review before signing if needed — a legitimate landlord will not pressure you to sign immediately.

### Tenancy Agreement Checklist

Before signing, verify these items are clearly stated:

**Parties and Property**
- [ ] Full name and IC number of landlord and tenant
- [ ] Full address and unit number of the property
- [ ] Description of what is included (furnishings, appliances, parking)

**Financial Terms**
- [ ] Monthly rent amount
- [ ] Rent payment date (e.g. 1st of each month)
- [ ] Accepted payment methods
- [ ] Security deposit amount (standard: 2 months rent)
- [ ] Utility deposit amount (standard: 0.5 month rent)
- [ ] Stamping fee responsibility (standard: shared or tenant pays)
- [ ] Conditions for deposit deduction at end of tenancy

**Duration and Renewal**
- [ ] Tenancy start and end date
- [ ] Renewal process and notice period
- [ ] Whether rent increases on renewal and by how much

**Maintenance and Repairs**
- [ ] Who is responsible for structural repairs (should be landlord)
- [ ] Who is responsible for minor maintenance (usually tenant)
- [ ] Process for requesting repairs

**Rules and Restrictions**
- [ ] Pet policy
- [ ] Subletting restrictions
- [ ] Guest and overnight visitor policy
- [ ] Renovation or alteration restrictions
- [ ] Notice required before landlord entry

**Termination**
- [ ] Notice period for tenant to terminate early
- [ ] Notice period for landlord to terminate
- [ ] Penalty for early termination
- [ ] Conditions under which landlord can terminate

**Dispute Resolution**
- [ ] Agreed method (negotiation, mediation, court)

### Stamping the Agreement

A tenancy agreement should be **stamped by LHDN** to be legally valid and enforceable in court. Unstamped agreements can still be used as evidence but attract a penalty if submitted in legal proceedings.

Stamping fees are based on annual rent — approximately:

| Annual Rent | Stamp Duty |
| --- | --- |
| Up to RM 2,400 | Exempt |
| RM 2,401 – RM 36,000 | RM 1 per RM 250 |
| Above RM 36,000 | RM 2 per RM 250 |

Stamp at any LHDN branch or via the e-Stamping portal (hasil.gov.my).

## Part 2: Tenant Rights During the Tenancy

### Quiet Enjoyment

Tenants have an implied right to **quiet enjoyment** of the premises — meaning the landlord cannot unreasonably enter, harass, or disturb you during the tenancy.

In practice this means:

* Landlord must give **reasonable notice** (typically 24–48 hours) before entering — except in genuine emergencies
* Landlord cannot cut off utilities to pressure you to leave
* Landlord cannot change locks or remove belongings without court process

**If a landlord enters without notice or cuts utilities:** this may constitute a breach of the tenancy agreement and potentially harassment — document and escalate.

### Repairs and Maintenance

General principle (where the agreement does not specify otherwise):

| Repair Type | Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| Structural (roof, walls, plumbing, electrical mains) | Landlord |
| Major appliances (if provided furnished) | Landlord |
| Minor day-to-day maintenance (light bulbs, blocked drains) | Tenant |
| Damage caused by tenant (beyond fair wear and tear) | Tenant |
| Air conditioning (if provided) | Usually landlord for major servicing; tenant for filter cleaning |

**Request repairs in writing** (WhatsApp is fine) — this creates a record that you reported the issue and the landlord did or did not respond.

If the landlord refuses to make structural repairs that affect habitability, you may have grounds to terminate the tenancy — consult a lawyer.

### Utility Disconnection

A landlord cannot legally disconnect utilities as a means of forcing a tenant out. Utility accounts in the landlord's name remain the landlord's obligation to maintain. A landlord who cuts electricity or water to evict a tenant may face civil liability.

## Part 3: Deposits — Getting Them Back

### Standard Deposit Structure

| Deposit Type | Standard Amount | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Security deposit | 2 months rent | Covers unpaid rent or damage beyond fair wear and tear |
| Utility deposit | 0.5 month rent | Covers outstanding utility bills |
| Additional deposits | Varies (pets, renovations) | Specific conditions in agreement |

Always get a **receipt** for every deposit payment. Pay by bank transfer where possible — the transaction record serves as evidence.

### At End of Tenancy — Deposit Return

**When you must get your deposit back:**

* All rent paid up to date
* No damage beyond fair wear and tear
* Property returned in clean condition
* All keys returned

**Timeline:** The agreement should specify when the deposit is returned. If not stated, a reasonable timeframe is **14–30 days** after the tenancy ends and the landlord has inspected.

### What Landlords Can Deduct

A landlord may legitimately deduct for:

| Deductible | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Unpaid rent | Any outstanding rent at end of tenancy |
| Damage beyond fair wear and tear | Holes in walls, broken fixtures, stained carpet from spills |
| Cleaning if left in poor condition | Professional cleaning cost if excessively dirty |
| Outstanding utility bills | If utilities were in tenant's name and unpaid |

A landlord **cannot** deduct for:

| Not Deductible | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Normal wear and tear | Expected deterioration from ordinary use |
| Pre-existing damage | Damage that was there before you moved in |
| Vague or unverifiable claims | Landlord must show proof of damage and cost |

**The pre-move-in photo record is your best protection** against false damage claims. Take time-stamped photos of every room, all appliances, and any pre-existing damage before moving in. Share them with the landlord by WhatsApp immediately — this creates a dated, documented baseline.

### If the Landlord Refuses to Return the Deposit

| Step | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Send formal written demand (WhatsApp or email) |
| 2 | Give 14-day deadline to return or provide itemised deductions |
| 3 | If no response, file at **Magistrates' Court** for recovery of deposit |
| 4 | Bring agreement, payment receipts, pre and post photos, and communications |

**TTPM does not cover tenancy disputes** — the Magistrates' Court is the forum for deposit recovery claims.

## Part 4: Early Termination and Eviction

### Tenant Terminating Early

Most agreements specify a penalty for early termination — commonly:

* Forfeiture of part or all of the security deposit
* Payment of rent for the notice period
* Payment of the landlord's re-letting costs (agent fees)

Before signing, negotiate reasonable early termination terms — especially if your employment situation may require relocation.

### Landlord Terminating Early

A landlord can terminate the tenancy if:

* Tenant breaches the agreement (unpaid rent, damage, subletting without permission)
* The agreement specifies other termination grounds

**Process for lawful termination:**

1. Landlord gives written notice as specified in the agreement
2. If tenant refuses to vacate, landlord must apply to court for a **writ of possession**
3. Court issues the writ if the landlord's case is valid
4. Bailiff enforces the writ

**Illegal eviction:** A landlord who forcibly removes a tenant's belongings, changes locks, or physically removes a tenant without a court order is acting illegally — regardless of how much rent is unpaid. This is self-help eviction and is actionable in court.

### What to Do If Faced with Illegal Eviction

1. Do not vacate under duress — document everything
2. Call PDRM if landlord is being physically threatening
3. Apply for an **injunction** at the High Court to prevent eviction while your case is heard — consult a lawyer immediately
4. Contact a legal aid centre if you cannot afford a lawyer (see below)

### Unpaid Rent — Landlord's Rights

Under the **Distress Act 1951**, a landlord can seize a tenant's movable goods (furniture, appliances) as security for unpaid rent — without going to court first. This is a significant legal power unique to Malaysian tenancy law.

However, the landlord must:

* Issue formal notice before distraining
* Not seize tools of the tenant's trade (used for livelihood)
* Not seize items up to a minimum value needed for basic living

## Practical Habits for Renters

Tenants who protect themselves effectively tend to:

* Always sign a stamped, written agreement before paying any money
* Pay all deposits and rent by bank transfer — never cash without a receipt
* Photograph the entire unit before moving in and share photos with the landlord via WhatsApp immediately
* Report all repair requests in writing — not verbally
* Read the termination clause before signing — especially early termination penalties
* Keep all communications with the landlord — WhatsApp messages are admissible evidence in Malaysian courts

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Deposit wrongfully withheld**

A tenant moved out of a rental unit and returned the keys. The landlord refused to return the RM 3,200 security deposit, claiming "general wear and tear damage" without providing any photos or invoices. The tenant had taken timestamped photos before moving in showing the property's pre-existing condition. She sent a formal demand letter with the photos attached. The landlord returned the deposit in full within a week — knowing the court would not support vague claims against documented evidence.

**Scenario 2: Utilities cut to force eviction**

A tenant fell two weeks behind on rent during a period of financial difficulty. The landlord cut off the electricity without notice. The tenant documented the disconnection, sent a formal written complaint citing breach of quiet enjoyment, and threatened civil action. The landlord restored the electricity and pursued the proper route — a written notice and, eventually, Magistrates' Court — to recover unpaid rent.

**Scenario 3: Verbal agreement, no recourse**

A tenant rented a room based on a verbal agreement after being assured by the landlord that "a contract is not necessary between friends." When the landlord sold the property and the new owner asked the tenant to vacate immediately with no deposit return, there was no written agreement to rely on. The tenant had limited legal recourse and lost the deposit. A one-page signed agreement would have changed the outcome entirely.

## FAQ

**Q: Does my tenancy agreement need to be witnessed or notarised?**
Witnessing is not legally required but is good practice. Notarisation is not required for standard residential tenancies. Stamping by LHDN is the important step — it makes the agreement admissible in court without penalty.

**Q: Can my landlord enter my unit without notice?**
In genuine emergencies (burst pipe, fire, structural danger), immediate entry is justified. For all other visits — routine inspection, repairs, showing to prospective tenants — reasonable notice (typically 24–48 hours) should be given. If your agreement specifies a notice period, the landlord must follow it.

**Q: What is fair wear and tear?**
Fair wear and tear is the normal deterioration of a property through ordinary use over time — faded paint, minor scuffs on walls, worn carpet in high-traffic areas. It is not damage caused by negligence, misuse, or accidents. A landlord cannot deduct for fair wear and tear.

**Q: Can a landlord increase rent during a fixed tenancy?**
No — rent is fixed for the duration of the tenancy as stated in the agreement. A landlord can propose a rent increase upon renewal but cannot unilaterally increase it mid-tenancy without your agreement.

**Q: What if my landlord does not stamp the agreement?**
An unstamped agreement is still a valid contract but is not admissible in court proceedings without paying a penalty stamp duty at that point. Both parties have an interest in stamping — the tenant should insist on it and can offer to arrange it if the landlord is reluctant.
