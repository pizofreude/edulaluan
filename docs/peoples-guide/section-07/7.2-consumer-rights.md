### 🧒 Simple

**What does the Consumer Protection Act protect you from?**

When you buy something in Malaysia, the law gives you rights automatically — whether the seller mentions them or not.

Your basic rights as a buyer:

* What you buy must actually work and be safe
* It must match what was advertised or described
* If it is defective, you can ask for a repair, replacement, or refund
* A seller cannot make you sign away these rights with a "no refund" clause

These rights apply to goods and many services — including online purchases.

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

The **Consumer Protection Act 1999 (CPA)** is Malaysia's primary legislation protecting buyers of goods and services from unfair practices, defective products, and misleading representations.

It applies to:

* Consumer transactions — purchases for personal, domestic, or household use
* Both physical and online purchases
* Goods and some services

It does **not** fully apply to:

* Business-to-business transactions (B2B)
* Financial products and services (covered by BNM and SC frameworks)
* Professional services in some contexts
* Real property transactions (land and buildings)

The CPA is enforced by **KPDNHEP** and adjudicated by **TTPM (Tribunal Tuntutan Pengguna Malaysia)** for claims up to RM 50,000.

## Part 1: Core Consumer Rights Under the CPA 1999

### The Eight Implied Guarantees (Part II, CPA 1999)

The CPA implies the following guarantees into every consumer contract — meaning they apply automatically whether or not the seller states them:

| Guarantee | What It Means | CPA Reference |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acceptable quality | Goods are fit for purpose, safe, durable, and free from defects | Section 32 |
| Fitness for particular purpose | If you state your specific purpose, goods must be fit for it | Section 33 |
| Description | Goods must match the description given | Section 34 |
| Sample | Goods must correspond to any sample shown | Section 35 |
| Price | Price must be reasonable if not agreed in advance | Section 36 |
| Spare parts and repairs | Supplier must ensure availability of parts and repair facilities for a reasonable period | Section 37 |
| Title | Seller must have the right to sell the goods | Section 38 |
| Services with care and skill | Services must be performed with reasonable care and skill | Section 53 |

**These guarantees cannot be excluded by contract.** A seller who includes a clause stating "we are not responsible for defects" or "no refund under any circumstances" is attempting to override implied guarantees — such clauses are unenforceable under the CPA.

## Part 2: Unfair Contract Terms and False Representations

### Unfair Contract Terms (Part IIIA, CPA 1999)

An unfair contract term is one that:

* Creates a significant imbalance between the parties' rights and obligations
* Operates to the detriment of the consumer
* Was not individually negotiated (typically pre-printed terms)

**Common examples of potentially unfair terms:**

| Clause | Why It May Be Unenforceable |
| --- | --- |
| "No refund, no exchange" | Overrides implied guarantee of acceptable quality |
| "We are not responsible for any damage" | Excludes liability for defects — not permitted under CPA |
| "Price may change without notice" | Creates unexpected financial obligation on consumer |
| "Management reserves the right to refuse service" | May be unfair if applied discriminatorily |
| Automatic renewal clauses without clear notice | Consumer not adequately informed |

**What this means in practice:** If a shop displays "no refund" but sells you a defective product, the sign does not override your legal rights. You are entitled to a remedy under the CPA regardless of the posted policy.

### False Representations (Part II, CPA 1999)

Sellers are prohibited from making false representations about:

* The nature, quality, or standard of goods
* The price — including fake "original price" markings to inflate perceived discounts
* Availability of goods or services
* Any testimonials or endorsements that are fabricated
* Guarantees or warranties that are misrepresented

**This includes online sellers.** A Shopee or Lazada listing that falsely describes a product's specifications violates the CPA in addition to the platform's own policies.

## Part 3: Product Safety and Liability

### Product Safety (Part VI, CPA 1999)

Suppliers have a duty not to supply goods that fail to comply with safety standards.

KKM, SIRIM, and the relevant ministries prescribe safety standards for specific product categories. Goods that do not meet these standards can be:

* Recalled by the manufacturer or supplier
* Seized by enforcement officers
* Subject to compensation claims from affected consumers

### Consumer Liability for Defective Products

If a defective product causes personal injury or property damage, the CPA allows consumers to seek compensation. Key points:

* Liability applies to the **manufacturer, importer, and supplier** — not just the retailer
* The consumer does not need to prove negligence — only that the product was defective and caused harm (strict liability)
* Limitation period: 3 years from discovery of defect

**If a defective product injures you:**

1. Seek medical attention first
2. Preserve the product — do not throw it away
3. Document the injury and damage with photos and medical records
4. Report to KPDNHEP and consider legal advice for compensation claims

## Part 4: Right to Redress — Repair, Replacement, Refund

### The 3Rs Framework

When goods fail to meet implied guarantees, you are entitled to a remedy:

| Remedy | When It Applies |
| --- | --- |
| Repair | Defect is minor and can be fixed; seller may repair first |
| Replacement | Repair is not possible, takes too long, or fails |
| Refund | Defect is major, cannot be fixed, or replacement is not available |

**What is a "major failure"?**

Under the CPA, a major failure includes situations where:

* The goods would not have been bought if the defect was known
* The goods are substantially unfit for purpose
* The goods are unsafe
* The defect cannot be repaired within a reasonable time or at all

For a major failure, the consumer can choose between a refund and a replacement — the seller does not have the right to insist on repair first.

### Reasonable Timeframes

The CPA does not specify exact timescales but requires remedies to be provided within a **reasonable time**. In practice:

| Situation | Reasonable Expectation |
| --- | --- |
| Minor repair | 7–14 working days |
| Replacement | Within stock availability, typically 1–4 weeks |
| Refund | Prompt upon agreement — within 7–14 days is reasonable |

If a seller is deliberately delaying, escalate to KPDNHEP or TTPM.

### Proof of Purchase

You do not need the original receipt to exercise warranty rights — but proof of purchase helps. Acceptable proof includes:

* Original receipt (physical or digital)
* Credit card or bank statement showing the transaction
* Order confirmation email or screenshot
* WhatsApp or message evidence of purchase

## Part 5: Digital and Online Purchases

### Distance Selling and Online Consumer Rights

The CPA applies to online purchases. Additional protections apply to **distance selling** (where buyer and seller are not physically present at the same time):

| Protection | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| Right to information | Seller must provide clear product description, price, and seller identity before purchase |
| Cooling-off period | Certain categories allow a cancellation window after purchase |
| Accurate product representation | Product images and descriptions must accurately represent what is sold |
| Delivery obligations | Seller must deliver within agreed or reasonable timeframe |

### Platform Responsibility

Online marketplaces (Shopee, Lazada, TikTok Shop) have their own buyer protection policies layered on top of the CPA:

* Most platforms hold payment in escrow until buyer confirms receipt
* Dispute resolution is available within the platform before escalating to KPDNHEP or TTPM
* Sellers on these platforms are still bound by CPA obligations

**Escalation path for online purchases:**

```
1 Contact seller directly → 2 Raise dispute via platform → 3 File with KPDNHEP (e-aduan.kpdn.gov.my) → 4 File with TTPM for compensation claim
```

Cross-reference: **Section 9.8.3 — E-Commerce and Consumer Service Escalation**

### Social Commerce (Instagram, Facebook, WhatsApp sellers)

Purchases from individual sellers on social media are trickier:

* CPA still applies if the seller is running a business
* Private individual-to-individual sales (casual, non-business) have weaker CPA protections
* Always verify the seller's SSM registration before large purchases from social media vendors
* Screenshot all product descriptions, prices, and seller commitments before paying

## Practical Habits for Asserting Your Consumer Rights

Consumers who successfully assert their rights tend to:

* Keep all receipts and order confirmations — digital or physical
* Photograph products immediately upon unpacking if buying expensive or fragile items
* Read terms and conditions for major purchases — specifically refund and warranty clauses
* Know that "no refund" signs do not override CPA rights for defective goods
* Escalate calmly and in writing — WhatsApp messages and emails create records
* Use KPDNHEP and TTPM as genuine tools, not last resorts

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: "No refund" sign ignored**

A consumer bought a RM 400 blender that stopped working after one week. The shop pointed to a "no refund" sign. The consumer cited Section 32 of the Consumer Protection Act 1999 (acceptable quality guarantee) and requested a replacement. The shop, aware of its legal obligations, replaced the blender the following day.

**Scenario 2: Online product misrepresentation**

A buyer purchased a laptop bag described online as "genuine leather." When it arrived, it was clearly synthetic. She raised a dispute on the platform and cited the false representation provisions of the CPA. The platform's buyer protection system, backed by CPA obligations, resulted in a full refund.

**Scenario 3: Service performed carelessly**

A customer had her sofa dry-cleaned. The service left visible staining. The cleaner argued the sofa material was unsuitable. Under Section 53 of the CPA (services with care and skill), the consumer argued the service provider should have advised on suitability before proceeding. She filed with TTPM and was awarded compensation for the damage.

**Step 1:** Confirm the product is genuinely defective — test it and document the fault with a video or photo.

**Step 2:** Contact the seller in writing (WhatsApp, email) — state the defect and request repair, replacement, or refund under the Consumer Protection Act 1999.

**Step 2:** Contact the seller and request a return and refund citing false representation under CPA.

**Step 3:** Raise a dispute on the platform (Shopee, Lazada, etc.) with the screenshots.

**Step 4:** If the platform does not resolve it, file at **e-aduan.kpdn.gov.my**.

## FAQ

**Q: Does the CPA apply to second-hand goods?**
Yes, but with reduced warranty expectations. The implied guarantee of acceptable quality is assessed in the context of the age and price of the second-hand item. A seller who misrepresents the condition of second-hand goods still violates the CPA.

**Q: I bought something "as is" — do I still have rights?**
"As is" sales reduce but do not eliminate your rights. If the seller specifically drew your attention to a defect, you cannot later claim for that defect. But undisclosed defects — even in "as is" sales — remain actionable.

**Q: Can a business put "no warranty" or "no refund" in a contract and make it stick?**
Not for the implied guarantees under the CPA — these cannot be excluded. A business can limit warranty scope for certain defects it specifically disclosed, but cannot exclude liability for defective goods entirely.

**Q: What if the product was a gift — can the recipient claim under CPA?**
The CPA claim runs with the original buyer's transaction. The recipient of a gift can exercise rights on the buyer's behalf, but the claim is technically the buyer's. In practice, most sellers will resolve this pragmatically.

**Q: Does the CPA apply to services as well as goods?**
Yes — Section 53 of the CPA requires services to be performed with reasonable care and skill. However, professional services (legal, medical) have additional frameworks and the CPA may interact with professional liability in complex ways.
