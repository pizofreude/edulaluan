## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is TTPM?**

TTPM (Tribunal Tuntutan Pengguna Malaysia) is a special court just for consumer disputes.

It is much simpler than a regular court:

* You represent yourself — no lawyer needed
* Filing costs very little (RM 5 for claims under RM 10,000)
* Cases are usually heard within 2 months
* If you win, the business must pay you

Use TTPM when a seller refuses to repair, replace, or refund a defective product — and KPDNHEP complaints did not get your money back.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

The **Tribunal Tuntutan Pengguna Malaysia (TTPM)** — Consumer Claims Tribunal — was established under the Consumer Protection Act 1999 to provide consumers with a fast, affordable, and accessible forum for resolving disputes with businesses.

It is specifically designed to avoid the cost and complexity of civil litigation. Lawyers are not permitted to represent either party — both the consumer and the business appear in person and present their own case.

TTPM does not enforce consumer laws (that is KPDNHEP's role) — it awards compensation to consumers who have suffered loss due to a business's failure to meet its obligations.

## Part 1: TTPM vs Civil Court — Key Differences

| Feature | TTPM | Magistrates' Court |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Claim limit | Up to RM 50,000 | Up to RM 100,000 |
| Filing fee | RM 5 (up to RM 10,000) / RM 10 (RM 10,001–RM 50,000) | Higher court fees |
| Lawyers | Not permitted | Permitted |
| Hearing timeline | Typically 30–60 days | Months to years |
| Formality | Informal — conversational hearing | Formal court procedures |
| Appeal | To High Court on points of law | Yes |
| Enforcement | Via TTPM enforcement mechanism | Via court bailiff |

**TTPM is almost always the better choice** for consumer disputes under RM 50,000. The only reason to go to court instead is if the dispute involves a legal complexity beyond the tribunal's scope (e.g. contractual interpretation requiring legal argument) or if the claim exceeds RM 50,000.

## Part 2: Who Can File and What Can Be Claimed

### Eligibility

| Requirement | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Claimant | Malaysian consumer — the buyer of goods or services for personal use |
| Respondent | The supplier, seller, or service provider (must be SSM-registered) |
| Transaction type | Consumer transaction — personal, domestic, or household use |
| Claim amount | RM 1 to RM 50,000 |
| Time limit | Within 3 years of the cause of action (when the problem arose) |

### What Can Be Claimed

| Claim Type | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Refund for defective goods | Product that stopped working; does not match description |
| Cost of repair | If consumer had to pay to fix seller's defective product |
| Replacement value | If defective goods cannot be repaired or replaced by seller |
| Compensation for consequential loss | E.g. spoiled food due to defective fridge; damage caused by defective product |
| Service refund | Service not performed with care and skill |
| False representation loss | Overpaid due to misleading product description |

### What TTPM Cannot Award

| Issue | Why Not TTPM |
| --- | --- |
| Claims above RM 50,000 | Exceeds jurisdiction — go to civil court |
| Financial services disputes | Goes to OFS or BNM TELELINK |
| Employment disputes | Goes to Labour Court or Industrial Relations |
| Property (land and buildings) | Goes to civil court |
| Criminal matters | Goes to PDRM |
| Personal injury claims (large) | May exceed jurisdiction — consider civil suit |

## Part 3: Step-by-Step Filing Process

### Before You File — Attempt Resolution First

TTPM expects claimants to have attempted to resolve the dispute with the seller before filing. Document your attempts:

* Written complaint to the seller (WhatsApp message, email)
* KPDNHEP complaint (if applicable)
* Platform dispute (for online purchases)

Keep all records — these will be part of your tribunal submission.

### Filing a Claim

| Method | Where |
| --- | --- |
| Online | ttpm.gov.my → e-Filing |
| Walk-in | Nearest TTPM office (all states) |

**Required documents at filing:**

| Document | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Claimant's MyKad | Identity verification |
| Proof of purchase | Receipt, invoice, order confirmation |
| Evidence of defect or complaint | Photos, videos, test results |
| Correspondence with seller | WhatsApp, email, letters |
| SSM details of respondent | Business registration of seller |

### Filing Fees

| Claim Amount | Filing Fee |
| --- | --- |
| Up to RM 10,000 | RM 5 |
| RM 10,001 to RM 50,000 | RM 10 |

### After Filing

```
Filing → Claim registered and reference number issued
         ↓
Respondent served (seller notified of claim)
         ↓
Hearing date set (typically within 30–60 days)
         ↓
Both parties notified of date, time, location
         ↓
Hearing conducted
         ↓
Award issued (same day or within days)
```

## Part 4: Preparing for the Hearing

### What to Bring

| Item | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| All evidence (physical) | Defective product if portable; all documents |
| Chronological summary | One-page timeline of events — date of purchase, defect, complaint, refusal |
| Copies of all evidence | Bring 3 sets — one for yourself, one for the President, one for the respondent |
| Witness (if any) | Person who observed the defect or transaction |
| Calculator | For presenting the claimed amount clearly |

### How to Present Your Case

TTPM hearings are informal — there is no need for legal argument or courtroom language. The President (tribunal officer) will guide the process.

**Structure your presentation simply:**

```
1. What you bought — product/service, price, date, seller
2. What went wrong — describe the defect or problem clearly
3. What you did about it — your attempts to get a remedy
4. What the seller did (or did not do) — their response
5. What you are asking for — the specific amount and why
```

**Practical tips:**

* Speak clearly and calmly — emotional presentations are less effective than factual ones
* Refer to documents as you present — hand copies to the President and respondent
* If the product is defective, bring it to the hearing if possible
* If the product is not portable, bring clear video evidence of the defect
* Do not exaggerate your claim — tribunals are experienced at assessing reasonable compensation
* Listen to the respondent's presentation without interrupting — you will have the opportunity to respond

### What the President Considers

| Factor | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| Whether goods met implied guarantees | Acceptable quality, fitness for purpose, match to description |
| Whether the seller was given opportunity to remedy | Did the consumer allow the seller to fix it first? |
| The loss actually suffered | Tribunals award for actual loss — not punitive damages |
| Credibility of both parties | Consistency of account and documentation |

## Part 5: After the Hearing — Awards and Enforcement

### Types of Awards

| Award Type | What It Orders |
| --- | --- |
| Refund | Seller returns the purchase price |
| Replacement | Seller provides a functioning replacement |
| Repair at seller's cost | Seller pays to have the product repaired |
| Compensation | Seller pays for consequential loss |
| Combined | e.g. partial refund + compensation for consequential damage |

### Timeline for Compliance

The respondent (business) must comply with the award within **14 days** of it being issued unless the tribunal specifies otherwise.

### If the Business Refuses to Pay

A TTPM award is legally binding and enforceable. If the respondent does not comply:

1. Apply to TTPM for a **certificate of award**
2. Register the certificate at the **Magistrates' Court**
3. The certificate becomes a court order
4. Enforcement proceeds via the court — including seizure of assets if necessary

**This process is free for the consumer.** The respondent bears the cost of non-compliance escalating to court enforcement.

In practice, most businesses comply with awards — the cost and reputational risk of court enforcement is greater than paying the claim.

## Practical Habits for TTPM Claimants

Consumers who succeed at TTPM tend to:

* Document the dispute thoroughly from the moment the problem arises — not after deciding to file
* Send all complaints to the seller in writing (WhatsApp, email) — verbal complaints are hard to evidence
* File within the 3-year limitation period — do not delay
* Present a clear, factual chronology rather than an emotional account
* Claim for actual loss only — inflated claims reduce credibility
* Attend the hearing in person — remote attendance is limited and in-person presence is more persuasive

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Mattress delivered damaged**

A consumer ordered a RM 2,800 mattress online. It arrived with a visible tear. The seller offered a RM 200 discount but refused a replacement. She filed at TTPM with photos of the damage, the product listing, and the delivery record. The tribunal ordered a full replacement. The seller complied within 10 days.

**Scenario 2: Renovation not completed**

A homeowner paid a contractor RM 8,000 for bathroom renovation. The contractor completed 60% of the work and disappeared. He filed at TTPM with the contract, payment receipts, and photos of the unfinished work. The tribunal awarded RM 5,000 — the estimated cost to complete the work with a new contractor.

**Scenario 3: Award enforced via court**

A consumer won a TTPM award of RM 3,200 against a furniture shop. The shop ignored the award for 6 weeks. She applied for a certificate of award, registered it at the Magistrates' Court, and the court issued an enforcement order. The shop paid in full within 5 days to avoid asset seizure.

## Full Wizard Content (Modal / Sidebar)

### Path A: I want to file a TTPM claim

**Step 1:** Confirm your claim is under RM 50,000 and within 3 years of the problem.

**Step 2:** Gather all documents — receipt, photos, correspondence with seller.

**Step 3:** File online at **ttpm.gov.my** or walk in to your nearest TTPM office.

**Step 4:** Pay RM 5 (under RM 10,000) or RM 10 (RM 10,001–RM 50,000).

**Step 5:** Await hearing date — typically within 30–60 days.

**Step 6:** Prepare a one-page chronology and bring 3 copies of all evidence.

---

### Path B: I won my TTPM award but the business is not paying

**Step 1:** Wait 14 days from the award date.

**Step 2:** Apply to TTPM for a certificate of award.

**Step 3:** Register the certificate at the nearest Magistrates' Court.

**Step 4:** The court issues an enforcement order — sheriff or bailiff can execute it.

This process is free for you. Contact TTPM for guidance on the certificate application.

---

### Path C: The business filed a TTPM claim against me as a consumer

This is rare but possible. Respond by attending the hearing date.

Bring all evidence supporting your position — including any prior complaints you made to the business.

TTPM hearings are balanced — both parties present their version. You do not need a lawyer.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a lawyer for TTPM?**
No — and in fact, lawyers are not permitted to represent either party at TTPM hearings. Both the consumer and the business must appear and present their own case. This is by design to keep the process accessible.

**Q: What if the business does not turn up to the hearing?**
If the respondent fails to appear without valid reason, TTPM may issue a default award in the consumer's favour based on the claimant's evidence alone.

**Q: Can I claim for emotional distress or inconvenience?**
TTPM awards for actual financial loss — not for emotional distress or general inconvenience. However, if the problem caused measurable consequential loss (e.g. hotel costs while waiting for a defective appliance to be repaired), that can be included.

**Q: What if my claim is RM 60,000 — can I split it into two TTPM claims?**
No. Artificially splitting a single claim to bring it within TTPM's jurisdiction is not permitted. Claims above RM 50,000 must go to civil court.

**Q: Can I file against an online marketplace (Shopee, Lazada) rather than the individual seller?**
TTPM claims are against the direct seller — the business that transacted with you. Marketplaces have their own dispute processes. If the seller is unregistered or untraceable, consult KPDNHEP about platform accountability.
