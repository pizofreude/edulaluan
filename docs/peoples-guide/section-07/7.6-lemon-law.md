## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is a lemon law?**

A "lemon" is a product — usually a vehicle — that has serious, recurring defects that cannot be fixed even after multiple repair attempts.

In many countries, lemon laws say: if a seller cannot fix a major defect after a reasonable number of tries, the buyer gets a replacement or a refund.

Malaysia is introducing this protection now — it was announced in Budget 2026 as an amendment to the Consumer Protection Act 1999.

For now, your main protections are: the CPA's implied guarantees, your manufacturer warranty, and TTPM if disputes arise.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

The term "lemon law" is widely used in Malaysia — but until recently, Malaysia had no dedicated lemon law. Consumer protections for defective goods have relied on the implied guarantees in the Consumer Protection Act 1999 (CPA), the terms of manufacturer warranties, and common law contract principles.

**This is changing.** The government announced the introduction of a Lemon Law — via amendments to the CPA — during the Budget 2026 presentation on 10 October 2025. The law has not yet been gazetted as of the time of writing.

This page covers:

* What the incoming Lemon Law will provide
* Your current rights under the CPA while the amendments are pending
* Manufacturer and extended warranty rights across vehicles, electronics, and appliances
* How to claim and what to do if refused

## Part 1: Malaysia's Incoming Lemon Law

### What Was Announced

Prime Minister Datuk Seri Anwar Ibrahim announced the Lemon Law as part of Budget 2026 on 10 October 2025. It will be implemented through **amendments to the Consumer Protection Act 1999**.

### Key Features of the Incoming Law

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Scope | New vehicles and other major consumer goods with persistent defects |
| Presumption of defect | If a serious defect appears within **6 months of purchase**, it is presumed to have existed at the time of sale — the burden shifts to the seller to prove otherwise |
| Available remedies | Repair, replacement, refund, or price reduction — depending on defect severity and persistence |
| Dispute resolution | Claims via TTPM (up to RM 50,000) without needing a lawyer |
| Applies to | Purchases made **after** the amendments are enacted — not retrospective |

### Why It Matters

Malaysia is catching up with Singapore, the US, South Korea, and China, which already have established lemon protections. The announcement followed widespread frustration among Malaysian vehicle buyers — particularly those with recurring defects in new cars that remained under manufacturer warranty but could not be permanently resolved.

### Current Status (As of This Page's Last Update)

| Stage | Status |
| --- | --- |
| Government announcement | ✅ Budget 2026 (October 2025) |
| Legal study | ✅ Completed (6-month study from March 2024) |
| Cabinet paper drafted | ✅ May 2025 |
| Parliamentary approval | ⏳ Pending |
| Gazetted and in force | ⏳ Not yet |

**What this means for you now:** The Lemon Law is not yet in force. Purchases made before enactment are governed by current CPA implied guarantees and warranty terms. Monitor KPDNHEP announcements for the gazette date.

## Part 2: Current Protections Under the CPA 1999

While the Lemon Law amendments are pending, your rights for defective products rest on the CPA's implied guarantees — which are substantial.

### The Relevant Guarantees

| Guarantee | What It Means for Defective Products |
| --- | --- |
| Acceptable quality (Section 32) | Goods must be safe, durable, and free from defects — including vehicles and electronics |
| Fitness for purpose (Section 33) | If you stated a specific use, the product must be fit for it |
| Right to repair, replace, or refund | Where goods fail implied guarantees, you are entitled to a remedy |
| Major failure = consumer's choice | For major defects, you choose between replacement and refund — seller cannot insist on repair |

Cross-reference: **Section 7.2 — Consumer Rights in Malaysia (CPA 1999)**

### Applying CPA to Persistent Defects (Pre-Lemon Law)

Even without a dedicated Lemon Law, the CPA provides meaningful protection for persistent defects:

* A vehicle or appliance that cannot be repaired after multiple attempts arguably fails the "acceptable quality" and "durable" standards
* A product unsafe due to defect fails the safety standard under Section 32
* Each failed repair attempt strengthens the case that the defect is a "major failure" entitling you to a refund or replacement

The challenge has been enforcement — taking a car manufacturer to TTPM for a RM 80,000 vehicle exceeds TTPM's RM 50,000 jurisdiction, pushing consumers to civil court. The incoming Lemon Law addresses this by creating clearer remedies within an accessible framework.

## Part 3: Manufacturer Warranties

### What a Manufacturer Warranty Is

A manufacturer warranty is a promise — made by the manufacturer or seller — that the product will perform as described for a specified period. It is additional to your CPA rights.

**Critical point:** A warranty does not replace your CPA rights. Even if a warranty period expires, you may still have CPA rights for defects that existed at the time of sale.

### Common Warranty Periods by Product Category

| Category | Typical Warranty | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| New vehicles | 3–5 years / 100,000 km (whichever comes first) | Some brands offer longer powertrain warranties |
| New motorcycles | 1–2 years | Varies by brand |
| Smartphones | 1 year (manufacturer) | Screen and battery often excluded or separate |
| Laptops and computers | 1 year (standard); extended available | On-site vs depot service varies |
| Home appliances (large) | 1–2 years (parts and labour) | Compressor on refrigerators / AC often 5–10 years |
| Home appliances (small) | 1 year | Often not worth repairing beyond warranty |
| Power tools | 1–2 years | Professional grade often longer |

### What Warranties Must Cover

Under Malaysian law, a warranty must:

* Be clearly stated — terms must be provided to the consumer
* Not reduce or replace the consumer's CPA rights
* Be honoured by an accessible service centre or authorised repairer

**A warranty that says "void if opened by consumer" or "does not cover manufacturing defects" may conflict with the CPA's implied guarantees and may be unenforceable** — particularly for defects that existed at the time of purchase.

### What Warranties Commonly Exclude

| Common Exclusion | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Physical damage by user | Legitimate exclusion |
| Water damage (electronics) | Usually legitimate — check IP rating |
| Normal wear and tear | Legitimate |
| Consumables (batteries, bulbs, filters) | Often excluded from main warranty |
| Damage from unauthorised repair | Legitimate if repair voided specific components |
| Commercial use of consumer product | Check if your use qualifies |

## Part 4: Extended Warranties — Are They Worth It?

Extended warranties (also called service plans or protection plans) are sold by retailers and manufacturers as add-ons beyond the standard warranty period.

### When Extended Warranties May Be Worth Considering

| Situation | Consideration |
| --- | --- |
| High-repair-cost product (large appliances, vehicles) | A single repair may exceed the extended warranty cost |
| Products with known reliability issues | Research the model — if it has a poor reliability record, extended cover has value |
| Products where you heavily depend on reliability (work laptop, main vehicle) | Downtime cost may justify the premium |
| Manufacturer's own extended warranty | Often better value than third-party retailer plans |

### When Extended Warranties Are Usually Not Worth It

| Situation | Why Not |
| --- | --- |
| Electronics with standard 1-year failure patterns | Most electronics fail early (covered by standard warranty) or late (beyond extended cover) — the middle period is lower risk |
| Low-cost items | Replacement cost may be close to extended warranty price |
| Third-party retailer warranties with complex claims processes | These often have exclusions that make claims difficult |
| Items you expect to replace or upgrade anyway | Paying for extended cover on something you will replace in 2 years |

### Key Questions Before Buying an Extended Warranty

* What exactly is covered — parts only, or parts and labour?
* Are there call-out or inspection fees on top of the plan cost?
* Who provides the repair — manufacturer authorised service centres or third-party?
* Is the warranty transferable if you sell the product?
* What happens if the warranty provider goes out of business?

## Part 5: How to Claim Under Warranty

### Step-by-Step Warranty Claim

```
Step 1: Identify the defect clearly
         → Describe specifically what is wrong, not just "it doesn't work"
         → Video evidence of the defect helps

Step 2: Check warranty validity
         → Confirm the product is within the warranty period
         → Confirm the defect is covered (not excluded)

Step 3: Contact the authorised service centre
         → Use manufacturer-authorised service — not third-party repairs
         → Third-party repairs may void your warranty for other components

Step 4: Submit for assessment
         → Bring: proof of purchase, warranty card, original packaging (if required)
         → Request a job sheet / repair order — keep a copy

Step 5: Follow up
         → Repair should be completed within a reasonable time
         → If parts are unavailable: ask for timeline or escalate

Step 6: Verify repair is complete
         → Test thoroughly before accepting the repaired product
         → If the same defect recurs after repair: document it — this strengthens a major failure claim
```

### Documenting for Persistent Defects

If the same defect recurs after one or more repairs:

| Record | Why |
| --- | --- |
| All job sheets / repair orders with dates | Shows repair history |
| Photos or videos of defect each time | Visual evidence |
| Communications with service centre | Shows you acted in good faith |
| Total time product was in for repair | Unreasonable downtime strengthens your case |

## Part 6: When Warranty Claims Are Refused

### Common Reasons for Refusal

| Reason Given | How to Respond |
| --- | --- |
| "Damage caused by user" | Ask for written evidence of how they determined this |
| "Warranty period expired" | Check if CPA implied guarantees still apply — a defect present at sale does not expire |
| "Tampered / third-party repair" | This may only void warranty for components affected by the repair — not the whole product |
| "Consumable item" | Confirm whether the failed component is genuinely classified as a consumable |
| "Not covered under this warranty" | Request the specific exclusion clause in writing |

### Escalation Path

```
1 Request written explanation of refusal from service centre
2 File a complaint with KPDNHEP (e-aduan.kpdn.gov.my)
3 File a claim at TTPM (ttpm.gov.my) for claims up to RM 50,000
4 For claims above RM 50,000 (vehicles, high-value goods) → civil court
  (or await the Lemon Law enactment for future purchases)
```

### Vehicle-Specific Escalation

For vehicle defects specifically, additional channels exist:

| Channel | What It Addresses |
| --- | --- |
| JPJ (Jabatan Pengangkutan Jalan) | Safety-related vehicle defects; recall notices |
| PUSPAKOM | Vehicle inspection and structural assessment |
| Manufacturer's Customer Service Head Office | Escalate above branch/dealer level |
| TTPM (within jurisdiction) | Claims up to RM 50,000 |
| Civil court | Claims above RM 50,000 — currently the main avenue for vehicle lemon cases |

## Practical Habits for Warranty Management

Consumers who successfully claim warranties tend to:

* Register products with the manufacturer immediately after purchase — some warranties require registration to be valid
* Keep all purchase receipts, warranty cards, and original packaging for the full warranty period
* Use only authorised service centres for repairs during the warranty period
* Request and keep all job sheets from every repair visit
* Document recurring defects with video — a video of a car stalling or a phone overheating is far more persuasive than a verbal description
* Never accept a repaired product without testing it thoroughly at the service centre

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: New car with recurring stalling — pre-Lemon Law**

A buyer purchased a new car that stalled intermittently from the third month. The authorised service centre repaired it three times over 14 months — same defect each time. Under the incoming Lemon Law, this pattern of persistent defect after multiple repairs would trigger remedy rights. Under current law, the buyer filed a KPDNHEP complaint and sought legal advice — the case was settled out of court with a replacement vehicle after the manufacturer weighed the reputational cost.

**Scenario 2: Laptop warranty refusal reversed**

A laptop purchased 8 months ago developed a motherboard fault. The service centre claimed it was caused by "liquid damage" despite the owner disputing this. The owner requested written evidence of the liquid damage finding. The service centre could not provide it. KPDNHEP's involvement resulted in the repair being honoured under warranty.

**Scenario 3: Extended warranty exclusion discovered too late**

A consumer paid RM 800 for an extended warranty on a washing machine. When the motor failed in year 3, the warranty provider cited an exclusion for "mechanical failure due to scale buildup" — a clause buried in the fine print. Reading the exclusion list before purchasing would have revealed this loophole. The consumer paid out of pocket.

## FAQ

**Q: When will the Lemon Law take effect?**
The Lemon Law was announced during Budget 2026 (October 2025) as an amendment to the CPA 1999. It requires parliamentary approval and gazettement before taking effect. Monitor KPDNHEP and the Attorney General's Chambers for the gazette date. It applies only to purchases made after enactment.

**Q: Does the current CPA cover vehicle defects?**
Yes — the CPA's implied guarantees (acceptable quality, fitness for purpose) apply to vehicles as consumer goods. The challenge is that vehicles often exceed TTPM's RM 50,000 claim limit, pushing disputes to civil court. The incoming Lemon Law addresses this with clearer remedies.

**Q: Can I void my warranty by servicing my car at a non-authorised workshop?**
For statutory warranty claims under the CPA, a seller cannot void your rights simply because you used a third-party service centre for routine maintenance. However, if the third-party repair caused or contributed to the specific defect you are claiming for, the manufacturer may have grounds to refuse. This is a contested area — seek legal advice for high-value claims.

**Q: My phone's screen cracked — is it covered by warranty?**
Screen cracks are almost universally excluded from manufacturer warranties as physical damage. However, if the crack occurred due to a manufacturing defect (spontaneous cracking without impact), document it carefully and escalate — this is a legitimate warranty claim even though it will be resisted.

**Q: Can I return a product just because I changed my mind?**
Not under the CPA — the right to return for change of mind is at the retailer's discretion. Retailers may offer a returns policy voluntarily (common in large retailers), but there is no legal right to return non-defective goods simply because you no longer want them. The CPA rights apply to defective goods only.
