## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is broadband?**

Broadband is the internet connection that comes into your home through a cable or fibre line — giving you Wi-Fi for all your devices.

In Malaysia, the main types are:

* **Fibre broadband** — fastest and most common in urban areas
* **Fixed wireless** — uses mobile signals; for areas without fibre
* **Satellite** — for rural and remote areas

The most important factors when choosing:

* Is the provider available at your address?
* How fast do you need it?
* What is the monthly cost and contract length?

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Home broadband in Malaysia has shifted almost entirely to fibre optic technology in urban and suburban areas. Several major providers operate their own fibre networks, while others resell or use shared infrastructure.

Provider availability is the first constraint — not all providers serve all addresses. Checking availability before comparing plans saves significant time.

## Part 2: Fibre vs Wireless Broadband

### Technology Comparison

| Type | How It Works | Speed Range | Reliability | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fibre (FTTH) | Dedicated fibre cable to your unit | 100 Mbps – 10 Gbps | High | Most households; consistent performance |
| Fixed Wireless (4G/5G) | Mobile signal received by a home router | 30 Mbps – 300 Mbps | Moderate | Renters; areas without fibre; temporary setups |
| Satellite | Signal from orbit via dish | 50 Mbps – 250 Mbps (Starlink) | Variable | Rural; remote locations; no other option |
| Cable / ADSL | Legacy copper wire (being phased out) | Up to 30 Mbps | Lower | Older properties not yet upgraded to fibre |

### Fibre: FTTH vs FTTB vs FTTN

In Malaysian high-rise residential contexts, you may encounter different fibre delivery types:

| Type | Meaning | Speed Impact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| FTTH (Fibre to the Home) | Fibre runs directly to your unit | Best — dedicated connection |
| FTTB (Fibre to the Building) | Fibre to building, copper/LAN within building | Good — shared within building |
| FTTN (Fibre to the Node) | Fibre to street cabinet, copper to premises | Lower — legacy setup |

For new condominiums and landed properties, FTTH is standard. Older buildings may use FTTB or FTTN.

## Part 3: Understanding Broadband Speeds

### What Mbps Means

Mbps = Megabits per second. This is the rate at which data is transferred.

**Download speed** affects how fast you receive data (streaming, browsing, downloading).
**Upload speed** affects how fast you send data (video calls, uploading files, live streaming).

Fibre plans are typically asymmetric — download speed is faster than upload.

### Speed Tiers and What They Mean in Practice

| Plan Speed | Suitable For | Not Suitable For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 30–50 Mbps | 1–2 users; light browsing, SD streaming | Multiple simultaneous 4K streams; heavy WFH |
| 100 Mbps | 2–4 users; HD streaming, video calls, WFH | Very large file downloads (gaming, editing) may feel slow with multiple users |
| 300–500 Mbps | 4–6 users; 4K streaming, gaming, heavy WFH | Rarely a bottleneck for household use |
| 1 Gbps | Power users, home offices, multiple heavy users | Most households will not saturate this |
| 2 Gbps+ | Small businesses, extreme multi-device households | Consumer use rarely justifies this cost |

### Specific Use Cases

| Activity | Minimum Speed | Recommended |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SD video streaming (Netflix, YouTube) | 3 Mbps | 5 Mbps |
| HD video streaming (1080p) | 5 Mbps | 10 Mbps |
| 4K video streaming | 25 Mbps | 50 Mbps per screen |
| Video call (Zoom, Teams, 1 person) | 3 Mbps up + down | 5 Mbps up + down |
| Work from home (multiple apps) | 10 Mbps | 30–50 Mbps |
| Online gaming (latency matters more than speed) | 3 Mbps | 10 Mbps; prioritise low ping |
| Large file downloads / game updates | 50 Mbps+ | 100 Mbps+ |

**Important: In Malaysia, a household of 3–4 people with typical usage (streaming, WFH, social media) is well-served by 100–300 Mbps.** Upgrading beyond this rarely produces a noticeable experience improvement for everyday use.

### Latency (Ping) — Why Gamers Care About More Than Speed

Latency is the delay between your action and the server's response — measured in milliseconds (ms).

* Under 20ms: Excellent — imperceptible delay
* 20–50ms: Good — suitable for most online gaming
* 50–100ms: Acceptable — noticeable in fast-paced games
* Over 100ms: Poor — significant lag in gaming; calls may stutter

Fibre broadband (especially TIME) generally offers lower latency than fixed wireless. Satellite has high latency by nature — even Starlink (40–60ms) is higher than fibre.

### Step 3: Compare Total Cost of Ownership

| Factor | What to Check |
| --- | --- |
| Monthly fee | Quoted price — note if promotional rate reverts to higher standard rate after 12 months |
| Installation / activation fee | Sometimes waived in promotions |
| Equipment deposit | Router deposit — refundable on termination |
| Contract length | 12 vs 24 months — and the early termination fee formula |
| Data cap | Most Malaysian fibre plans are uncapped — confirm |
| Bundle discounts | Mobile + broadband bundles can offer meaningful savings |

### Step 4: Read the Contract Before Signing

Key clauses to check:

* Early termination fee — typically the remaining months' fee or a fixed penalty
* Minimum guaranteed speed — what the provider commits to; may differ from advertised speed
* Fair use policy — if the plan is "unlimited," are there throttling conditions?
* Price lock period — does the price change after the promotional period?

## Part 5: Installation Process and What to Expect

### Standard Fibre Installation

```
Step 1: Apply online or at provider outlet — bring MyKad
Step 2: Receive installation appointment confirmation (typically 5–14 working days)
Step 3: Technician visits to install:
         → Fibre cable entry point (small hole drilled if needed)
         → ONT (Optical Network Terminal) — the fibre modem
         → Wi-Fi router (provided by ISP or bring your own)
Step 4: Test the connection with the technician present
Step 5: Service active — router configured with network name and password
```

### What to Prepare for Installation

* Be present at the premises during the appointment window
* Know where you want the router placed — cable length from ONT affects placement
* Prepare your unit number and access (strata title properties may require lift access or JMC coordination)
* Have your MyKad ready for identity verification

### Common Installation Issues

| Issue | Typical Cause | Resolution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Appointment rescheduled multiple times | High demand areas; technician availability | Contact provider and request expedited scheduling |
| No signal after installation | Fibre splice issue; ONT fault | Request technician callback immediately; do not accept "it will settle" |
| Slow speeds after installation | Router placement; Wi-Fi interference | Test via LAN cable first to isolate Wi-Fi vs line issue |
| No fibre port in building | Building not yet approved / wired | Escalate to MCMC; check alternative providers |

## Practical Habits for Broadband Consumers

Broadband consumers who get the best experience tend to:

* Check availability at their specific unit address — not just the building or road
* Test speed via **fast.com** or **speedtest.net** regularly — particularly after service issues
* Place routers centrally and elevated — not inside cabinets or behind TVs
* Use wired LAN (ethernet) for devices where speed matters (desktop PC, Smart TV, gaming console)
* Document service outages with screenshots of speed tests for complaint escalation
* Review their plan annually — promotional rates from new customers are often better than loyalty rates

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Provider not available at address**

A family signed up for TIME broadband after seeing excellent reviews — only to find at installation that their older condominium in Cheras was not yet wired for TIME's fibre. They had already given notice to their previous provider. Checking availability at their specific unit address before cancelling the existing service would have prevented the gap.

**Scenario 2: Speed upgrade not needed**

A household upgraded from 100 Mbps to 500 Mbps believing it would improve their Netflix and Zoom experience. The improvement was imperceptible — their issues were caused by Wi-Fi interference and router placement, not line speed. Testing via LAN cable first would have identified that the line was fine and router placement was the issue.

**Scenario 3: Persistent outage escalated to MCMC**

A WFH professional had repeated 2–4 hour outages every few weeks on her Unifi line. After four calls to Unifi support with temporary fixes, she documented the outage dates and speeds, filed an eAduan with MCMC, and included Unifi's case reference numbers. MCMC engaged Unifi directly. A permanent infrastructure fix was made within two weeks.

## FAQ

**Q: Is TIME or Unifi better in Malaysia?**
TIME is consistently rated higher for speed and reliability in its coverage areas — but its coverage is limited to denser urban areas (primarily Klang Valley, Penang, JB). Unifi has far wider coverage but more variable performance. If TIME is available at your address, it is worth considering for performance. If not, Unifi is the default for most Malaysians.

**Q: What is a good ping for gaming in Malaysia?**
Under 30ms ping to Southeast Asian game servers (Singapore, Japan) is excellent. Most fibre connections in major Malaysian cities achieve this. Fixed wireless tends to have higher ping (30–80ms). Satellite ping is higher still (40–100ms for Starlink; much higher for traditional satellite).

**Q: Can I use my own router instead of the provider's?**
Generally yes — most providers allow you to connect your own router to the ONT. You may need to configure PPPoE credentials provided by the ISP. Using a quality third-party router can significantly improve Wi-Fi coverage and performance over the ISP's bundled router.

**Q: What is the difference between download and upload speed?**
Download speed affects how fast you receive data — streaming, browsing, downloading files. Upload speed affects how fast you send data — video calls, uploading to cloud storage, live streaming. For most consumers, download speed matters more. For video conference-heavy WFH or content creators, upload speed is equally important.

**Q: Is Starlink available in Malaysia?**
Yes — Starlink launched residential service in Malaysia. It is most relevant for rural and remote areas without fibre or reliable fixed wireless. Urban users with good fibre options will generally prefer fibre for lower latency and cost. Check starlink.com for current Malaysian pricing and availability.
