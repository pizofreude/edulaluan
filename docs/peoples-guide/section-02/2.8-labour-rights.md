## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What are labour rights?**

Labour rights are rules that protect workers. These rules say:

* How many hours you can work
* When overtime must be paid
* When salary must be paid
* What leave you must receive
* How employers must treat workers

**Why is this important?**

Because not all employees know their rights. If you know the rules, you can protect yourself.

**Examples of basic rights:**

You generally have the right to:

* Receive your salary on time
* Get a payslip
* Receive overtime pay if eligible
* Take annual leave
* Take sick leave
* Work in a safe environment

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Malaysian employees are protected by several labour laws, primarily:

* Employment Act 1955
* Industrial Relations Act 1967
* Occupational Safety and Health Act 1994
* Minimum Wages Order

As of 2023 amendments, the Employment Act now covers **all employees regardless of salary**, although some benefits (like overtime eligibility) still depend on wage thresholds and job category.

Labour rights generally cover:

* Working hours
* Salary protection
* Leave entitlements
* Termination protection
* Workplace safety
* Anti-harassment protection

---

## Basic Employment Rights

### Working hours

Standard limits:

| Item                 | Rule                             |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Maximum weekly hours | 45 hours                         |
| Typical daily hours  | 8–9 hours                        |
| Rest day             | At least 1 day per week          |
| Breaks               | Required after long work periods |

---

### Overtime rights

Overtime usually applies when:

* Working beyond normal hours
* Working on rest days
* Working on public holidays

Typical overtime rates:

| Situation           | Minimum pay rate |
| ------------------- | ---------------- |
| Normal overtime     | 1.5× hourly rate |
| Rest day work       | 2× hourly rate   |
| Public holiday work | 3× hourly rate   |

Eligibility depends on job type and salary structure.

---

### Salary protection

Employers must:

* Pay salary no later than the 7th day after salary period
* Provide payslips
* Not make illegal deductions
* Pay agreed wages in full

Illegal deductions may include:

* Unexplained penalties
* Damage charges without process
* Forced purchases
* Deposits not allowed by law

---

### Leave entitlements (minimum standards)

Annual leave depends on years of service:

| Years of service  | Minimum annual leave |
| ----------------- | -------------------- |
| Less than 2 years | 8 days               |
| 2–5 years         | 12 days              |
| More than 5 years | 16 days              |

Sick leave entitlement:

| Situation          | Leave                           |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| No hospitalisation | 14–22 days depending on service |
| Hospitalisation    | Up to 60 days                   |

Other protected leave may include:

* Maternity leave (98 days)
* Paternity leave (7 days for eligible employees)
* Public holidays (minimum 11 days)

---

## Termination Rights

Employers cannot terminate employment arbitrarily without following proper procedures.

Proper termination usually requires:

* Notice period (as per contract)
* Payment in lieu of notice (if immediate termination)
* Valid reason (misconduct, redundancy, performance)

Typical notice periods:

| Length of service | Minimum notice |
| ----------------- | -------------- |
| Less than 2 years | 4 weeks        |
| 2–5 years         | 6 weeks        |
| More than 5 years | 8 weeks        |

Wrongful dismissal may be challenged at the Industrial Relations Department.

---

## Workplace Safety Rights

Employees have the right to:

* Safe working environment
* Safety equipment where required
* Training on workplace risks
* Reporting unsafe conditions

Workplace injuries should be reported immediately and may qualify for PERKESO claims.

---

## Harassment and Workplace Misconduct

Employers must address:

* Sexual harassment
* Workplace bullying
* Discrimination
* Abuse of authority

Employees can:

* Report internally (HR)
* File formal complaints
* Escalate to authorities if unresolved

---

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Unpaid overtime**

An employee worked late daily but received no overtime. After learning his rights, he raised the issue and received back pay.

**Scenario 2: Salary delay**

A company repeatedly paid salaries late. Employees reported the matter to the Labour Department, which ordered compliance.

**Scenario 3: Forced resignation**

A worker was pressured to resign instead of being formally terminated. This may qualify as constructive dismissal.

**Scenario 4: No rest day**

A retail worker worked 14 straight days without a rest day. This violates labour standards and can be reported.

### Path A: I think my rights are violated

**Step 1 of 2: Gather evidence**

Collect:

* Payslips
* Employment contract
* Attendance records
* Messages from employer
* Bank payment records

**Step 2 of 2: Try internal resolution**

Raise with:

* HR
* Immediate supervisor
* Company grievance process

If unresolved, escalate externally.

---

### Path B: I want to file a complaint

Possible agencies:

| Issue              | Agency                          |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Salary issues      | Labour Department (JTK)         |
| Wrongful dismissal | Industrial Relations Department |
| Safety issues      | DOSH                            |
| PERKESO issue      | PERKESO                         |

---

### Path C: I was unfairly terminated

**Step 1 of 2: Check your termination letter**

Verify:

* Reason given
* Notice period
* Final salary payment

**Step 2 of 2: File representation**

You may file a representation for unfair dismissal at the Industrial Relations Department within **60 days**.

---

### Path D: Employer forcing illegal practices

Examples:

* Forced unpaid overtime
* No rest days
* Withheld salary
* Illegal deductions

You may report these.

## FAQ

**Q: Does the Employment Act cover all employees?**
Yes, but some protections vary depending on salary level and job scope.

**Q: Can my employer force unpaid overtime?**
Generally no, unless exempt categories apply.

**Q: Can salary be delayed?**
Salary must normally be paid within 7 days after the salary period.

**Q: Can I be fired without warning?**
Only in serious misconduct cases following due process.

**Q: Should I sign a resignation if forced?**
You should seek advice first. Forced resignation may affect your rights.
