## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is JPP?**
JPP stands for Jabatan Perhubungan Perusahaan, or the Department of Industrial Relations. They help workers who were fired unfairly by their employer. They also help employers and workers settle serious workplace disagreements.

**When should you go to JPP?**
- Your employer fired you and you believe it was unfair or without good reason.
- Your employer forced you to resign by making your work environment unbearable.
- You want to be reinstated to your job after being dismissed.

**What is the most important thing to remember?**
You only have **60 days** from the date of dismissal to file your complaint. After that, JPP may not be able to help you.

**How do you contact JPP?**
- Website: jpp.mohr.gov.my
- Walk in to your nearest JPP state office with your IC and dismissal letter.

### 🧑 Detailed

**Overview**

Jabatan Perhubungan Perusahaan Malaysia (JPP) was established to promote and maintain positive and harmonious relations between employers and employees and their trade unions, to create a peaceful and stable industrial relations environment in the country.

JPP is in charge of managing and maintaining positive and harmonious relations between workers and employers and their trade unions, including settling workplace disputes between workers and employers, and the formation of collective agreements. JPP will first attempt to help both parties settle the issue out of court. If the matter cannot be resolved, JPP will then refer the case to the Industrial Court.

**Key legislation:**

| Act | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| Industrial Relations Act 1967 (IRA) | Unfair dismissal, reinstatement, trade unions, collective agreements |
| Employment Act 1955 | Used in conjunction for determining entitlements |

**Important 2021 amendment:**
Effective January 1, 2021, the Industrial Relations Act 1967 was amended to remove the Minister of Human Resources' role to refer unfair dismissal representations to the Industrial Court. Instead, the Director General of Industrial Relations (DGIR) is now empowered to directly refer cases to the Industrial Court. This streamlined the process significantly, removing a political bottleneck that used to delay many workers' cases for years.

**What JPP can and cannot do:**

| JPP Can Handle | JPP Cannot Handle |
|---|---|
| Unfair or wrongful dismissal | Unpaid wages (go to JTK, Page 2.1) |
| Constructive dismissal (forced resignation) | Workplace safety violations (go to JKKP, Page 2.3) |
| Reinstatement claims | Criminal matters |
| Collective agreement disputes | Complaints outside the 60-day window |
| Trade union recognition disputes | Civil suits (go to civil court) |

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Suriya dismissed without domestic inquiry**
Suriya was accused of poor performance and dismissed verbally on a Friday afternoon with no written warning, no performance improvement plan, and no domestic inquiry. Employers who fail to conduct a domestic inquiry or investigation before dismissal, or who do not give the employee an opportunity to respond or defend themselves, may be found to have carried out an unfair dismissal. Suriya filed with JPP within 30 days. The conciliation session resulted in a settlement of three months compensation.

**Scenario 2: Hafiz and constructive dismissal**
Hafiz's employer began reducing his responsibilities, removing his team, and cutting his allowances without explanation after he raised a concern about accounting irregularities. The working conditions became unbearable and he resigned. This is constructive dismissal. An employee can raise a claim for constructive dismissal at the Industrial Relations Department as per Section 20 of the Industrial Relations Act 1967. Hafiz filed within 60 days of his resignation date.

**Scenario 3: Retrenchment without proper process**
A manufacturing company retrenched 40 workers due to restructuring. Some workers suspected the retrenchment was used to remove union members selectively. JPP investigated whether the retrenchment was conducted fairly and whether proper selection criteria were applied. Workers who could demonstrate discriminatory selection had grounds for an unfair dismissal claim.

**Scenario 4: Rejected reinstatement offer at conciliation**
During a JPP conciliation session, a company offered an employee reinstatement without any admission of liability. The employee refused the offer. The DGIR took the view that since the company was prepared to reinstate the employee with arrears of salary being paid, the representation which could have been resolved was unreasonably rejected. This case is a reminder: carefully consider all reinstatement offers at the conciliation stage before refusing.

**Q: What is your situation?**
- I was dismissed and believe it was unfair or without just cause
- I was forced to resign due to unbearable working conditions
- I was retrenched and believe the process was unfair or discriminatory
- I am an employer responding to a JPP complaint

---

**Path A: I was dismissed and believe it was unfair**

Step 1 of 4: Act within 60 days of dismissal. This is a hard deadline.
- The clock starts on the date your employment officially ended.
- Do not wait to gather all evidence before filing. File first, gather additional evidence after.

Step 2 of 4: Prepare your documents.
- IC and dismissal letter (or letter of termination).
- Employment contract or offer letter.
- Any written warnings, performance reviews, or show cause letters.
- Payslips for the last 3 months.
- Any communications (emails, WhatsApp) relevant to the dismissal.
- Witness names if applicable.

Step 3 of 4: File your representation at the nearest JPP state office.
- Form: Representation Under Section 20(1) of the Industrial Relations Act 1967.
- Filing is free. No lawyer required at this stage.
- You will receive an acknowledgement and be assigned a case officer.

Step 4 of 4: Attend the JPP conciliation session.
- Both you and your employer will be invited to a conciliation session separately or together.
- JPP's goal is to reach a settlement without going to court.
- If a settlement is reached through compensation, the case ends at this stage. If no settlement is reached, the DGIR shall refer the case to the Industrial Court.

---

**Path B: I was forced to resign (constructive dismissal)**

Step 1 of 3: Understand constructive dismissal.
- Constructive dismissal occurs when an employer's conduct makes it impossible or unreasonable for you to continue working.
- Examples: sudden demotion without cause, salary reduction without agreement, harassment, removal of duties, relocation without contractual basis.
- Your resignation date is treated as your dismissal date for the 60-day deadline.

Step 2 of 3: Document the employer's conduct thoroughly.
- Dates and descriptions of incidents.
- Any written communications showing the change in conditions.
- Witness accounts if available.

Step 3 of 3: File with JPP within 60 days of your resignation.
- Same process as Path A above.
- Constructive dismissal cases are harder to prove. A lawyer's advice before filing is helpful, though not required.

---

**Path C: I was retrenched and believe the process was unfair**

Step 1 of 3: Distinguish between lawful retrenchment and unfair dismissal.
- Retrenchment due to genuine business restructuring is legal in Malaysia.
- It becomes an unfair dismissal claim if: selection was discriminatory, union members were specifically targeted, or the retrenchment was a pretext to remove you specifically.

Step 2 of 3: Check your retrenchment entitlements with JTK first.
- Termination benefits, notice pay, and outstanding leave encashment are handled by JTK (Page 2.1).
- JPP handles the fairness of the retrenchment process itself.

Step 3 of 3: File with JPP within 60 days if you believe the retrenchment was discriminatory or carried out in bad faith.

---

**Path D: I am an employer responding to a JPP complaint**

Step 1 of 2: Attend the conciliation session when notified.
- Non-attendance without valid reason reflects poorly on your case.
- Bring your HR records, show cause letters, domestic inquiry minutes, and any performance documentation.

Step 2 of 2: Consider the conciliation outcome carefully.
- The employer carries the burden of proof to show the dismissal was with just cause or excuse in the Industrial Court. Settlement at conciliation stage is often more cost-effective than a full Industrial Court hearing.

---

## Remedies Available if Your Case Succeeds

If the Industrial Court finds in your favour, it may award: reinstatement to your previous job with full backwages; compensation in lieu of reinstatement of up to 24 months backwages for permanent employees, or up to 12 months for probationers; 1 month salary per year of service as compensation instead of reinstatement; and additional payments such as gratuities, unclaimed leave, or bonuses depending on the employment contract.

## FAQ

**Q: What is the difference between JPP and JTK?**
JTK enforces employment terms, wages, and entitlements under the Employment Act 1955. JPP handles the fairness of dismissal and reinstatement under the Industrial Relations Act 1967. If your employer owes you unpaid wages AND fired you unfairly, you file with JTK for wages and JPP for the dismissal. Both complaints can run simultaneously.

**Q: Do I need a lawyer to file with JPP?**
No. Filing with JPP and attending conciliation does not require a lawyer. If the case proceeds to the Industrial Court, parties may self-represent or be represented by an advocate with the court's permission. Legal advice before filing is helpful for complex cases.

**Q: What if I missed the 60-day deadline?**
Filing a claim with JPP must be done within 60 days from dismissal. Missing this window typically bars you from the JPP and Industrial Court route. You may still have options through the civil court, but these are more expensive and complex. Consult a lawyer immediately if you have missed the deadline.

**Q: Can a probationer file for unfair dismissal?**
Yes, though the threshold for proving unfair dismissal is slightly higher for probationers. Employers have more discretion during probation but still cannot dismiss without any basis. Probationers may receive up to 12 months backwages if the Industrial Court rules in their favour.

**Q: What is constructive dismissal and how is it different from resignation?**
Constructive dismissal is when an employer's conduct leaves you with no reasonable choice but to resign. It is treated legally as a dismissal, not a voluntary resignation. The key difference is that you must prove the employer's actions fundamentally breached your employment contract. It is harder to prove than direct dismissal, so document everything carefully.
