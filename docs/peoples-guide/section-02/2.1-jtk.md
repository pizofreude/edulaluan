## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is JTK?**
JTK stands for Jabatan Tenaga Kerja, or the Department of Labour. They make sure employers treat workers fairly and follow the law. If your boss does something unfair, JTK is the place to go.

**What can JTK help you with?**
- Your salary was not paid or was paid late.
- You were fired unfairly.
- Your employer is not following the law.
- You are not getting the leave or benefits you are entitled to.

**How do you contact JTK?**
- Walk in to your nearest JTK office.
- Use the Working for Workers (WFW) app on your phone.
- Website: jtksm.mohr.gov.my (Peninsular Malaysia)

**Remember:**
- JTK is free to use. You do not need a lawyer to file a complaint.
- Your employer cannot fire you for making a complaint to JTK.

### 🧑 Detailed

**Overview**

Jabatan Tenaga Kerja Semenanjung Malaysia (JTKSM), under the Ministry of Human Resources (MOHR), is responsible for overseeing employment laws, resolving workplace disputes, and ensuring fair labour practices across Peninsular Malaysia. Sabah and Sarawak operate their own separate JTK offices under state jurisdiction with similar but distinct legislation.

**Key legislation enforced by JTK:**

| Act | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| Employment Act 1955 (Act 265) | Core employment terms, wages, leave, dismissal, overtime |
| Minimum Wages Order 2024 | Minimum wage rates nationwide |
| Workers' Minimum Standards of Housing and Amenities Act 1990 | Employer-provided accommodation standards |
| National Wages Consultative Council Act 2011 | Wage policy and enforcement |

**Important 2023 and 2024 updates:**
- Effective January 1, 2023, all employers are legally required to issue itemised payslips to employees.
- The Employment Act 1955 now covers all employees regardless of their wages, ensuring equal protection for all workers.
- Since October 1, 2024, the salary ceiling for both EIS and SOCSO contributions has increased from RM5,000 to RM6,000 for both local and foreign employees.

**Key employee entitlements under the Employment Act 1955:**

| Entitlement | Details |
|---|---|
| Working hours | Normal working hours must not exceed 45 hours per week |
| Annual leave | Less than 2 years: 8 days. 2 to 5 years: 12 days. More than 5 years: 16 days |
| Sick leave | Less than 2 years: 14 days. 2 to 5 years: 16 days. More than 5 years: 22 days |
| Hospitalisation leave | Up to 60 days |
| Maternity leave | 98 consecutive days including rest days and public holidays |
| Paternity leave | 7 consecutive days including rest days and public holidays |
| Overtime pay | Not less than 1.5x the hourly rate for hours beyond regular working hours. 3x the hourly rate on paid public holidays |
| Minimum wage | RM1,700 per month from February 1, 2025 for employers with 5 or more employees |

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Farid's unpaid salary**
Farid resigned from his job after his employer stopped paying his salary for two months. His employer claimed the company was facing cash flow problems. Farid filed a complaint with JTKSM via the WFW app. JTK investigated and ordered the employer to pay all outstanding wages within a specified period. Employees do not need a lawyer for this process.

**Scenario 2: Suriya fired without notice**
Suriya was verbally told her services were no longer needed on a Friday afternoon. No written notice was given. No termination benefits were offered. Under the Employment Act 1955, employers must provide written notice based on the duration of service. Suriya filed a complaint with JTK and was subsequently referred to the Industrial Relations Department (JPP) for an unfair dismissal claim.

**Scenario 3: Foreign worker housed in overcrowded quarters**
A group of migrant workers in a factory in Johor were housed in a building with broken toilets and no ventilation. A local NGO filed a complaint with JTKSM on their behalf. JTKSM conducted an inspection under the Workers' Minimum Standards of Housing and Amenities Act 1990 and issued a compliance notice to the employer.

**Scenario 4: Flexible work request ignored**
Wani, a mother of two, submitted a written request for flexible working hours under the Employment Act 1955. Her employer did not respond for over three months. Under Sections 60P and 60Q, employers must respond within 60 days, providing reasons and explaining reasonable business grounds in case of rejection. Wani filed a complaint with JTKSM for failure to respond within the statutory period.

**Q: What is your issue?**
- My salary was not paid or was underpaid
- I was terminated without proper notice or benefits
- My employer is not providing legal entitlements (leave, overtime, etc.)
- My workplace conditions are unsafe or unlawful
- I want to understand my rights before filing anything

---

**Path A: My salary was not paid or was underpaid**

Step 1 of 4: Gather your evidence.
- Payslips (last 3 to 6 months).
- Bank statements showing salary deposits.
- Employment contract or offer letter.
- Any written or WhatsApp communication about salary delays.

Step 2 of 4: File a complaint with JTK.
- App: Working for Workers (WFW), available on App Store and Google Play.
- Walk-in: nearest JTK office with your IC and evidence.
- Online: jtksm.mohr.gov.my (Peninsular Malaysia).

Step 3 of 4: Attend the JTK conciliation session.
- Both you and your employer will be called in separately or together.
- JTK officers will mediate. Most wage disputes are resolved at this stage.
- If unresolved, the case proceeds to the Labour Court.

Step 4 of 4: Labour Court hearing.
- Free of charge. No lawyer required.
- JTK officers represent the process. You present your evidence.
- Orders can include full payment of outstanding wages plus interest.

---

**Path B: I was terminated without proper notice or benefits**

Step 1 of 3: Confirm your entitlements.

Notice period under Employment Act 1955:

| Length of Service | Notice Period |
|---|---|
| Less than 2 years | 4 weeks |
| 2 to 5 years | 6 weeks |
| More than 5 years | 8 weeks |

Termination benefits (for those earning below RM4,000 or covered under Act):

| Length of Service | Benefit Per Year |
|---|---|
| 1 to 2 years | 10 days wages |
| 2 to 5 years | 15 days wages |
| More than 5 years | 20 days wages |

Step 2 of 3: Determine the right agency.
- Unpaid termination benefits and notice pay: file with JTK.
- Unfair or wrongful dismissal (no just cause): file with JPP (Industrial Relations Department). See Page 2.2.

Step 3 of 3: File your complaint within 60 days of termination.
- Delays beyond 60 days may affect your eligibility for certain claims.
- Bring your termination letter, employment contract, and IC.

---

**Path C: I am not receiving legal entitlements**

Step 1 of 2: Check which entitlements apply to you.
- Use the entitlement table above as a reference.
- Confirm your entitlement category based on your salary and years of service.

Step 2 of 2: File a complaint with JTK.
- Common complaints: no payslip issued, annual leave denied, overtime not paid, maternity leave disrupted.
- JTK will conduct a workplace inspection if warranted.

---

**Path D: My workplace conditions are unsafe or unlawful**

Step 1 of 1: For physical workplace safety issues such as dangerous machinery, chemical exposure, or inadequate safety equipment, this falls under JKKP (DOSH), not JTK. See Page 2.3.
- For employer-provided accommodation issues: file with JTKSM under the Workers' Minimum Standards of Housing Act.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a lawyer to file a complaint with JTK?**
No. The JTK complaint and Labour Court process is designed for employees to self-represent. JTK officers will guide you through the process at no cost.

**Q: Can my employer fire me for making a complaint to JTK?**
Retaliatory dismissal after filing a JTK complaint is a separate offence. If this happens, file an additional complaint immediately. Document all communications with your employer after the original filing date.

**Q: What is the difference between JTK and JPP?**
JTK handles wage disputes, entitlement violations, and employment conditions under the Employment Act 1955. JPP (Industrial Relations Department) handles unfair dismissal and reinstatement cases under the Industrial Relations Act 1967. Some cases overlap and JTK will refer you to JPP if needed. JPP is covered in Page 2.2.

**Q: Does the Employment Act cover all workers in Malaysia?**
The Employment Act 1955 covers Peninsular Malaysia and Labuan. Sabah is governed by the Sabah Labour Ordinance and Sarawak by the Sarawak Labour Ordinance. Labour laws in Peninsular Malaysia are mainly regulated by the Employment Act of 1955. Sabah is regulated by the Sabah labour ordinance and Sarawak is regulated by the Sarawak labour ordinance. Foreign workers are also covered under the Act.

**Q: What is the minimum wage in Malaysia right now?**
Starting February 1, 2025, employers with five or more employees must pay workers at least RM1,700 monthly. Small businesses with fewer than five employees can pay RM1,500 until July 31, 2025, after which RM1,700 applies to them as well. Domestic workers are excluded from the national minimum wage order.
