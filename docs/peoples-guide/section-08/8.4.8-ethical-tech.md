## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**Where does your phone come from?**

Your smartphone contains metals mined from multiple countries, assembled in factories with thousands of workers, shipped around the world before it reaches you.

Along the way, several questions arise:

* Are the miners paid fairly and working safely?
* Are factory workers treated well, or working excessive hours under poor conditions?
* What happens to the phone when it breaks or you replace it?

Ethical tech consumption means thinking about these questions and making choices informed by them, where you can.

No one has a perfectly ethical phone. But awareness and small choices, at scale, push companies toward better practices.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

The technology products we use daily; smartphones, laptops, earphones, smart home devices; are the visible endpoints of long, complex global supply chains involving mining, component manufacturing, assembly, logistics, software development, and platform operations.

At each stage, choices have been made about labour conditions, environmental standards, and corporate accountability. Some of those choices reflect values we would endorse if we could see them. Others do not.

This page takes a balanced view: individual consumer choices have real but limited power; systemic change requires regulation, corporate accountability, and collective action. Both the personal and structural dimensions matter.

## Part 1: Labour Rights in Tech Supply Chains

### The Supply Chain Journey of a Smartphone

A modern smartphone contains components sourced from dozens of countries, assembled in factories, typically in East Asia, before reaching Malaysian consumers.

The labour concerns span the full chain:

| Stage | Common Labour Concerns |
| --- | --- |
| Mining (cobalt, lithium, coltan) | Child labour in artisanal mines (particularly DRC cobalt); dangerous conditions; poverty wages |
| Component manufacturing (chips, screens) | Excessive working hours; restricted mobility for migrant workers; factory dormitory conditions |
| Final assembly (China, Vietnam, India) | Long hours; productivity pressure; limited union rights |
| Logistics and delivery | Gig worker classification; inadequate pay and protections for last-mile delivery workers |
| Platform labour (content moderation, data labelling) | Low pay; psychologically damaging work; often in Global South countries |

### The Foxconn Example

Foxconn, the world's largest electronics contract manufacturer, assembling iPhones and devices for most major brands, has faced documented concerns about:

* Working hours exceeding legal limits
* Physical working conditions
* Worker welfare and mental health

Multiple major brands (Apple, Dell, HP, Microsoft) have committed to auditing their supply chains and improving conditions. Progress has been made but challenges remain.

### Platform Labour in Malaysia

Malaysia is directly part of the tech labour ecosystem:

* **Gig workers**: Delivery riders (Grab, Lalamove, Foodpanda) work without employment protections under current Malaysian classification
* **Content moderators**: Malaysian workers employed by outsourced moderation firms reviewing harmful content for major platforms, work that carries documented psychological impact
* **Factory workers**: Malaysia hosts factories for electronics and component manufacturing; occupational health and labour rights protections apply but enforcement quality varies

Cross-reference: **Section 2.8 — Know Your Labour Rights** for Malaysian workers in these sectors.

## Part 2: Environmental Impact of Tech Consumption

### The Full Lifecycle

Tech devices have environmental impact at every stage:

| Stage | Impact |
| --- | --- |
| Raw material extraction | Mining disrupts ecosystems; produces toxic waste; is energy-intensive |
| Manufacturing | High energy consumption; chemical use; water consumption |
| Shipping and logistics | Carbon footprint of global supply chains |
| Use | Electricity consumption; data centres powering cloud services draw enormous energy |
| Disposal | E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally |

### E-Waste in Malaysia

Malaysia is both a producer of e-waste (discarded devices by consumers) and, historically, a destination for imported e-waste from wealthier countries — a practice restricted but not eliminated by legislation.

Key facts:

* Malaysians generate a growing volume of e-waste annually — smartphones, laptops, cables, batteries
* Informal e-waste recycling — burning or acid-washing components to extract metals — releases toxic substances
* Formal e-waste collection points exist but are underutilised

**How to dispose of devices responsibly in Malaysia:**

| Method | Where |
| --- | --- |
| Manufacturer take-back | Some brands (Dell, HP, Apple) offer trade-in or recycling programmes |
| Retailer recycling | AEON, Harvey Norman, and selected electronics retailers have e-waste collection |
| MyeWaste | Government-facilitated e-waste recycling programme — myewaste.gov.my |
| Scheduled Waste Licensed Collectors | For businesses with larger volumes — DOE licensed facilities |

### Cloud Computing and Energy

Every search, video stream, and cloud sync consumes energy at a data centre. Global data centres collectively consume approximately 1–2% of world electricity — comparable to the aviation industry.

Major tech companies (Google, Microsoft, Apple) have made net-zero commitments with varying credibility. Smaller providers vary widely.

This does not mean avoiding cloud services — it means being aware that "the cloud" has physical infrastructure with real environmental costs.

## Part 3: Corporate Accountability — How to Research a Tech Company's Practices

### Why Research Matters

Purchasing decisions are one lever consumers have. Researching before buying — or before deciding which platform to support with your attention and data — is a form of accountability.

### Useful Research Tools and Resources

| Resource | What It Covers | Link |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fairphone Transparency Reports | Supply chain transparency benchmark | fairphone.com |
| Electronic Industry Citizenship Coalition (EICC / RBA) | Responsible Business Alliance audits of electronics supply chains | responsiblebusiness.org |
| Greenpeace Guide to Greener Electronics | Environmental performance of major tech brands | greenpeace.org/greener-electronics |
| Know The Chain | Supply chain benchmark across industries including tech | knowthechain.org |
| Ranking Digital Rights | How tech companies respect user rights (privacy, expression) | rankingdigitalrights.org |
| Corporate Human Rights Benchmark | Overall human rights performance of major companies | corporatebenchmark.org |

### What to Look For

When researching a tech company:

| Question | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Does the company publish supply chain audits? | Transparency indicates accountability |
| Does it have a formal conflict minerals policy? | Addresses cobalt and coltan mining concerns |
| Does it allow independent third-party audits? | Self-reporting has limited credibility |
| Does it have a credible net-zero or environmental commitment? | With timeline and methodology, not just a pledge |
| How does it score on Ranking Digital Rights? | Indicates commitment to user privacy and freedom of expression |
| Has it faced credible labour or environmental violations? | Search "[company] labour violations" or "[company] environmental scandal" |

### The Limits of Corporate Accountability

Corporate social responsibility (CSR) and supply chain audits have well-documented limitations:

* Audits can be gamed — factories prepare for announced audits
* Complex supply chains make full traceability extremely difficult
* Voluntary commitments without binding enforcement have inconsistent follow-through
* Ranking systems use publicly available data — companies with poor transparency rank poorly but are not necessarily performing poorly

This does not make research useless — it means interpreting findings with appropriate scepticism.

## Part 4: What Ethical Tech Consumption Looks Like in Practice

### The Individual vs Systemic Tension

Ethical consumption faces a genuine structural challenge: the conditions being criticised are embedded in global supply chains that individual purchasing decisions can influence only marginally.

Buying a Fairphone instead of an iPhone does not clean up cobalt mining in the DRC. Using Signal instead of WhatsApp does not change Meta's fundamental business model.

At the same time, aggregate consumer choices do influence corporate behaviour over time — particularly when combined with:

* Regulatory pressure (the EU's supply chain due diligence laws are the most significant example)
* Investor pressure (ESG investing, shareholder activism)
* Journalism and civil society scrutiny

Individual action matters most when it is part of a broader pattern of demand — and when it is combined with advocacy for systemic change.

### Practical Choices Available to Malaysian Consumers

| Choice | Impact | Ease |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Keep devices longer | Reduces manufacturing demand; greatest individual environmental impact | Easy |
| Buy refurbished devices | Extends device life; reduces e-waste and new manufacturing | Moderate |
| Dispose of e-waste properly | Prevents toxic informal recycling | Easy (once you know where to go) |
| Research brands before purchasing | Supports more accountable companies | Moderate |
| Use open source software on existing devices | Reduces dependence on data-exploitative platforms | Easy to moderate |
| Support regulation of tech supply chains | Systemic — but individual advocacy matters | Variable |

### Keeping Devices Longer — The Highest-Impact Action

The single most effective individual environmental action in tech consumption is **keeping your device longer**.

Every year a phone is not replaced:

* Avoids the manufacturing energy and material cost of a new device
* Reduces e-waste
* Reduces demand for conflict minerals

The pressure to upgrade annually — through planned obsolescence, software update cut-offs, and marketing — is a commercial incentive, not a technical necessity.

**Software updates and longevity:** Manufacturers that provide longer software update support (Apple currently provides the longest iOS support; Google Pixel and some Samsung flagships have extended support) extend the usable life of devices. This is a meaningful purchasing criterion.

## Part 5: Malaysian Context

### Malaysia in the Global Tech Supply Chain

Malaysia participates in the global tech supply chain as both a manufacturing country and a consumer market:

* Malaysia hosts significant semiconductor and electronics manufacturing — including Intel, Western Digital, and Malaysian companies in the Penang tech corridor
* Malaysian workers in these facilities are covered by Malaysian labour law — though enforcement quality and worker voice vary
* Malaysia's SOSCO and EPF provide some protections for formal sector workers; gig workers in the platform economy have less coverage

### Malaysian E-Waste Context

* The Department of Environment (DOE) regulates scheduled waste including e-waste
* MyeWaste programme facilitates formal e-waste collection
* Informal e-waste processing, particularly from imported waste, remains a documented concern
* Consumer awareness of proper e-waste disposal remains low

### Local Ethical Tech Initiatives

| Organisation / Initiative | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| Centre for Independent Journalism (CIJ) Malaysia | Digital rights, platform accountability |
| Suhakam (Human Rights Commission) | Human rights including labour rights in supply chains |
| Persatuan Sahabat Alam Malaysia (SAM) / Friends of the Earth Malaysia | Environmental advocacy including e-waste |
| Malaysian Trades Union Congress (MTUC) | Labour rights for workers in tech manufacturing |

## A Realistic Summary: What Each of Us Can Do

Ethical tech consumption is not about achieving purity. It is about making better choices where you can, staying informed, and supporting the systemic changes that individual choices alone cannot create.

| Action | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Keep your phone for 3–4 years instead of upgrading annually | Largest individual environmental impact |
| Dispose of devices at formal e-waste collection points | Prevents toxic informal processing |
| Choose brands with credible supply chain transparency when you do buy | Marginal but real influence on corporate behaviour |
| Support regulation of tech supply chains | Systemic leverage that individual choices alone lack |
| Stay informed about the labour and environmental conditions behind your devices | Informed citizens make better collective decisions |

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: E-waste disposed irresponsibly**

A family upgraded their smartphones and discarded four old devices — including a laptop, two phones, and a tablet — in regular household rubbish. These devices contain lead, mercury, and other toxic substances that leach into soil and groundwater in landfills. Bringing them to MyeWaste or a retailer recycling point would have taken 20 minutes and prevented the contamination.

**Scenario 2: Supply chain research before purchase**

A consumer researching a laptop purchase spent 10 minutes on Greenpeace's Guide to Greener Electronics and Ranking Digital Rights. She found that her shortlisted brand had a significantly lower environmental score than an alternative with comparable specs and price. She chose the higher-scoring brand — a small signal in aggregate purchasing data that responsible companies do track.

**Scenario 3: Gig worker rights as an ethical tech dimension**

A frequent Grab Food user learned that delivery riders in Malaysia are classified as independent contractors — not employees — meaning they lack EPF contributions, sick leave, and SOCSO protections for ordinary illness. He began tipping consistently and supported advocacy for gig worker protections — recognising that his convenience came at a cost to the workers behind it.

## FAQ

**Q: Is there a truly ethical smartphone?**
No — but some are meaningfully better than others. Fairphone (a Dutch company) is the most transparent about its supply chain and designs for repairability — though it is not widely available in Malaysia. Among mainstream brands, Apple and Google have made the most progress on environmental commitments and software longevity. No option is without concern.

**Q: Does buying refurbished help?**
Yes — significantly. Refurbished devices extend the life of existing hardware, reduce demand for new manufacturing, and are typically cheaper. In Malaysia, refurbished electronics are available through platforms like Mudah, Carousell, and specialist refurbishers. Verify the condition grading and warranty before purchasing.

**Q: Is it hypocritical to criticise tech companies while using their products?**
No. Critique is not the same as abstaining. Using the available tools while advocating for better conditions is both coherent and necessary — most meaningful social change has come from participants in a system calling for its improvement, not from those who opted out entirely.

**Q: Does individual action on tech consumption actually matter?**
In direct market terms — marginally. In aggregate and in combination with advocacy, journalism, and regulation — yes. The EU's supply chain due diligence laws (Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive) are the most significant external pressure on tech supply chains, and they emerged from sustained civil society and consumer advocacy. Individual action is most effective when it is part of a broader ecosystem of pressure.

**Q: How do I know if an e-waste collector is legitimate?**
Check whether the collector is licensed by Malaysia's Department of Environment (DOE) as a scheduled waste contractor. MyeWaste's listed partners are verified. Informal collectors offering cash for old electronics may not process waste responsibly.
