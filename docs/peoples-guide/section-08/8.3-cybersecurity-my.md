### 🧒 Simple

**What is CyberSecurity Malaysia?**

CyberSecurity Malaysia is a government agency that helps protect Malaysians and Malaysian organisations from online threats.

If your email, social media, or online accounts are hacked — or if you suspect malware on your device — you can report it to them via **Cyber999**.

Common threats they help with:

* Hacked accounts
* Malware and ransomware
* Phishing attacks
* Data breaches
* Website defacement

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

**CyberSecurity Malaysia** is a national agency under the Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation (MOSTI). It serves as Malaysia's primary body for cybersecurity incident response, awareness, and capability development.

Its operational arm — **Cyber999** — is the national Computer Emergency Response Team (CERT), providing incident response support to individuals, businesses, and government entities.

CyberSecurity Malaysia does not enforce laws (that is MCMC and PDRM's role) — it provides technical assistance, incident coordination, and security advisory.

## Part 1: What CyberSecurity Malaysia Does

### Core Functions

| Function | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| Incident response (Cyber999) | Receives and responds to cybersecurity incident reports from individuals and organisations |
| Vulnerability research | Identifies and discloses security vulnerabilities in Malaysian digital infrastructure |
| Security awareness | Produces public education on cybersecurity threats and best practices |
| Digital forensics | Provides forensic analysis support for cybercrime investigations (works with PDRM) |
| Certification and standards | Administers cybersecurity professional certifications and standards for the industry |
| Critical information infrastructure (CII) protection | Protects banking, utilities, healthcare, and government digital systems |

### What CyberSecurity Malaysia Is Not

| Misconception | Reality |
| --- | --- |
| A law enforcement agency | PDRM investigates cybercrime — Cyber999 provides technical support |
| An ISP or network operator | Cannot block sites or intercept traffic (that is MCMC / provider level) |
| A consumer compensation body | Cannot recover lost funds — refer to bank and PDRM |
| A replacement for antivirus software | Provides guidance and incident response, not real-time device protection |

## Part 3: Common Cyber Threats in Malaysia

### Account Takeover

The most common cyber incident affecting individual Malaysians.

| Method | How It Happens |
| --- | --- |
| Credential stuffing | Attacker uses username/password lists from previous data breaches |
| Phishing | Fake login page harvests credentials |
| SIM swapping | Attacker convinces telco to transfer your number; receives OTP |
| Malware keylogger | Malicious software records your keystrokes |
| Social engineering | Attacker tricks you into revealing credentials directly |

**Warning signs of account takeover:**

* Unexpected login notifications from unfamiliar locations
* Password reset emails you did not request
* Contacts receiving messages you did not send
* Account locked or email changed without your action

### Malware

Malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorised access to devices.

| Malware Type | What It Does |
| --- | --- |
| Ransomware | Encrypts your files and demands payment for decryption key |
| Trojan | Disguises itself as legitimate software; creates backdoor access |
| Spyware | Monitors activity and exfiltrates data silently |
| Adware | Delivers unwanted ads; often bundled with free software |
| Banking malware | Specifically targets online banking sessions |

**Common infection vectors in Malaysia:**

* APK files (Android apps) downloaded from non-official sources
* Email attachments from unknown senders
* Links in WhatsApp or Telegram messages from unknown contacts
* Pirated software downloads

### Phishing

Fraudulent communications designed to steal credentials or deliver malware.

| Variant | Channel |
| --- | --- |
| Email phishing | Fake bank, government, or platform emails with malicious links |
| SMS phishing (smishing) | Fake delivery, banking, or government SMS with links |
| Voice phishing (vishing) | Phone calls impersonating banks or authorities |
| WhatsApp phishing | Fake messages from compromised contacts or unknown numbers |

**In Malaysia:** Phishing frequently impersonates Maybank, CIMB, Public Bank, LHDN, Pos Malaysia, and major delivery companies.

### Data Breach

Unauthorised access to and exfiltration of personal or organisational data.

**What happens when your data is breached:**

* Email/password combinations sold on dark web marketplaces
* IC numbers used for identity fraud
* Credit card details used for unauthorised purchases
* Personal information used to craft targeted scams

**Check if your email has been in a known breach:**

* **haveibeenpwned.com** — free, globally recognised breach database
* If your email appears: change the password for that service and any others using the same password

## Part 4: Personal Cybersecurity Habits

### The Core Five

These five habits prevent the vast majority of common cyber incidents:

| Habit | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Use unique passwords for every account | Password reuse means one breach compromises all accounts |
| Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts | Even if password is stolen, attacker cannot log in without second factor |
| Keep devices and apps updated | Most updates patch known security vulnerabilities |
| Only install apps from official stores | APK files from unofficial sources are the primary malware vector in Malaysia |
| Never share OTP, TAC, or login codes — with anyone | Banks and governments never ask for these; anyone who does is a threat |

### Password Management

| Approach | Recommendation |
| --- | --- |
| Password manager | Strongest option — generates and stores unique passwords (Bitwarden is free and open source; 1Password and Dashlane are paid alternatives) |
| Strong passphrases | Four or more random words (e.g. "correct-horse-battery-staple") — easier to remember, hard to crack |
| Avoid | Single words, names, dates of birth, "password123" variants |

### Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

| 2FA Method | Security Level | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy, Microsoft Authenticator) | High | Best option — not vulnerable to SIM swap |
| Hardware key (YubiKey) | Highest | Strongest protection; more complex setup |
| SMS OTP | Medium | Vulnerable to SIM swapping — avoid for high-value accounts |
| Email OTP | Medium-Low | Only as secure as your email account |

Enable 2FA on: email, banking apps, social media, government portals, work systems — in that priority order.

### Device Security

| Habit | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Screen lock with PIN/biometric | Prevents physical access to device |
| Full-device encryption | Enabled by default on most modern smartphones |
| Remote wipe capability | Enable "Find My Device" or equivalent |
| Regular backups | Cloud or offline backup protects against ransomware |
| Avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions | Use mobile data or VPN on public networks |

## Part 5: Incident Response — What to Do After a Breach

### If Your Account Is Compromised

```
Immediately:
1. Change your password from a secure device
2. Revoke all active sessions (most platforms: Settings → Security → Active Sessions → Sign Out All)
3. Check if recovery email and phone number have been changed — restore if so
4. Enable 2FA if not already active
5. Check sent messages and activity logs for what the attacker did

Within 24 hours:
6. Change passwords on any other accounts using the same password
7. Notify contacts if attacker sent messages in your name
8. Report to the platform (Facebook, Google, etc.) and to Cyber999
```

### If Your Device Has Malware

```
1. Disconnect from the internet immediately (Wi-Fi and mobile data off)
2. Do not use the device for any financial or sensitive access
3. Run a reputable antivirus scan (Malwarebytes, Kaspersky, Windows Defender)
4. If ransomware: do NOT pay — contact Cyber999 first; free decryption tools exist for some variants
5. If device cannot be cleaned: factory reset (ensure backup is not also infected)
6. Report to Cyber999 (cyber999.my)
```

### If Your Personal Data Is Leaked

```
1. Change passwords for affected service and any reused passwords
2. Enable 2FA on affected account
3. Monitor bank accounts and CCRIS for suspicious activity
4. If IC number was leaked: alert your bank; consider a credit freeze if available
5. Report to Personal Data Protection Commissioner (pdp.gov.my) if the leak was by a Malaysian organisation
6. Report to Cyber999 for technical guidance
```

### For Businesses — Incident Response

Businesses facing a significant cybersecurity incident should:

| Step | Action |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Activate internal incident response plan (or create one now — before an incident) |
| 2 | Isolate affected systems from the network |
| 3 | Contact Cyber999 for technical guidance and coordination |
| 4 | Report to PDRM if criminal activity is suspected |
| 5 | If personal data of customers is involved: notify Personal Data Protection Commissioner within prescribed period |
| 6 | Preserve all logs and evidence for forensic analysis |
| 7 | Notify affected individuals as required under PDPA |

Cross-reference: **Section 8.4.2 — Data Privacy and PDPA** for business obligations following a data breach.

## Practical Habits for Cyber Resilience

Individuals and businesses that avoid most cyber incidents tend to:

* Use a password manager — the single highest-impact security improvement most people can make
* Enable 2FA on all important accounts — especially email and banking
* Never install APK files from unofficial sources — the primary malware vector in Malaysia
* Check haveibeenpwned.com periodically for their email addresses
* Maintain offline or cloud backups of important data — ransomware protection
* Update all devices and applications promptly when updates are available

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Instagram account takeover recovered**

A content creator's Instagram account was hijacked via a phishing link disguised as a brand collaboration enquiry. She reported to Cyber999 and Instagram simultaneously. Cyber999 provided step-by-step guidance on the account recovery process and evidence documentation. The account was recovered within 72 hours with Instagram's support.

**Scenario 2: Ransomware on SME server**

A small accounting firm found all their files encrypted by ransomware on a Monday morning. They called Cyber999 before doing anything else. The team advised them not to pay the ransom, helped identify the ransomware variant, and found a free decryption tool available for that specific variant. 80% of files were recovered. The remaining 20% were restored from a backup made two weeks earlier — reinforcing why regular backups matter.

**Scenario 3: Data breach discovered via haveibeenpwned**

An HR manager ran the company's domain through haveibeenpwned.com and found 12 employee email addresses in a major service provider breach. She immediately required all affected employees to change passwords and enable 2FA. No further incidents occurred. The check took 5 minutes and cost nothing.

## FAQ

**Q: Is Cyber999 only for major incidents or for individuals too?**
Cyber999 serves everyone — individuals, SMEs, large organisations, and government agencies. A hacked personal Instagram account is as valid a report as a corporate data breach. Volume of cases means responses may vary, but all reports are recorded and contribute to national threat intelligence.

**Q: Can Cyber999 recover my hacked account for me?**
No — Cyber999 provides guidance and coordinates with platforms where possible, but cannot directly access or recover your accounts. Account recovery is done through the platform's own process, with Cyber999 advising you on the steps and documentation needed.

**Q: Should I pay ransomware demands?**
Payment is not recommended — it funds criminal operations, does not guarantee decryption, and may mark you as a target for repeat attacks. Contact Cyber999 first — free decryption tools exist for many ransomware variants. If the encrypted data is critical to business survival, seek professional cybersecurity advice before deciding.

**Q: Is a Malaysian VPN safe to use?**
VPN quality varies significantly. A reputable VPN with a no-logs policy improves privacy on public networks. Malaysian-operated VPNs are subject to Malaysian law — including potential data disclosure requests. For sensitive use, research the VPN provider's jurisdiction, privacy policy, and independent audits before trusting it.

**Q: How do I know if an app is safe to install?**
Install only from official stores (App Store, Google Play Store). Read reviews and check the developer's identity. Be cautious of apps requesting excessive permissions. Never install APK files from WhatsApp, Telegram, or unofficial websites — this is the primary malware vector in Malaysia.
