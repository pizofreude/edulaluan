## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is personal data?**

Personal data is information that can identify you — your name, IC number, phone number, email address, location, medical history, financial records.

When you sign up for an app, buy something online, or visit a clinic — you share personal data.

The **Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA)** is a law that tells private companies what they can and cannot do with your data.

Your basic rights:

* You can ask to see what data a company holds about you
* You can ask them to correct wrong information
* You can ask them to stop using your data for marketing

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

The **Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA)** is Malaysia's primary legislation governing the collection, use, storage, disclosure, and disposal of personal data by **commercial organisations**.

It establishes seven data protection principles that organisations must follow, and gives individuals (data subjects) specific rights over their personal data.

The PDPA is enforced by the **Personal Data Protection Commissioner** under the Ministry of Digital.

### What the PDPA Is Not

The PDPA is not a comprehensive privacy law in the European GDPR sense. Its scope is deliberately limited:

* It applies only to **commercial transactions** — data processed in the context of business
* It **does not apply to the federal or state government** — a significant and frequently criticised gap
* It does not cover personal data processed for domestic (non-commercial) purposes
* It does not apply to credit reference agencies processing data under other laws

## Part 1: What the PDPA Covers and Who It Applies To

### Who Must Comply

| Entity | Covered by PDPA? |
| --- | --- |
| Private companies and businesses | Yes — if processing personal data in commercial transactions |
| Sole proprietors and partnerships | Yes — in commercial contexts |
| Non-profit organisations | Partially — depends on whether commercial transactions are involved |
| Federal government agencies | No — explicitly exempted |
| State government agencies | No — explicitly exempted |
| Political parties | No — exempted |
| Individuals processing data for personal/domestic use | No |

### What Counts as Personal Data Under PDPA

| Category | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Basic identity | Name, IC number, passport number |
| Contact information | Phone number, email address, home address |
| Financial data | Bank account details, credit card numbers, income |
| Health data | Medical records, prescriptions, health screening results |
| Biometric data | Fingerprints, facial recognition data |
| Location data | GPS coordinates, home/work address |
| Online identifiers | IP address, cookies, device IDs |
| Employment data | Job title, salary, performance records |

### Sensitive Personal Data

The PDPA identifies a subset of **sensitive personal data** that requires explicit consent to process:

* Physical or mental health conditions
* Political opinions
* Religious beliefs
* Offences and criminal proceedings
* Other categories as prescribed by the Minister

## Part 2: The Seven Data Protection Principles

Every organisation subject to the PDPA must comply with these seven principles:

| Principle | What It Requires |
| --- | --- |
| 1. General principle | Personal data must not be processed unless the data subject has consented, or one of the specified conditions is met |
| 2. Notice and choice principle | Data subjects must be notified of what data is collected, why, and who it may be disclosed to — before collection |
| 3. Disclosure principle | Data must not be disclosed for purposes different from why it was collected without consent |
| 4. Security principle | Organisations must take practical steps to protect personal data from loss, misuse, and unauthorised access |
| 5. Retention principle | Data must not be kept longer than necessary for the purpose it was collected |
| 6. Data integrity principle | Data must be accurate, complete, and up-to-date |
| 7. Access principle | Data subjects have the right to access their personal data and to correct inaccurate data |

### The Privacy Notice

Any organisation collecting your personal data must provide a **privacy notice** — a document explaining:

* What data is being collected
* Why it is being collected
* How long it will be kept
* Who it may be shared with
* Your rights as a data subject

If you have never received a privacy notice from a company that holds your data — that may be a PDPA violation.

## Part 3: Your Rights as a Data Subject

### Right of Access

You can request a copy of personal data an organisation holds about you.

| Step | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Submit written request to the organisation | Usually via their privacy officer or DPO (Data Protection Officer) |
| Organisation must respond within 21 days | Subject to allowable extensions |
| Fee may be charged | A reasonable fee may apply |

### Right of Correction

You can request correction of inaccurate or incomplete personal data.

| Step | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Submit correction request in writing | Include evidence of the correct information |
| Organisation must correct or explain why it won't | Within 21 days |
| If refused | You may request a statement of the disagreement be attached to your record |

### Right to Withdraw Consent

You can withdraw consent to the processing of your personal data at any time.

**Important limitation:** Withdrawal of consent does not affect processing that occurred before withdrawal, and does not apply where processing is required by law.

### Right to Prevent Processing for Direct Marketing

You can request that an organisation stop using your data for direct marketing purposes. This is sometimes called an **opt-out** right.

Organisations must comply with this request — they cannot make it a condition of service that you accept marketing.

### Practical Exercise of Rights

To exercise any of these rights:

1. Identify the organisation's **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** or privacy contact — usually in their privacy notice or website footer
2. Submit a written request (email is acceptable)
3. Reference the specific right you are exercising and the PDPA 2010
4. Allow 21 days for response
5. If unsatisfied → file a complaint with the PDPA Commissioner

## Part 4: Obligations of Organisations Handling Your Data

### Key Obligations

| Obligation | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Obtain consent before collecting | Or meet one of the PDPA's specified lawful bases |
| Provide privacy notice | Before or at the time of collection |
| Appoint a Data Protection Officer | Recommended; mandatory for certain sectors |
| Implement security measures | Physical and technical safeguards against breach |
| Retain data only as long as necessary | Delete or anonymise after the stated retention period |
| Not transfer data overseas | Unless the destination country has adequate protection or consent is given |
| Respond to data subject requests | Within 21 days |

### Cross-Border Data Transfers

The PDPA restricts transferring personal data to countries outside Malaysia unless:

* The destination has been approved by the Minister as having adequate data protection
* The data subject has consented
* The transfer is necessary for a contract with the data subject

**Practical implication:** When a Malaysian company uses overseas cloud services, overseas payment processors, or international partners, PDPA's cross-border provisions apply — though enforcement has been limited in practice.

## Part 5: Data Breaches — What Organisations Must Do

### Current State: No Mandatory Breach Notification (As of 2025)

Unlike the GDPR and some other jurisdictions, the PDPA 2010 **does not currently mandate that organisations notify data subjects or the Commissioner of data breaches** within a specified timeframe.

This is a widely criticised gap — organisations have experienced data breaches affecting millions of Malaysians without a legal obligation to notify affected individuals promptly.

**PDPA amendments** have been under discussion for several years to introduce mandatory breach notification. As of 2025, confirm current amendment status at **pdp.gov.my**.

### What Organisations Should Do (Best Practice)

Even without a mandatory requirement, best practice following a data breach includes:

```
1. Contain the breach — stop ongoing unauthorised access
2. Assess the scope — what data was affected, how many individuals
3. Notify affected individuals promptly — especially if financial or sensitive data involved
4. Notify the PDPA Commissioner
5. Document the incident and response
6. Review security measures to prevent recurrence
```

### What You Should Do If Your Data Is Breached

If you are notified of a data breach affecting your data:

1. Change passwords for the affected service and any reused passwords
2. Enable 2FA on the account
3. Monitor bank accounts and credit activity for suspicious transactions
4. If IC number was exposed: alert your bank
5. Report to the PDPA Commissioner if you believe the organisation handled your data negligently

## The Government Exemption — An Honest Assessment

The PDPA explicitly exempts the **federal and state governments** from its provisions.

This means:

* Government agencies that collect your data — LHDN, JPN, EPF, KKM, MySejahtera — are **not legally bound by the PDPA's data protection principles**
* There is no statutory right of access or correction for government-held personal data under the PDPA
* Data collected by government agencies can be retained and used under broader discretionary powers

**Why this matters:**

Government agencies hold enormous amounts of personal data — biometrics (JPN), health records (KKM), financial records (LHDN), employment data (EPF/PERKESO), and the aggregated data from MySejahtera (which became a significant dataset during the COVID-19 pandemic).

The absence of PDPA coverage for government data has been raised by legal scholars, civil society organisations, and the Bar Council as a significant gap in Malaysia's data protection framework.

**Current state of reform:** Discussions around extending PDPA coverage to government agencies have been ongoing. As of early 2025, no amendment has been enacted to close this gap. Monitor **pdp.gov.my** and parliamentary proceedings for updates.

This does not mean government agencies operate without any data governance — but the governance framework is internal and administrative rather than legally enforceable by individuals.

## Part 6: How to Make a Complaint Under PDPA

### When to Complain

File a PDPA complaint when:

* An organisation collected your data without consent or a valid lawful basis
* You did not receive a privacy notice
* An organisation refused a valid access or correction request
* Your data was disclosed to a third party without consent
* An organisation is using your data for direct marketing after you opted out
* You have evidence of a data security breach caused by negligence

### How to File

| Channel | Contact | What to Provide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Online complaint | pdp.gov.my | Organisation details, incident description, evidence |
| Email | aduan@pdp.gov.my | Written complaint with supporting documents |
| Walk-in | PDPA Commissioner office (Putrajaya) | Full complaint documentation |
| Phone enquiry | 03-8911 5000 | For guidance before formal complaint |

### What the Commissioner Can Do

| Power | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Investigate complaints | Request information from organisations |
| Issue enforcement notices | Order organisations to comply with PDPA |
| Impose fines | Penalties for PDPA violations |
| Prosecute | Refer serious cases for criminal prosecution |
| Publish guidance | Issue codes of practice and advisory |

### Penalties for PDPA Violations

| Offence | Maximum Penalty |
| --- | --- |
| Failure to comply with data protection principles | RM 500,000 fine or 3 years imprisonment or both |
| Failure to comply with enforcement notice | RM 1,000,000 fine or 3 years imprisonment or both |
| Transferring data overseas unlawfully | RM 300,000 fine or 2 years imprisonment or both |

## Practical Habits for Personal Data Protection

Individuals who manage their data privacy effectively tend to:

* Read privacy notices before clicking "I agree" — at minimum, look for what data is collected and who it is shared with
* Use a separate email address for sign-ups and newsletters — reducing the impact of marketing data exposure
* Regularly review app permissions on their phone — revoke unnecessary access to location, contacts, microphone
* Exercise the right to opt out of marketing promptly — do not ignore it as spam
* Check haveibeenpwned.com periodically for their email addresses
* Know they can request access to their data from any PDPA-covered organisation

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Data sold to third-party marketers**

A consumer signed up for a loyalty card programme at a retail chain. Within weeks she received calls from insurance agents she had never contacted — having been sold her data by the retailer to a third-party marketing partner. She filed a PDPA complaint and requested the retailer produce the consent record for the disclosure. Unable to demonstrate consent, the retailer was investigated and issued an enforcement notice.

**Scenario 2: Medical data shared without consent**

A patient discovered his chronic illness diagnosis had been shared with his employer by a private clinic — without his consent. He filed a PDPA complaint citing the disclosure principle and the sensitive personal data provisions. The clinic was found to have violated the PDPA and required to implement corrective measures.

**Scenario 3: Government data collection — PDPA does not apply**

During a national health initiative, personal health data was collected through a government app. A data advocacy group raised questions about data retention and access policies. Because the app was operated by a government agency, the PDPA did not apply — and there was no formal legal mechanism for individuals to request access to or deletion of their data. The group's advocacy highlighted the government exemption gap in public discourse.

## FAQ

**Q: Does the PDPA apply to WhatsApp groups or private chats?**
The PDPA applies to personal data processed in commercial transactions. Personal communications between individuals are generally outside its scope. However, if a business is using WhatsApp to collect and process customer data commercially, PDPA provisions may apply to that business.

**Q: Can I ask a company to delete my data?**
The PDPA does not include a general "right to erasure" equivalent to GDPR's Article 17. You can withdraw consent and request cessation of processing for marketing, but a broad right to deletion does not currently exist in Malaysian law. PDPA amendments under discussion may address this.

**Q: Does PDPA apply to foreign companies operating in Malaysia?**
Companies registered or operating in Malaysia and processing personal data of Malaysian individuals are subject to PDPA. The practical challenge is enforcement against companies with no physical presence in Malaysia — which remains difficult.

**Q: What is a DPO (Data Protection Officer)?**
A DPO is a designated person within an organisation responsible for ensuring PDPA compliance. Not all organisations are currently required by law to appoint one — though it is best practice and some sectors require it. The DPO is your main point of contact for exercising data subject rights.

**Q: Is health data collected by private hospitals covered by PDPA?**
Yes — private hospitals and clinics processing patient data in commercial transactions are covered by the PDPA. Health data is classified as sensitive personal data requiring explicit consent. Government hospitals and clinics are not covered by the PDPA government exemption.
