## If You Just Got Scammed

**Act in this order — every minute matters:**

```
1. Call your bank immediately to freeze or recall the transfer
2. Call NSRC hotline: 997
3. File a police report at the nearest police station or via polis.gov.my
4. Report at ccid.rmp.gov.my (PDRM Commercial Crime)
5. Report the scammer's account at Semak Mule (semakmule.rmp.gov.my) to protect others
```

Do not be embarrassed. Scams are sophisticated and affect all ages and income levels.

## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**How do scams work?**

A scammer tries to trick you into giving them your money or personal information.

They may pretend to be:
* A bank officer calling about a "suspicious transaction"
* A romantic partner you met online
* An employer offering a job
* A government officer saying you owe a fine

They create urgency — "act now or your account will be frozen."

**The golden rule:** If someone you don't know contacts you asking for money or personal details — stop, verify, and never transfer.

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Malaysia loses billions of ringgit to scams annually. The PDRM Commercial Crime Investigation Department (CCID) recorded over 70,000 scam cases in 2023 alone — and this likely undercounts unreported cases.

Scams in Malaysia are not random — they are industrialised operations, often run from overseas, with sophisticated scripts, fake documents, and social engineering tactics refined over years.

Understanding the most common scam types, knowing the tools to verify before transacting, and knowing exactly what to do if targeted are the three pillars of scam protection this page covers.

## Part 1: Common Scam Types in Malaysia

### Macau Scam (Penipuan Macau)

Malaysia's most financially damaging scam category.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | Caller impersonates a police officer, MACC officer, Bank Negara, or court official; claims victim is under investigation for money laundering or drug trafficking |
| Urgency tactic | Victim is told to keep quiet, not inform family, and transfer funds to a "safe account" |
| Target | All ages — but older Malaysians are disproportionately targeted |
| Average loss | Tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of ringgit |
| Red flag | Any call from an "authority" asking you to transfer money to protect yourself |

**No legitimate government agency will ever ask you to transfer money to a "safe account."**

### Love Scam (Penipuan Cinta)

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | Scammer builds a romantic relationship online over weeks or months, then asks for money (medical emergency, travel, investment opportunity) |
| Platform | Dating apps, Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp |
| Target | Singles of all ages seeking companionship — increasingly targeting divorced and widowed individuals |
| Red flag | Never meeting in person; always has reasons to postpone video calls; asks for money |

### Investment Scam (Penipuan Pelaburan)

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | Promises high, guaranteed returns with minimal risk — often via WhatsApp investment groups, "trading platforms," or celebrity-endorsed schemes |
| Variants | Fake forex, fake crypto, fake stock trading, Ponzi schemes, MLM hybrids |
| Red flag | Guaranteed returns, pressure to recruit others, unlicensed operator (check SC and BNM) |
| Verification | Check Securities Commission investor alert list: **sc.com.my/investor-alert** |

### Job Scam (Penipuan Kerja)

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | Fake job offer — usually work-from-home — that requires upfront payment for "training materials," "registration," or "equipment deposits" |
| Advanced variant | Victim is trafficked overseas to work in scam call centres — report to PDRM and SUHAKAM |
| Red flag | Job that requires you to pay first; vague job scope; contacted via WhatsApp not official channels |

### E-Commerce Scam

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | Seller takes payment and does not deliver; or buyer claims non-receipt to reverse payment fraudulently |
| Platform | Facebook Marketplace, Carousell, informal WhatsApp selling groups |
| Red flag | Price too good to be true; seller insists on bank transfer not platform escrow; no SSM registration |
| Verification | Check SSM registration of business sellers; use platform escrow payments where available |

### Parcel / Delivery Scam

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | SMS or call claiming a parcel is held due to unpaid customs duty; link leads to fake payment page harvesting card details |
| Red flag | Unexpected notification; link does not match official courier domain; requests card details upfront |

### Phishing and Account Takeover

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| How it works | Fake bank, government, or platform login page harvests credentials; or OTP is tricked out of victim via social engineering |
| Red flag | Urgent SMS with link to verify account; caller asking for TAC or OTP "for verification" |
| Critical rule | **No bank, government agency, or legitimate platform will ever ask for your OTP or TAC via phone or message** |

## Part 2: Semak Mule — How to Use It

### What Is Semak Mule?

**Semak Mule** (semakmule.rmp.gov.my) is an online tool operated by the Royal Malaysian Police (PDRM) that allows anyone to check whether a bank account number or phone number has been reported as involved in scam activity.

"Mule" refers to money mule accounts — bank accounts used to receive and launder scam proceeds. These accounts are often opened by people who sell or lend their account details to scammers.

### How to Use Semak Mule

```
1. Go to semakmule.rmp.gov.my
2. Enter the bank account number OR phone number you want to check
3. Select the category (bank account / phone)
4. View the result — the system shows if the number has been reported
```

### What the Results Mean

| Result | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| "Tiada rekod" (No record) | No reports found — but this does not guarantee the account is safe |
| Reports found | The account or number has been reported in scam cases — do not transact |

**Important:** A clean Semak Mule result is not a guarantee of legitimacy — scam accounts are added to the database only after being reported. New scam accounts will not yet appear.

### When to Use Semak Mule

* Before transferring money to an unknown seller or service provider
* Before investing with an individual who sends you their bank account
* When someone you met online asks you to transfer money
* When verifying any transaction initiated by an unsolicited contact

## Part 3: Penipu.my — Community Scammer Database

### What Is Penipu.my?

**Penipu.my** is a community-driven database where Malaysians can report and look up known scammers — including phone numbers, bank accounts, names, and scam descriptions.

Unlike Semak Mule (PDRM-operated), Penipu.my is community-sourced — entries are submitted by users who have been targeted or scammed.

### How to Use It

```
1. Go to penipu.my
2. Search by phone number, bank account number, or name
3. Read submitted reports — note dates and scam type described
4. Submit your own report if you have been targeted
```

### Semak Mule vs Penipu.my

| Feature | Semak Mule | Penipu.my |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Operated by | PDRM (official) | Community (unofficial) |
| Data source | Official police reports | Community submissions |
| Coverage | Bank accounts and phone numbers | Phone numbers, bank accounts, names, ICs |
| Verification | Linked to actual police cases | User-submitted — may include errors |
| Best for | Pre-transaction verification | Research and community awareness |

**Use both tools together** — Semak Mule for official verification, Penipu.my for broader community intelligence.

## Part 4: Protecting Yourself Proactively

### The Core Habits

| Habit | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Never share OTP, TAC, or PIN with anyone — including callers claiming to be from your bank | Banks and government agencies never ask for these |
| Verify before transferring — check Semak Mule and Penipu.my | Most scam losses happen in the first transfer |
| Call the institution directly using official numbers | Do not call back numbers provided in suspicious messages — look up the official number independently |
| Use platform escrow for online purchases | Marketplace escrow protects both parties — never pay direct bank transfer to strangers |
| Check SC and BNM investor alert lists before investing | Unlicensed investment operators are common — sc.com.my/investor-alert and bnm.gov.my/financial-consumer-alert |
| Enable transaction notifications on all bank accounts | Real-time alerts allow faster detection of unauthorised transactions |
| Use strong, unique passwords and enable 2FA | Account takeovers are often stepping stones to financial fraud |

### The Pause Test

When something feels urgent and involves money or personal details:

```
Stop → Pause for 24 hours before acting
      → Call a trusted family member or friend
      → Independently verify using official channels
      → If still unsure: do not proceed
```

Scammers manufacture urgency deliberately — "your account will be frozen in 2 hours" — because they know that pausing breaks the scam.

## Part 5: What to Do If You Have Been Scammed

### Immediate Actions (First 60 Minutes Matter Most)

| Step | Action | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Call your bank immediately | Banks can attempt to recall transfers if acted on quickly |
| 2 | Call NSRC: **997** | National Scam Response Centre can coordinate bank freezing |
| 3 | Preserve all evidence | Screenshots, transaction records, messages, call logs |
| 4 | Do not transfer more money | Scammers often follow up claiming to "help recover" lost funds — a secondary scam |

### Filing Reports

| Channel | How | What It Does |
| --- | --- | --- |
| PDRM CCID | ccid.rmp.gov.my or 03-2610 1559 | Official police report — required for insurance and legal purposes |
| NSRC (997) | Call 997 | Coordinates between banks and police for rapid response |
| Semak Mule | semakmule.rmp.gov.my | Report the scammer's account to warn others |
| Penipu.my | penipu.my | Community report to warn other Malaysians |
| Bank's fraud line | Your bank's official number | Request transaction reversal or account freeze |
| Cyber999 | cyber999.my or 1-300-88-2999 | For cybercrime elements — phishing, account takeover |

### Reporting to Your Bank

When calling your bank:

* State clearly: "I am a scam victim and need to recall a transfer urgently"
* Provide: transaction reference number, amount, date and time, recipient account number
* Ask for: a case number and the bank's fraud investigation contact

**Most banks have a 24-hour fraud line** — use it, not the general customer service number.

### Managing the Aftermath

| Action | Why |
| --- | --- |
| File a police report even if recovery seems unlikely | Required for insurance claims; contributes to PDRM statistics that drive enforcement |
| Inform your employer if workplace accounts were involved | Employer may need to secure their systems |
| Change passwords on all accounts accessed from the same device | Device may be compromised |
| Monitor your CCRIS report | Scammers who obtained IC details may attempt loan applications |
| Seek emotional support if needed | Scam victims often experience shame and trauma — this is normal and help is available |

## Practical Habits for Scam Prevention

Malaysians who avoid scams consistently tend to:

* Never act on unsolicited contact — if a call, message, or email initiates contact, verify independently before doing anything
* Keep a mental model of what banks and government agencies will and will not ask for (never OTP, TAC, or transfers)
* Check Semak Mule before any transfer to an unknown account — takes 30 seconds
* Enable transaction alerts on all bank accounts — mobile banking apps make this easy
* Discuss scam tactics with elderly family members — the Macau scam disproportionately targets older Malaysians and awareness within families is a key prevention layer

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Macau scam averted by pause**

A 58-year-old received a call from someone claiming to be a "Bank Negara officer" saying her account was flagged for money laundering. She was told to transfer RM 40,000 to a "safe account." She felt panicked but remembered reading that BNM never asks for transfers. She hung up, called her daughter, and called BNM's official number — confirming it was a scam. Her savings were intact.

**Scenario 2: Semak Mule check prevented e-commerce loss**

A buyer was about to transfer RM 1,200 for a second-hand laptop from a Facebook Marketplace seller. He checked the seller's bank account on Semak Mule — it had multiple scam reports. He did not transfer and reported the account. The listing was taken down within days.

**Scenario 3: Fast bank call recovered funds**

A victim of an investment scam transferred RM 15,000 to a fake trading platform. Within 30 minutes of realising the scam, she called her bank's fraud line. The bank placed a hold on the receiving account — which was still active — and initiated a recall. RM 11,000 was successfully recovered. The 30-minute window made the difference.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I recover money I transferred to a scammer?**
Recovery is possible but not guaranteed — and time-critical. Banks can attempt recalls if the receiving account still holds funds. Acting within the first hour gives the best chance. Contact your bank and NSRC (997) immediately. Complete recovery is rare; partial recovery happens when funds have not yet been withdrawn.

**Q: Should I be embarrassed to report a scam?**
No. Scam victims include doctors, lawyers, engineers, and retired civil servants. These operations are sophisticated and designed to bypass rational thinking by exploiting urgency and authority. Reporting protects others — your report may be the data point that triggers enforcement action.

**Q: Is Penipu.my official?**
No — Penipu.my is community-driven, not operated by PDRM. Entries are user-submitted and may contain errors or outdated information. Use it as supplementary intelligence alongside Semak Mule, not as a definitive official check.

**Q: What if I gave my OTP to a scammer and no money has moved yet?**
Call your bank immediately and report that your OTP was compromised. Request a temporary account freeze while the bank investigates. Change your online banking password immediately from a secure device.

**Q: Can I sue a scammer?**
Theoretically yes — but practically difficult when scammers operate from overseas or use money mule networks. Focus on reporting to PDRM for criminal action and bank recall for financial recovery. Civil suits against overseas scam syndicates are rarely successful.
