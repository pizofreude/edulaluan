## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is misinformation?**

Misinformation is false or inaccurate information — shared by people who may not know it is wrong.

**Disinformation** is false information shared on purpose, to deceive.

Both spread the same way: quickly, through social media, WhatsApp, and word of mouth.

Before you share something:

* Does it make you feel very angry, scared, or excited? That is a sign to pause.
* Can you find the same story on a trusted news site?
* Does the image or video look too perfect or too shocking?

If you are unsure — do not share.

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Misinformation is one of the defining challenges of the digital age. In Malaysia, it spreads rapidly through WhatsApp groups, Facebook, TikTok, and Telegram — often among family members and trusted community networks, which amplifies credibility.

Understanding how misinformation works, how to verify it, and how to recognise AI-generated fakes are practical skills every Malaysian internet user needs. This page is a working toolkit — not just awareness.

## Part 1: Types of Misinformation and How They Spread

### The Misinformation Spectrum

| Type | Definition | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Misinformation | False content shared without intent to deceive | Sharing an old photo believing it is recent |
| Disinformation | False content shared intentionally to deceive | Fabricated news article designed to influence opinion |
| Malinformation | True content used to harm | Leaking private messages out of context to damage someone |
| Satire misunderstood | Satirical content taken literally | Satirical headline shared as real news |
| Manipulated content | Genuine content altered or taken out of context | Real video with fabricated subtitles |
| Fabricated content | Entirely false content — fake quotes, fake videos, fake documents | AI-generated voice clip of a public figure |

### Why Misinformation Spreads

Research consistently shows that misinformation spreads faster than corrections because:

* **Emotional resonance:** False content tends to be more emotionally provocative — generating fear, outrage, or excitement — which drives sharing
* **Novelty:** Surprising or shocking content gets more engagement than accurate but unremarkable information
* **Social trust:** Content shared by family and friends carries assumed credibility
* **Confirmation bias:** People are more likely to share content that confirms what they already believe
* **Speed:** False content spreads before corrections can catch up

### Malaysian-Specific Misinformation Patterns

Malaysia has documented recurring misinformation patterns:

| Pattern | Context |
| --- | --- |
| Health misinformation | False vaccine, medication, and traditional remedy claims — amplified in family WhatsApp groups |
| Political misinformation | Election-related false claims; fabricated quotes attributed to politicians |
| Religious and racial misinformation | Content designed to inflame ethnic or religious tension — historically a serious risk in Malaysia |
| Financial scam misinformation | False investment opportunities; fake government grant announcements |
| Disaster misinformation | During floods and emergencies, false evacuation orders and casualty numbers spread rapidly |
| Historical revisionism | Selective or false historical narratives spread on social media |

## Part 2: How to Verify Claims and Images

### The SIFT Method

A four-step approach to evaluating any piece of content:

```
S — Stop
    Before reacting or sharing, pause. Ask: "Do I know if this is accurate?"
    Strong emotional responses are a signal to slow down, not speed up.

I — Investigate the source
    Who created this? What is their track record?
    Is this a verified account? A known outlet? A first-time poster?

F — Find better coverage
    Search for the same claim on multiple reliable sources.
    If only one source has it — especially a source you have never heard of — be skeptical.

T — Trace claims
    Follow the original source, not just the reshare.
    Does the original article, photo, or video actually say what the reshare claims?
```

### Verifying Images

| Tool | What It Does | Link |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Google Reverse Image Search | Find where an image originally appeared and when | images.google.com |
| TinEye | Reverse image search with date of first appearance | tineye.com |
| InVID / WeVerify | Analyses videos and images for manipulation | invid-project.eu |
| Yandex Images | Often finds more results than Google for certain images | yandex.com/images |

**How to use Google Reverse Image Search:**

1. Right-click on an image → "Search image with Google"
   Or go to images.google.com and drag the image in
2. Check when the image first appeared online
3. Check whether the image is being used in a different context than claimed

**Common pattern in Malaysia:** Old disaster, crime, or protest photos recycled with new captions claiming they are from a recent event. A reverse image search immediately shows the original date and context.

### Verifying Text Claims

| Approach | How |
| --- | --- |
| Search the exact claim | Put the key claim in quotes in a search engine — find the original source |
| Check the date | Is this actually new, or being reshared out of context? |
| Read the full article | Headlines and excerpts are frequently misrepresented |
| Check the URL | Fake news sites often use URLs that resemble legitimate outlets (e.g. "thestarnews.com" vs "thestar.com.my") |
| Look for bylines and authorship | Anonymous or impossible-to-verify authorship is a red flag |

## Part 4: Deepfakes — How to Spot Them

### What Deepfakes Are

Deepfakes are AI-generated or AI-manipulated audio, video, or images designed to make it appear that a real person said or did something they did not.

**Categories:**

| Type | Description | Common Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Face swap video | AI replaces one person's face with another in video | Political manipulation, non-consensual intimate imagery |
| Voice clone | AI replicates a person's voice from a short sample | Fraud, fake authoritative statements |
| Lip sync manipulation | AI alters lip movements to match fabricated audio | Fake political speeches, false statements |
| Image generation | AI creates realistic-looking photos of non-existent events | False news, stock photo fraud |
| Text-to-video | AI generates video of a person from text description | Emerging; increasingly accessible |

### Red Flags for Deepfake Video

| Red Flag | What to Look For |
| --- | --- |
| Facial blurring or flickering | Edges of face, especially ears and hairline |
| Unnatural blinking | Too frequent, too rare, or asymmetric |
| Lip sync errors | Mouth movements slightly out of sync with audio |
| Lighting inconsistencies | Face lighting doesn't match background |
| Unnatural skin texture | Overly smooth or plasticky appearance |
| Eye irregularities | Unusual eye shape, reflection, or movement |
| Audio-visual mismatch | Voice quality differs from background audio |

### Red Flags for AI-Generated Images

| Red Flag | What to Look For |
| --- | --- |
| Hands and fingers | Too many, too few, or unnaturally shaped fingers |
| Teeth and gums | Irregular number, shape, or blending |
| Background details | Distorted text, impossible geometry, repeating patterns |
| Accessories | Glasses that merge into face; jewellery that doesn't connect |
| Hair | Unnatural blending with background or face |
| Text in image | Garbled, misspelled, or distorted |

### Tools for Detecting Deepfakes

| Tool | What It Does | Link |
| --- | --- | --- |
| InVID / WeVerify | Video verification and deepfake analysis | invid-project.eu |
| Microsoft Video Authenticator | Analyses videos for manipulation probability | (via Microsoft) |
| Sensity AI | Deepfake detection (enterprise-focused) | sensity.ai |
| Deepware Scanner | Consumer-facing deepfake detection app | deepware.ai |

**Important caveat:** Deepfake detection tools are in an arms race with deepfake generation tools. No detection tool is 100% accurate. Use these as supplementary checks, not definitive verdicts. When in doubt, verify through other means — multiple sources, official confirmation, direct contact.

### Voice Clone Red Flags

| Red Flag | What to Notice |
| --- | --- |
| Slightly robotic or flat affect | Natural speech has more variation |
| Unusual pauses or pacing | AI voice generation can produce unnatural rhythm |
| Audio quality inconsistency | Voice sounds different from background audio |
| Request for urgent financial action | High-stakes request via audio/video is a scam signal |

**Practical rule:** If you receive an audio or video message from a known person making an unusual or urgent financial request — verify by calling them directly on a number you have independently stored. Do not use a number provided in the suspicious message.

## Part 5: What to Do When You Encounter Misinformation

### If You Received Misinformation

```
1. Do not share it
2. Verify using SIFT and fact-checking resources
3. If debunked, consider gently correcting in the same thread
4. If it is harmful (health, racial, electoral), report to the platform and relevant authority
```

### If You Already Shared It

```
1. Delete the post or message if possible
2. Post a correction in the same thread / group
3. Do not over-dramatise the correction — a clear, factual correction is enough
4. Learn from it — what signal made you share without verifying?
```

### Correcting Others — Gently Works Better

Research on misinformation correction shows that:

* Aggressive corrections backfire — people dig in when publicly shamed
* Gentle, private corrections work better in close networks (WhatsApp family groups)
* Providing the correct information rather than just saying "this is false" is more effective
* Asking questions ("I saw this earlier — do you know if it's accurate?") is less threatening than assertions

### Reporting Misinformation

| Harmful Misinformation Type | Where to Report |
| --- | --- |
| Health misinformation | KKM (moh.gov.my) |
| Political or electoral misinformation | SPR (Elections Commission) / MCMC |
| Price-related false claims | KPDNHEP (e-aduan.kpdn.gov.my) |
| Racial or religious incitement | PDRM / MCMC (aduan.skmm.gov.my) |
| Platform content (Facebook, TikTok, etc.) | Platform reporting tools |
| Government impersonation | MCMC and the relevant agency |

## Practical Habits for an Accurate Information Diet

People who consistently navigate misinformation well tend to:

* Pause before sharing content that triggers strong emotion — the emotional response is the signal
* Verify images with Google Reverse Image Search before forwarding in groups
* Diversify their news sources — no single outlet has a monopoly on accuracy
* Follow at least one Malaysian fact-checker (Sebenarnya.my, AFP Fact Check MY, Mafindo)
* Distinguish between opinion and factual reporting — many pieces that look like news are commentary
* Accept being wrong graciously — correcting a past share promptly is better than defending it

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Flood emergency false information**

During a major flood in Johor, a voice note circulated on WhatsApp claiming a specific dam had burst and urging immediate evacuation of named neighbourhoods. The information was false. However, it caused hundreds of families to flee their homes unnecessarily — some injuring themselves in the rush. Official channels (JKM, state disaster management) had not issued any such warning. Checking the official emergency management channel before acting on forwarded voice notes would have prevented this.

**Scenario 2: Health misinformation in family group**

A widely shared image in BM claimed that a particular common medication caused kidney failure when mixed with a popular drink. The claim was false — and was fact-checked and debunked on Sebenarnya.my. A family member who received the image searched it on Sebenarnya.my, found the debunking, and shared the correction link in the family group alongside a gentle note. The original misinformation was removed from the group.

**Scenario 3: Deepfake political video**

During an election campaign, a video circulated appearing to show a prominent politician making inflammatory statements. Several markers of deepfake manipulation were present — slight lip sync errors, unnatural skin texture at the face border. An independent media outlet used InVID to analyse the video and published a fact-check noting the manipulation indicators. The story was corrected within hours — but not before the video had been shared hundreds of thousands of times. Knowing the red flags would have allowed earlier scepticism.

## FAQ

**Q: Is sharing misinformation illegal in Malaysia?**
Sharing content that is false and causes harm can potentially attract action under Section 233 of the Communications and Multimedia Act 1998 (offensive, false communications) or other provisions. In practice, prosecution targets systematic disinformation campaigns more than accidental individual shares. The ethical and social obligation to verify before sharing is clear regardless of legal risk.

**Q: Can I be held liable for defamation if I share false content about someone?**
Yes — republication of defamatory content is actionable under the Defamation Act 1957, even if you did not originate it. "I was just sharing what someone else said" is not a complete defence.

**Q: Are deepfakes illegal in Malaysia?**
Creating and sharing deepfakes for malicious purposes — particularly non-consensual intimate imagery — can be actionable under Section 233 CMA and potentially the Penal Code. There is no standalone deepfake law in Malaysia as of 2025. Cross-reference: **Section 8.4.3 — Digital Rights and Online Ethics**

**Q: How do I tell satire from misinformation?**
Legitimate satire is typically clearly labelled, published on known satire outlets, and exaggerated to the point of obvious absurdity. Satire that circulates without its source context — just a screenshot of the headline — is frequently mistaken for real news. Searching for the source publication is the clearest check.

**Q: Is Sebenarnya.my unbiased?**
Sebenarnya.my is operated by MCMC — a government body. Its fact-checking is useful but should be read with awareness of its operator, particularly for politically sensitive claims. Using multiple fact-checking sources including independent ones (AFP, Mafindo) gives a more complete picture.
