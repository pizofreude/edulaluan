## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What does MCMC do?**

MCMC (Suruhanjaya Komunikasi dan Multimedia Malaysia — Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission) is the government body that:

* Oversees phone companies, internet providers, and broadcasters
* Handles complaints about bad telco service
* Can order the removal of illegal or harmful online content
* Enforces rules about what can be published and communicated online

If your Unifi or Maxis service is poor and the company is not helping, MCMC is where you escalate.

If you encounter child abuse material, terrorism content, or scam websites online, MCMC can act on reports.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

MCMC was established under the **Communications and Multimedia Act 1998 (CMA)** as the regulator for Malaysia's communications and multimedia industries. Its mandate covers:

* Telecommunications — mobile networks, fixed lines, internet service providers
* Broadcasting — radio, television, streaming services
* Online content — websites, social media, digital platforms
* Postal services — including courier regulation

For everyday Malaysians, MCMC is most relevant as:

* The escalation point for unresolved telco and internet service complaints
* The body that can order illegal online content removed
* The regulator whose enforcement powers affect online expression

## Part 2: Your Rights as an Online User

### Freedom of Expression and Its Limits in Malaysia

Malaysia does not have a constitutional guarantee of absolute free speech. The Federal Constitution's Article 10 guarantees freedom of speech — but allows Parliament to restrict it in the interest of security, public order, morality, and other grounds.

Online, the key legal frameworks that shape what you can say are:

| Law | What It Covers | Enforcement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Communications and Multimedia Act 1998 (CMA) | Offensive, indecent, false, menacing online communications | MCMC / PDRM |
| Sedition Act 1948 | Content deemed seditious | PDRM / AG Chambers |
| Defamation Act 1957 | Civil and criminal defamation | Civil court / PDRM |
| Official Secrets Act 1972 | Unauthorised disclosure of official information | PDRM |
| Personal Data Protection Act 2010 | Misuse of personal data | PDPA Commissioner |

### Section 233, CMA 1998 — The Key Provision

Section 233 of the CMA is the provision most commonly applied to online expression. It makes it an offence to transmit content that is **obscene, indecent, false, menacing, or offensive** with the intent to annoy, abuse, threaten, or harass.

Conviction carries a fine of up to **RM 50,000** or imprisonment of up to **1 year**, or both.

**The honest picture:** Section 233 has been used both to prosecute genuinely harmful content (harassment, threats) and to act against criticism, satire, and political commentary. This dual application is contested in Malaysian civil society and legal discourse.

**What this means for you:** Online communication in Malaysia — including social media posts, WhatsApp messages, and comments — can carry legal consequences. This does not mean self-censorship is required, but awareness of the legal framework is important.

### Website Blocking

MCMC can order ISPs to block websites under the CMA. In practice, blocked sites include:

* Sites hosting CSAM (child sexual abuse material)
* Gambling sites (not licensed in Malaysia)
* Some piracy sites
* Sites flagged for spreading false information

Website blocking orders are made without prior notice to the site owner in most cases. There is no formal pre-blocking appeal process for most categories.

### Right to Request Content Removal

If your personal content — photos, videos, personal information — is published online without your consent, you can:

* Request removal directly from the platform (most platforms have a reporting mechanism)
* Report to MCMC for platforms operating in Malaysia
* File a police report if the content constitutes a criminal offence (e.g. non-consensual intimate images — NCII)

Non-consensual intimate image sharing is a criminal offence under Section 233 CMA and potentially under the Penal Code.

## Part 3: Filing Telco and Internet Complaints

### When to Escalate to MCMC

Escalate to MCMC when:

* You have complained to your service provider and the issue is unresolved after a reasonable period (typically 2–4 weeks)
* The provider has failed to respond within their stated resolution timeframe
* You believe the provider is in breach of their licence conditions

### How to File a Complaint

| Channel | How | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| eAduan MCMC | aduan.skmm.gov.my | Detailed complaints with documentation |
| MCMC Hotline | 1-800-18-0300 | Quick enquiries and urgent issues |
| Walk-in | MCMC offices (KL and state offices) | Complex cases |

### Complaint Process

```
Step 1: File complaint with your service provider first
         → Use their dedicated complaints unit (not general customer service)
         → Reference number should be provided

Step 2: If unresolved within provider's stated period
         → Escalate to MCMC at aduan.skmm.gov.my
         → Include: provider reference number, dates, issue description, evidence

Step 3: MCMC contacts provider on your behalf
         → Most providers resolve complaints promptly once MCMC is involved

Step 4: If still unresolved
         → MCMC may initiate formal investigation and enforcement
```

### Common Telco Complaints MCMC Handles

| Complaint Type | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Service quality | Persistent poor signal, slow broadband, frequent outages |
| Billing disputes | Unauthorised charges, incorrect billing |
| Contract disputes | Early termination fees, contract term disagreements |
| Number porting | Delays or failures in mobile number portability |
| Unsolicited communications | Spam SMS, unsolicited marketing calls |
| Service termination | Unlawful or premature service termination |

Cross-reference: **Section 9.8.1 — Telco Termination and Complaints** for the full escalation flow.

## Part 4: Reporting Illegal or Harmful Online Content

### What MCMC Can Act On

| Content Type | MCMC Action |
| --- | --- |
| Child sexual abuse material (CSAM) | Immediate action — block and refer to PDRM |
| Terrorism and extremism content | Coordinate with PDRM and relevant agencies |
| Scam and fraud websites | Block on request; coordinate with PDRM CCID |
| Non-consensual intimate images | Can order removal; refer to PDRM |
| False information (selected cases) | Can act under CMA provisions |
| Defamatory content | Civil matter — MCMC has limited direct role |

### How to Report

| Report Type | Channel | Link |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Illegal online content | MCMC Content Complaints | aduan.skmm.gov.my |
| Cybercrime (hacking, phishing) | CyberSecurity Malaysia | cyber999.my |
| Scam websites | PDRM CCID | ccid.rmp.gov.my |
| CSAM | MCMC / PDRM | aduan.skmm.gov.my and nearest police station |

### Social Media Content

MCMC works with social media platforms (Meta, TikTok, X/Twitter, YouTube) under agreed cooperation frameworks. However:

* Platforms may not remove content simply because MCMC requests it — they apply their own community standards
* For content that violates platform rules, report directly to the platform first
* For content that violates Malaysian law, report to MCMC and PDRM in parallel

## Part 5: MCMC's Enforcement Powers and Limitations

### What MCMC Can Do

| Power | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Issue directives to licensees | Order telcos and ISPs to comply with regulatory requirements |
| Block websites | Order ISPs to block specific URLs under CMA |
| Compound and prosecute | Fine and prosecute individuals and companies for CMA violations |
| Suspend or revoke licences | For serious or repeated licence breaches |
| Cooperate with PDRM | Refer criminal matters for police investigation |

### What MCMC Cannot Do

| Limitation | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Cannot order content removal from non-Malaysian platforms unilaterally | Requires platform cooperation or court order |
| Cannot physically arrest | Arrests are made by PDRM based on MCMC referrals or PDRM's own investigation |
| Cannot compensate consumers directly | Consumer compensation goes through the provider or civil court |
| Cannot adjudicate defamation | Civil defamation is a court matter |

### The Honest Assessment: Enforcement and Free Expression

MCMC's powers under the CMA give it — and by extension, law enforcement acting on CMA referrals — significant ability to act against online content. This has been applied to:

* Genuine harms: harassment campaigns, scam networks, CSAM, terrorist content
* Contested cases: political criticism, satire, commentary on public figures, journalism

Malaysian courts have handled challenges to CMA enforcement with varying outcomes. The Malaysian Bar, civil society organisations, and international press freedom bodies have raised concerns about the law's application to legitimate expression.

This page does not adjudicate these policy debates. What it provides is:

* Awareness that online communication in Malaysia carries legal risk under certain circumstances
* Practical guidance on using MCMC's complaint mechanisms for genuine harms
* Context to understand when and why MCMC acts

For deeper reading on digital rights and the legal framework, see **Section 8.4.3 — Digital Rights and Online Ethics**.

## Practical Habits for Online Users

Malaysians who navigate online rights effectively tend to:

* Know MCMC exists as an escalation point for unresolved telco complaints — not just as an enforcement body
* Report harmful content (scams, CSAM, harassment) through the appropriate channel rather than only to the platform
* Understand that online communications — including private messages — can carry legal consequences under the CMA
* Keep records of telco complaint reference numbers before escalating to MCMC
* Seek legal advice before responding to any MCMC or PDRM inquiry about online content

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Broadband complaint resolved via MCMC**

A small business owner had persistent broadband outages for six weeks. His ISP's customer service offered repeated "escalation promises" with no resolution. He filed a complaint with MCMC citing the outages and unresponsiveness. Within five working days, the ISP's technical team visited, identified a faulty line, and repaired it. The ISP also provided a service credit.

**Scenario 2: Scam website reported and blocked**

A consumer received an SMS linking to a fake banking portal. Instead of just deleting it, she reported the URL to MCMC and to PDRM CCID. The URL was blocked by Malaysian ISPs within 48 hours — protecting other potential victims from the same phishing link.

**Scenario 3: CMA investigation prompted review of online posts**

A social media user received a PDRM notice related to a comment made on a public forum under investigation for CMA Section 233. Though the comment was intended as satire, the notice required a response to police. He sought legal advice from the Bar Council Legal Aid Centre. The matter was resolved without prosecution after legal counsel provided context. Awareness that satire and criticism can attract scrutiny — even if ultimately not prosecuted — is relevant for all online users.

### Path A: My telco or internet provider is not resolving my complaint

**Step 1:** File a formal complaint with the provider's dedicated complaints unit — get a reference number.

**Step 2:** Give the provider their stated resolution timeframe (check terms of service — usually 7–14 days).

Identify the content type:

* CSAM → **aduan.skmm.gov.my** AND nearest police station immediately
* Scam website → **ccid.rmp.gov.my** AND **aduan.skmm.gov.my**
* Phishing / cybercrime → **cyber999.my**
* Harassing content targeting you → Report to platform AND **aduan.skmm.gov.my**

Do not ignore it.

Do not respond without understanding the legal implications.

Contact the **Bar Council Legal Aid Centre** (lawyersforall.com.my) or a private lawyer immediately.

## FAQ

**Q: Can MCMC read my private messages?**
MCMC as a regulator does not routinely access private communications. However, under the CMA and with appropriate warrants, law enforcement (PDRM) can access communications records from service providers as part of criminal investigations.

**Q: Can I be investigated for something I posted years ago?**
Technically yes — there is no statute of limitations on CMA Section 233 prosecutions equivalent to civil limitation periods. Historical posts have been cited in investigations. This is particularly relevant for content that gained attention long after posting.

**Q: Does MCMC regulate WhatsApp and Telegram?**
MCMC regulates communications services in Malaysia broadly. WhatsApp and Telegram, as foreign platforms, are not directly licensed by MCMC — but their users in Malaysia are subject to Malaysian law, including the CMA, for content they create or share.

**Q: What if a website I need to use is blocked in Malaysia?**
MCMC's website blocking orders apply to ISPs operating in Malaysia. Access to blocked sites via VPN is technically possible but exists in a grey area under Malaysian law. This page does not advise on circumventing MCMC blocking orders.

**Q: Can MCMC force a social media platform to remove a post?**
MCMC can request content removal from platforms. Platforms are not legally required to comply in the same way ISPs are required to comply with blocking orders — but most major platforms cooperate with lawful government requests where they meet the platform's own legal threshold.
