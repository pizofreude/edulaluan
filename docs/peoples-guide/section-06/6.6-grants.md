## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What is the difference between a grant and a loan?**

* **Grant:** Money given to your business that you do not need to pay back. Usually competitive — not everyone gets it.
* **Loan:** Money borrowed that must be repaid, usually with interest.
* **Equity:** An investor gives you money in exchange for a share (ownership) of your business. You do not repay — but you give up some control.

The government offers all three types. Choosing the right one depends on your stage, what you need the money for, and whether you are willing to share ownership.

### 🧑 Detailed

## Part 1: The Funding Landscape — What You Are Actually Applying For

Before applying for anything, understand what type of funding it is.

### The Four Types

| Type | Repayment | Ownership Impact | Best For |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Grant (geran) | No | None | Specific activities: digitalisation, R&D, market entry |
| Soft loan (pinjaman lembut) | Yes — at concessional rate | None | Working capital, equipment, expansion |
| Equity (ekuiti) | No — but gives up shares | Yes — investor becomes co-owner | High-growth startups needing scale capital |
| Quasi-equity / convertible | Repayable OR converts to shares | Conditional | Startups bridging grant and equity stages |

### Dilutive vs Non-Dilutive

| Concept | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Non-dilutive funding | Grants and loans — your ownership percentage does not change |
| Dilutive funding | Equity investment — investors receive shares; founders' percentage reduces |

**Most first-time business owners should exhaust non-dilutive options first** — grants and concessional loans — before taking equity investment. Equity is expensive in the long run if your business succeeds.

### Grant Conditions — What People Miss

Grants are not free money in the practical sense:

* **Matching requirement:** Many grants require you to co-fund a portion (e.g. 1:1 matching means you put in RM 50,000 to receive RM 50,000)
* **Activity-specific:** Grant funds must be used for the approved purpose — not general operations
* **Reporting obligation:** You must submit progress reports, receipts, and sometimes audited accounts
* **Clawback risk:** Misuse of grant funds can result in repayment demands
* **Timeline commitment:** Most grants have a project completion deadline

## Part 3: Startup Grants and Equity Funding

### Cradle Fund — CIP (Commercialisation of R&D Fund)

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Open to | Malaysian-incorporated tech companies |
| Stage | Pre-revenue to early revenue |
| Type | Non-dilutive grant (CIP Catalyst) and co-investment (CIP Spark) |
| Amount | CIP Catalyst: up to RM 300,000; CIP Spark: up to RM 500,000 |
| Focus | Commercialising technology, IP development |
| Portal | cradle.com.my |

Cradle is one of Malaysia's most respected startup funding bodies. CIP Catalyst is non-dilutive — the grant does not require giving up equity. CIP Spark involves co-investment alongside private investors.

### MRANTI (Malaysia Research Accelerator for Technology and Innovation)

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Open to | Tech startups, researchers, deep tech companies |
| Stage | R&D to commercialisation |
| Type | Grants, co-funding, facility access, ecosystem support |
| Focus | Deep tech — biotech, agritech, advanced manufacturing, cleantech |
| Portal | mranti.my |

MRANTI consolidates several former agencies (MDT, MTDC, MIGHT functions). It is the primary body for deep tech R&D commercialisation support.

### MAVCAP (Malaysia Venture Capital Management)

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Open to | Malaysian tech startups |
| Stage | Early to growth stage |
| Type | Equity investment (dilutive) via fund-of-funds and direct investment |
| Focus | Tech startups with scalable business models |
| Portal | mavcap.com |

MAVCAP is government-owned but operates as a venture capital firm. It invests via fund managers and directly into startups. Receiving MAVCAP funding means giving up equity — it is dilutive.

### PIU (Penjana Impact Unit) and PENJANA-related Programmes

Launched under Malaysia's economic recovery framework, several PENJANA-era programmes continue in modified form:

* **Tech entrepreneur support** via MDEC and MOSTI
* **Digital grants** for SME digitalisation
* **Innovation vouchers** for R&D activities

Check current active programmes at **smecorp.gov.my** and **mdec.my** — PENJANA programmes have evolved and some have been superseded.

## Part 6: Non-Dilutive vs Equity-Based Funding — When to Choose What

### Decision Framework

| Question | If Yes → | If No → |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Do you need capital for a specific, defined activity (equipment, digitisation, marketing)? | Grant or soft loan | Equity or working capital loan |
| Are you at pre-revenue or early-revenue stage? | Cradle CIP, angel investment | Later-stage equity (MAVCAP, VC) |
| Is your business high-growth / scalable to a large market? | Equity investment makes sense | Stick to grants and loans |
| Are you willing to give up partial ownership and accept investor governance? | Equity | Grants and loans only |
| Do you need capital quickly for operations? | SME bank loan (fastest) | Grants (slow — 3–12 months to receive) |
| Do you have strong technology or IP? | MRANTI, Cradle | SME Corp, MDEC digitalisation |

### Honest Assessment of Grant Applications

Grants are valuable but the process has real costs:

| Reality | What It Means |
| --- | --- |
| Application takes 20–60 hours | Staff time has opportunity cost |
| Approval takes 3–12 months | Cannot plan operations around grant timing |
| Matching requirements tie up your own capital | You need cash reserves before applying |
| Reporting is ongoing | Compliance burden during project period |
| Rejection rate is high | Most applicants do not succeed on first try |

This is not a reason to avoid grants — it is a reason to apply strategically and not rely on grant approval for operational survival.

## Part 7: How to Apply — Universal Preparation Checklist

Regardless of which programme you apply for, these documents are almost always required:

| Document | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| SSM registration certificate | Current and not expired |
| MyKad (director / applicant) | |
| Bank statements (6–12 months) | Business account |
| Latest financial statements | Audited or management accounts |
| Business plan | See below |
| Project or activity proposal | Specific to the grant — what you will do with the money |
| Quotations for proposed expenditure | At least 3 quotes for major purchases |
| Supporting letters | From partners, customers, or ecosystem bodies if relevant |

### Business Plan Basics

Most business plans for grant applications should cover:

```
1. Executive summary (1 page)
2. Business description — what you do, for whom, and why
3. Market opportunity — size, target segment, competition
4. Product or service — what makes it different
5. Business model — how you make money
6. Financial projections — 3 years (revenue, costs, profit)
7. Team — founders and key people
8. Funding request — how much, for what, expected impact
```

A plain, honest plan beats a glossy consultant-prepared document that does not reflect the reality of your business.

### Common Reasons Applications Fail

* Incomplete documentation — missing a required document
* Vague use of funds — "general business expenses" is not accepted
* No matching funds available — applicant cannot demonstrate co-funding capacity
* Business not yet registered with SSM
* Financial projections are unrealistic or unsupported
* Business is in an ineligible sector for that specific grant
* Applying to the wrong programme for your stage

## Practical Habits for Funding Applications

Founders who succeed at securing grants and funding tend to:

* Apply to multiple programmes simultaneously — grant processes are slow and success rates are low
* Keep SSM registration and financial records current at all times — applications stall on outdated documents
* Build relationships with SME Corp advisors or ecosystem bodies before applying — they know which programmes are active and realistic for your stage
* Treat grant applications as a pipeline, not a one-shot bet
* Never plan operations around an anticipated grant — apply for what you would do anyway, not as the precondition for doing anything
* Document everything during the grant project period — receipts, reports, photos — rejection of claims on technical documentation grounds is common

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Grant used for wrong purpose**

A food manufacturer received an SME digitalisation grant of RM 5,000 and used part of it to pay staff overtime instead of the approved accounting software subscription. During the post-grant audit, the non-compliant expenditure was flagged and the manufacturer was required to refund that portion. Reading the grant conditions carefully before spending would have prevented this.

**Scenario 2: Equity taken too early**

A two-person app startup accepted angel investment at a low valuation before proving their product worked. They gave up 30% equity for RM 150,000. Eighteen months later, with traction, their second round valued the company at 10 times more. The original 30% would have been worth far more had they exhausted Cradle CIP (non-dilutive) options first. Sequencing funding correctly has long-term ownership consequences.

**Scenario 3: Regional grant missed**

A Penang-based animation studio applied to multiple federal grants but was unaware of CEDC's CAT grant specifically for Penang creative businesses. The CAT grant had lower competition and was purpose-built for their sector. Checking state-level programmes alongside federal ones would have opened a better-matched opportunity.

### Path B: I am an established SME — what funding is available?

**Start at SME Portal:** smecorp.gov.my — filter by your sector and size.

**Key programmes to explore:**

* DISF (Domestic Investment Strategic Fund) for expansion
* CGC loan guarantee for bank financing without collateral
* MDEC ScaleUp if you are a tech-enabled business with RM 1M+ revenue

**State programmes:** Check your SEDC or state economic development body.

---

### Path C: I want to understand if I should take equity investment

Ask yourself:

1. Is your market large enough to justify a 10x return for an investor?
2. Are you comfortable with investor governance and board oversight?
3. Have you exhausted non-dilutive options?
4. Do you need more capital than grants or loans can provide?

If yes to all four — equity may be appropriate. If no to any — exhaust non-dilutive options first.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I apply for a grant and a loan from the same body at the same time?**
Generally yes — grants and loans serve different purposes. A grant might fund a specific activity while a loan covers working capital. Read each programme's terms carefully for any restrictions on simultaneous applications.

**Q: Do I need to be profitable to apply for grants?**
No — most grants are designed for businesses that need support to grow, not those already profitable. However, you typically need to demonstrate that the business is viable and has a realistic plan.

**Q: Is there a central database of all Malaysian grants?**
The closest is **smecorp.gov.my** for MSME programmes and **mymagic.my** for startup programmes. No single portal covers all federal and state grants comprehensively — which is why checking both federal and state bodies is important.

**Q: What is the typical timeline from application to receiving grant funds?**
For most government grants: 3–9 months from application to approval, then a further 1–3 months to first disbursement. Some grants reimburse expenditure rather than paying upfront — meaning you spend first and claim later. Plan cash flow accordingly.

**Q: Can a company with foreign shareholders apply for Malaysian government grants?**
Eligibility varies by programme. Most require Malaysian incorporation (SSM registered Sdn Bhd) but some have Malaysian ownership thresholds (e.g. majority Malaysian shareholding). Check each programme's eligibility criteria — foreign-owned but Malaysian-incorporated companies are sometimes excluded.
