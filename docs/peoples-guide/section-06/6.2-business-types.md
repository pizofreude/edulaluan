## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**Why does business structure matter?**

When you start a business, you need to decide what kind of legal entity it is.

This affects:

* Whether your personal savings and home are at risk if the business fails
* How much tax you pay
* How easy it is to get investors or bank loans
* How much paperwork you need to do every year

Most small businesses start simple — as sole proprietors. As they grow, many convert to a private limited company (Sdn Bhd) for better legal protection.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Choosing a business structure is one of the most consequential early decisions for any business owner. It determines your legal liability, tax obligations, administrative burden, and ability to raise capital.

In Malaysia, the five main structures are registered under two pieces of legislation:

* **Registration of Businesses Act 1956** — sole proprietorships and partnerships
* **Companies Act 2016** — Sdn Bhd, PLT, and Berhad

Each structure has a distinct risk profile, cost, and compliance obligation. This page explains each, compares them side by side, and provides a decision framework.

---

## Part 1: The Five Structures

### 1. Sole Proprietorship (Enterpris Milikan Tunggal)

A business owned and operated by one individual. The simplest and most common starting structure in Malaysia.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Owners | 1 (the proprietor) |
| Legal identity | Not separate from owner |
| Liability | Unlimited — personal assets at risk |
| Tax filing | Form B (personal income tax) |
| Registration | SSM via ezBiz |
| Annual fee | RM 60 (trade name) |
| Renewal | Annual |
| Suitable for | Freelancers, hawkers, home businesses, first-time entrepreneurs |

**Key risk:** If the business incurs debts or faces a lawsuit, the owner's personal savings, property, and assets are all exposed. There is no legal separation.

---

### 2. Partnership (Perkongsian)

A business owned jointly by two to twenty individuals under a partnership agreement.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Owners | 2–20 partners |
| Legal identity | Not separate from partners |
| Liability | Unlimited — all partners personally liable |
| Tax filing | Form P (partnership) + Form B (each partner's share) |
| Registration | SSM via ezBiz |
| Annual fee | RM 60 (trade name) |
| Renewal | Annual |
| Suitable for | Professional practices (pre-PLT), family businesses, small collaborations |

**Key risk:** Partners are jointly and severally liable — one partner's actions can expose all partners' personal assets. A written partnership agreement is strongly recommended even though it is not legally required.

---

### 3. Private Limited Company (Syarikat Sendirian Berhad — Sdn Bhd)

The most popular structure for growing businesses. A separate legal entity from its shareholders.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Owners | 1–50 shareholders |
| Legal identity | Separate legal entity |
| Liability | Limited to share capital — personal assets protected |
| Tax filing | Form C (corporate tax — 17% on first RM 600,000 for qualifying SMEs; 24% above) |
| Registration | SSM via MyCoID |
| Setup cost | RM 1,010 incorporation + company secretary fees |
| Annual obligations | Annual Return, audited accounts, tax filing |
| Suitable for | Growing businesses, businesses seeking investment, government tender applicants |

**Key advantage:** Shareholders' personal assets are protected. If the company fails, creditors can only claim against company assets — not the directors' or shareholders' personal wealth (unless personal guarantees have been given or fraud is involved).

**Key obligation:** A licensed company secretary must be appointed at all times. Annual Returns and audited financial statements are mandatory.

---

### 4. Limited Liability Partnership (Perkongsian Liabiliti Terhad — PLT)

A hybrid structure combining partnership flexibility with limited liability. Introduced under the Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2012.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Owners | 2 or more partners |
| Legal identity | Separate legal entity |
| Liability | Limited — partners not personally liable for others' negligence |
| Tax filing | Form PT (partnership tax) + each partner's personal tax on their share |
| Registration | SSM via MyCoID |
| Setup cost | RM 500 (registration) |
| Annual obligations | Annual Declaration (simpler than Sdn Bhd) |
| Suitable for | Professional service firms (lawyers, accountants, consultants), creative agencies |

**Key advantage:** Partners are not personally liable for the negligent acts of other partners — unlike a conventional partnership. Combined with the operational simplicity of a partnership.

**Limitation:** Cannot issue shares and cannot raise equity capital from investors in the same way as a Sdn Bhd. Less familiar to banks and investors than Sdn Bhd.

---

### 5. Public Company (Syarikat Berhad — Berhad)

A company that can offer shares to the public and may be listed on Bursa Malaysia.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Owners | Unlimited shareholders |
| Legal identity | Separate legal entity |
| Liability | Limited |
| Tax filing | Form C (corporate tax) |
| Registration | SSM via MyCoID |
| Setup cost | Significantly higher — legal, compliance, and listing costs |
| Annual obligations | Extensive — Bursa Malaysia listing rules if listed, Securities Commission oversight |
| Suitable for | Large enterprises seeking public capital markets |

**Relevance for this page:** Most readers starting or growing a business will not use this structure. It is included for completeness. Conversion from Sdn Bhd to Berhad is a milestone event for large, established businesses — typically guided by investment bankers and corporate lawyers.

## Part 3: Decision Matrix — Which Structure Is Right for You?

Work through these questions to find the most appropriate starting structure:

### Question 1: Are you starting alone or with partners?

* Alone → **Sole Proprietorship** or **Sdn Bhd**
* With 1–19 others → **Partnership**, **PLT**, or **Sdn Bhd**

### Question 2: Is protecting your personal assets important?

* Yes → **Sdn Bhd** or **PLT**
* No / not a priority yet → **Sole Proprietorship** or **Partnership**

### Question 3: Do you plan to seek external investment (angel investors, VCs)?

* Yes → **Sdn Bhd** (only structure that supports equity investment)
* No → Any structure depending on other factors

### Question 4: Are you in a professional services field (law, accounting, consulting)?

* Yes → **PLT** is purpose-built; also consider **Sdn Bhd**
* No → **Sdn Bhd** or **Sole Proprietorship** depending on scale

### Question 5: How much compliance overhead can you manage right now?

* Minimal overhead preferred → **Sole Proprietorship** (start here, convert later)
* Willing to invest in compliance for protection → **Sdn Bhd**

### Recommended Starting Points by Situation

| Situation | Recommended Structure |
| --- | --- |
| Testing a business idea, low revenue | Sole Proprietorship |
| Freelancer or consultant formalising | Sole Proprietorship or PLT |
| Two friends starting a café together | Partnership (short term) → Sdn Bhd |
| Professional firm (lawyers, accountants) | PLT |
| Startup seeking investors | Sdn Bhd |
| E-commerce business scaling up | Sdn Bhd |
| Side income becoming a real business | Sole Proprietorship → Sdn Bhd when ready |
| Family business with multiple members | Sdn Bhd |

## Part 4: Converting Between Structures

### Why Convert?

Most businesses start simple and convert to a more formal structure as they grow. Common triggers:

* Revenue has grown substantially and tax optimisation favours Sdn Bhd
* A business partner or investor requires a Sdn Bhd
* Personal liability exposure has become a concern
* Government tenders or contracts require Sdn Bhd status
* The business is hiring employees and a formal structure is needed

### Sole Proprietor → Sdn Bhd

This is the most common conversion in Malaysia.

| Step | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Incorporate new Sdn Bhd | Via MyCoID — full new registration |
| 2. Transfer business assets | Formally transfer from sole prop to company |
| 3. Update contracts and agreements | Notify clients, suppliers, and banks of new entity |
| 4. Open new corporate bank account | Sole prop account cannot transfer to Sdn Bhd |
| 5. Register new entity with LHDN, EPF, SOCSO | New tax and contribution registrations required |
| 6. Deregister sole proprietorship | At SSM once transition is complete |

**Important:** There is no direct "conversion" process in Malaysian law. The Sdn Bhd is a new legal entity. Assets, contracts, and liabilities must be formally transferred — a process that benefits from legal and accounting advice.

### Partnership → PLT

Existing partnerships can convert to PLT under the Limited Liability Partnerships Act 2012. SSM provides a formal conversion pathway — consult the SSM portal or a licensed company secretary for the current procedure.

### Sdn Bhd → Berhad (Public Company)

This is a complex corporate exercise requiring Securities Commission involvement, investment bank advisory, and legal counsel. Not a DIY process — beyond the scope of this page.

## Practical Habits When Choosing a Structure

Business owners who choose wisely tend to:

* Start with the simplest structure that meets their current needs — complexity can be added as the business grows
* Consult a company secretary or accountant before incorporating Sdn Bhd — the compliance costs are real and ongoing
* Avoid registering a Sdn Bhd prematurely — for a one-person freelance business, the compliance overhead rarely justifies the cost until revenue is significant
* Get a written partnership agreement even for informal partnerships — the law does not require it but disputes between partners without one are costly
* Factor in the cost of a company secretary (RM 600–RM 2,400/year) and auditor (RM 1,500–RM 5,000/year) when deciding on Sdn Bhd

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Sole proprietor, personal assets exposed**

A sole proprietor running a small construction subcontracting business was sued by a client over a disputed project. Because the business was unincorporated, the court judgment was against him personally. His car and personal savings were seized to satisfy the judgment. Had he incorporated as Sdn Bhd, only the company's assets would have been at risk.

**Scenario 2: Premature Sdn Bhd incorporation**

A freelance graphic designer incorporated a Sdn Bhd on a friend's advice before her income justified it. She spent RM 3,000 on company secretary fees, RM 2,000 on audited accounts, and filed tax returns for two years before dissolving the company — having earned less than RM 40,000 per year. A sole proprietorship would have been far more appropriate at that stage.

**Scenario 3: Partnership without agreement**

Two brothers started a bakery as a partnership without a written agreement. When they disagreed on expansion direction, there was no document to resolve the dispute. The partnership dissolved acrimoniously, with significant legal costs on both sides. A simple partnership agreement covering profit sharing, decision-making, and dissolution would have prevented this.

**Are you in a professional services field (law, accounting, consulting)?**

* Yes → Consider **PLT** — purpose-built for professional partnerships.

## FAQ

**Q: Can a foreigner be a shareholder or director of a Malaysian Sdn Bhd?**
Yes, with conditions. Foreign equity is permitted in most sectors but may be restricted or capped in strategic industries (banking, media, certain professional services). Directors must include at least one Malaysian resident.

**Q: Can a Sdn Bhd have just one shareholder and one director?**
Yes. Since the Companies Act 2016, a Sdn Bhd can be incorporated with a single shareholder who is also the sole director — effectively a one-person company with limited liability.

**Q: Is a PLT suitable for a tech startup?**
Generally no. PLTs cannot issue shares and are not structured for equity investment. Investors expect a Sdn Bhd. PLT is best for professional service partnerships, not growth-stage startups.

**Q: Do I need a lawyer to incorporate a Sdn Bhd?**
Not legally required, but a licensed company secretary is mandatory. Many company secretaries handle the full incorporation process without requiring a separate lawyer. For complex arrangements (multiple founders, special share classes, investor terms), legal advice is worth the cost.

**Q: What happens to my sole proprietorship's contracts when I convert to Sdn Bhd?**
Contracts signed under the sole proprietorship remain with you personally — they do not automatically transfer to the new company. Each contract must be novated (assigned with the other party's consent) to the Sdn Bhd. This is a real practical challenge and a reason to convert before entering major contracts rather than after.
