## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**What taxes does a business pay in Malaysia?**

A business in Malaysia may need to deal with several types of tax:

* **Income tax** — on profits the business makes
* **SST** — collected from customers on sales or services (if above a threshold)
* **PCB** — tax deducted from employees' salaries and sent to LHDN

Each has its own registration, filing, and payment deadlines.

Missing deadlines costs money. Good records prevent bigger problems.

---

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Malaysian business tax operates on **self-assessment** — you declare, calculate, and pay your own taxes. LHDN reviews and may audit. Getting it right from the start is far cheaper than corrections after the fact.

This page covers business tax from a practical operating standpoint — what you need to register for, when to file, what you can deduct, how to manage staff tax obligations, and how to keep records that protect you. For deeper technical coverage of tax types, see **Section 3.6 — Business Tax Basics**.

## Part 1: Income Tax by Business Structure

### Overview

| Structure | Tax Form | Tax Basis | Rate |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Sole proprietor | Form B | Personal income tax (progressive) | 0–30% |
| Partnership | Form P + Form B (each partner) | Personal income tax on each partner's share | 0–30% |
| Sdn Bhd | Form C | Corporate tax | 17% on first RM 600,000 (qualifying SME); 24% above |
| PLT | Form PT + Form B (each partner) | Personal income tax on each partner's share | 0–30% |

### Sole Proprietor and Partnership Tax

Business profit is added to your personal income and taxed at the progressive individual rate. This means:

* Low-revenue businesses may pay little or no tax
* High-revenue sole proprietors can face rates up to 30% on the upper portion
* This is one of the key triggers for converting to Sdn Bhd — corporate tax at 17% can be lower than personal rates at the same income level

### Sdn Bhd Tax

A qualifying SME Sdn Bhd pays **17% on the first RM 600,000** of chargeable income and **24% on the remainder**.

To qualify for the 17% rate, the company must:

* Have paid-up capital of RM 2.5 million or less
* Not be more than 50% owned by a related company with paid-up capital exceeding RM 2.5 million
* Have gross business income not exceeding RM 50 million

This rate advantage is a significant reason growing sole proprietorships convert to Sdn Bhd.

## Part 2: SST Registration Walkthrough

### When SST Applies

**Service Tax** applies to businesses providing taxable services. Registration is mandatory when:

* Taxable service revenue exceeds **RM 500,000 in any 12-month period**

**Sales Tax** applies to manufacturers of taxable goods. Registration is mandatory when:

* Taxable goods sales exceed **RM 500,000 annually**

### Step-by-Step SST Registration

```
Step 1: Determine if your services / goods are taxable
        → Check the Service Tax (Rate of Tax) Order and Sales Tax (Goods Exempt From Tax) Order
        → Not all services are taxable — professional services, IT, F&B above threshold are taxable
        → Financial services, education, healthcare are generally exempt

Step 2: Track your 12-month rolling revenue
        → Monitor monthly — the threshold is rolling, not calendar year
        → Once threshold is crossed, registration must be done immediately

Step 3: Register via MySSTportal
        → mysst.customs.gov.my
        → Prepare: SSM certificate, business details, financial records, MyKad

Step 4: Receive SST registration number
        → Typically within 1–5 working days of complete application

Step 5: Start charging SST on invoices
        → Service Tax: 8% on taxable services
        → Sales Tax: 5% or 10% depending on goods category

Step 6: File returns every 2 months (taxable period)
        → Due by the last day of the month following the taxable period
        → Remit collected SST to RMCD at the same time
```

### Common Mistake: Starting to Charge SST Before Registration

You must be **registered** before charging SST to customers. Charging SST without a registration number is an offence under the Service Tax Act 2018. Conversely, failing to register once the threshold is crossed is also an offence. Monitor your revenue and register proactively.

### SST on Invoices

Once registered, your invoices must show:

* Your SST registration number
* The taxable amount
* The SST amount (8% for service tax)
* The total amount payable

## Part 3: Tax Planning Basics for Business Owners

Tax planning is legal and encouraged. Tax evasion is illegal and prosecuted.

### The Core Principle

**Reduce chargeable income through legitimate deductions and structure choices — before the tax is due, not after.**

### Key Planning Levers for Business Owners

| Strategy | How It Works |
| --- | --- |
| Maximise deductible expenses | Ensure all legitimate business expenses are claimed — do not over-claim, but do not under-claim either |
| Capital allowance claims | Equipment, machinery, and vehicles qualify for annual allowances reducing taxable income |
| Timing of income and expenses | Deferring income to the next tax year or accelerating deductible expenses into the current year |
| Structure choice | At higher income levels, Sdn Bhd at 17% may be more efficient than personal rates |
| Reinvestment allowance | Available for manufacturing companies that reinvest in qualifying assets |
| Pioneer status / investment tax allowance | For qualifying industries — substantial tax relief available; apply via MIDA |

### When to Engage a Tax Agent

Self-assessment is manageable for simple businesses. Engaging a **LHDN-registered tax agent** is strongly recommended when:

* Revenue exceeds RM 500,000
* You have multiple income streams
* You are considering structure changes or major capital investments
* You receive an LHDN audit notice
* You operate in a sector with complex deductibility rules (property, construction, financial services)

Tax agent fees are themselves a deductible business expense.

## Part 4: Common Deductible Business Expenses

Under Malaysian income tax law, expenses are deductible if they are:

* Wholly and exclusively incurred in the production of income
* Not capital in nature (capital items go through capital allowance instead)
* Not specifically prohibited by the Income Tax Act

### Commonly Deductible

| Expense Category | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Staff salaries, EPF, SOCSO | All employer contributions fully deductible |
| Office rent | Fully deductible for business premises |
| Utilities (business premises) | Electricity, water, internet — business portion |
| Business travel | Flights, accommodation, transport for business purposes — keep receipts |
| Marketing and advertising | Digital ads, print, event sponsorship |
| Professional fees | Accountant, lawyer, company secretary, consultant fees |
| Insurance (business) | Business premises, professional indemnity, fire insurance |
| Subscriptions and software | Business tools, professional memberships, SaaS subscriptions |
| Bad debts (written off) | Specific bad debts that have been genuinely written off |
| Training and development | Staff training costs |
| Bank charges | Business account fees and charges |

### Not Deductible

| Expense | Reason |
| --- | --- |
| Personal expenses | Must be wholly for business |
| Entertainment (generally) | 50% restriction applies to entertainment expenses |
| Fines and penalties | Including LHDN late payment penalties |
| Capital expenditure | Goes through capital allowance instead |
| Personal vehicle (private use portion) | Only the business use portion qualifies |
| Income tax payments | Not deductible against income |

### Capital Allowance (for Assets)

Business assets (equipment, computers, machinery, commercial vehicles) are not expensed immediately — instead, they qualify for **annual allowance (AA)** and **initial allowance (IA)** under the Income Tax Act:

| Asset Type | Initial Allowance | Annual Allowance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Plant and machinery | 20% | 14% |
| Office equipment / computers | 20% | 10–20% |
| Commercial motor vehicles | 20% | 20% |
| Industrial buildings | — | 3% |

Keep purchase invoices and asset registers — LHDN may require these during audit.

## Part 5: Tax Obligations When Hiring Staff

Hiring your first employee triggers several simultaneous obligations beyond just paying a salary.

### Monthly Tax Deduction (PCB — Potongan Cukai Bulanan)

PCB is the employer's obligation to deduct income tax from employees' monthly salaries and remit it to LHDN.

| Step | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Register as employer with LHDN | Via mytax.hasil.gov.my → Employer Registration |
| 2. Calculate PCB monthly | Using LHDN's PCB calculator or payroll software |
| 3. Deduct from employee salary | Before paying net salary |
| 4. Remit to LHDN | By the **15th of the following month** |
| 5. Issue EA Form annually | By **last day of February** each year |

**Failure to remit PCB on time** results in penalties. The penalty is imposed on the employer — not the employee.

### EA Form

The EA Form is the annual earnings statement issued by every employer to every employee. It summarises:

* Total gross income for the year
* Total PCB deducted
* EPF contributions
* Other deductions

Employees use the EA Form to file their personal income tax (Form BE or Form B). Employers who fail to issue EA Forms by the deadline face penalties.

### Full Employer Obligations Summary

| Obligation | Deadline | Where |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Register as employer (LHDN) | Before first payroll | mytax.hasil.gov.my |
| Register for EPF | Before first payroll | kwsp.gov.my |
| Register for SOCSO | Before first payroll | perkeso.gov.my |
| Register for EIS | Before first payroll | perkeso.gov.my |
| Monthly PCB remittance | 15th of following month | mytax.hasil.gov.my |
| Monthly EPF contribution | 15th of following month | kwsp.gov.my |
| Monthly SOCSO + EIS | 15th of following month | perkeso.gov.my |
| Annual EA Form to employees | Last day of February | Physical or e-EA |
| Annual E Form (employer return) | 31 March | mytax.hasil.gov.my |

Cross-reference: **Section 2.4 (EPF)**, **Section 2.5 (SOCSO)**, **Section 2.6 (EIS)**, **Section 2.7 (Understanding Your Payslip)**

## Part 6: Record Keeping Requirements

### Legal Requirement

Under the Income Tax Act 1967, businesses must keep records for a minimum of **7 years** from the end of the year of assessment. LHDN can audit any year within this window.

### What to Keep

| Document Type | Why | Retention |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sales invoices and receipts | Proof of income | 7 years |
| Purchase invoices and receipts | Proof of deductible expenses | 7 years |
| Bank statements (business account) | Income and expense verification | 7 years |
| Payroll records | PCB, EPF, SOCSO compliance | 7 years |
| EA Forms issued | Employer obligation | 7 years |
| Asset purchase invoices | Capital allowance claims | 7 years |
| Contracts and agreements | Income and expense basis | 7 years |
| SST returns and records | RMCD requirement | 7 years |
| Company resolutions and minutes | Sdn Bhd obligation | Permanently |

### Practical Record Keeping Systems

| Approach | Best For | Tools |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cloud accounting software | Most businesses | QuickBooks, Xero, SQL Accounting, AutoCount |
| Spreadsheet | Very small businesses | Google Sheets, Excel |
| Physical folders + digital scan | Hybrid approach | Scan with CamScanner, save to Google Drive |
| Outsourced bookkeeper | Businesses without in-house capacity | Monthly fee, usually RM 300–RM 800/month |

**The most important habit:** Reconcile your bank statement against your records **monthly** — not at year-end. Year-end reconciliations on 12 months of unsorted receipts are expensive and error-prone.

## Practical Habits for Business Tax Compliance

Business owners who stay tax-compliant tend to:

* Set up a dedicated business bank account from day one — mixing personal and business accounts makes bookkeeping exponentially harder
* Reconcile records monthly — not at year-end
* File and pay on time even if the amount is uncertain — late filing penalties are worse than underpayment
* Engage a bookkeeper or accounting software before revenue makes manual tracking painful — not after
* Keep all receipts digitally — paper fades; a phone photo uploaded to cloud storage takes seconds and satisfies LHDN requirements
* Build a tax reserve — set aside 17–25% of net profit monthly into a separate savings account for tax payments

---

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: PCB not remitted, penalty compounding**

A small business owner hired two staff and ran payroll but did not realise PCB had to be remitted to LHDN monthly. By the time LHDN sent a notice 18 months later, penalties had accumulated to more than the original PCB amount. Setting up employer registration and a payroll calendar on day one would have cost nothing.

**Scenario 2: SST threshold crossed, not registered**

A digital marketing consultancy crossed the RM 500,000 service revenue threshold in March but only discovered SST obligations in October during an accountant review. Back-calculating and remitting six months of unregistered SST — plus penalties — was a significant cash flow shock. Monthly revenue tracking against the threshold would have triggered timely registration.

**Scenario 3: Capital allowance not claimed**

A sole proprietor bought RM 40,000 of equipment for her studio and wrote off the full amount as an expense in her accounts. Her accountant later pointed out that capital allowance claims were the correct treatment — and that she had overclaimed, creating a tax risk. Understanding the deductible vs capital expenditure distinction before filing saved her from an incorrect return.

### Path A: I just started a sole proprietorship — what tax do I need to set up?

**Step 1:** Register as a taxpayer with LHDN at **mytax.hasil.gov.my** if not already registered.

**Step 2:** Track monthly revenue — when you approach RM 500,000 in taxable services, prepare to register for SST.

**Step 3:** File Form B annually by **30 June**.

**Step 4:** Keep all receipts and records — 7 years minimum.

---

### Path B: I just incorporated a Sdn Bhd — what tax setup do I need?

**Step 1:** Register company for tax at **mytax.hasil.gov.my**.

**Step 2:** Submit CP204 (tax estimate) 30 days before financial year begins.

**Step 3:** Pay CP204 instalments monthly throughout the year.

**Step 4:** File Form C within 7 months of financial year end.

**Step 5:** Monitor SST threshold if providing taxable services.

**Step 6:** Appoint a tax agent — Form C and audit requirements make this strongly advisable.

---

### Path C: I want to hire my first employee

**Step 1:** Register as employer with LHDN (mytax.hasil.gov.my).

**Step 2:** Register with EPF (kwsp.gov.my).

**Step 3:** Register with SOCSO and EIS (perkeso.gov.my).

**Step 4:** Set up payroll — calculate PCB using LHDN's calculator or payroll software.

**Step 5:** Remit PCB, EPF, SOCSO, and EIS by the **15th of each following month**.

**Step 6:** Issue EA Forms to all employees by the **last day of February** each year.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I deduct my home office expenses as a sole proprietor?**
Yes — a proportional amount based on the area used for business relative to total home area. Document the calculation. Internet, utilities, and rent (or notional rent) can be proportionally claimed. Be conservative and consistent — excessive home office claims attract scrutiny.

**Q: Do I need to register for SST if I only sell to overseas clients?**
Exported services are generally zero-rated or out of scope for SST — they do not count toward the RM 500,000 threshold and are not subject to service tax when consumed outside Malaysia. Confirm the specific position for your service type with RMCD or a tax agent.

**Q: What if I cannot afford to pay my tax bill?**
Contact LHDN before the deadline. LHDN offers instalment payment arrangements — these reduce penalty exposure compared to ignoring the bill. Filing on time even if you cannot pay immediately reduces penalties significantly.

**Q: Is bookkeeping software mandatory?**
No — LHDN accepts manual records if complete and accurate. However, cloud accounting software reduces errors, makes audit responses far easier, and typically pays for itself in time savings within months.

**Q: What triggers an LHDN audit?**
Common triggers include: large deduction claims relative to revenue, significant year-on-year income fluctuations, industry benchmarking anomalies, tip-offs, and random selection. Good records and conservative, consistent filings are the best audit protection.
