## Audience Tabs

### 🧒 Simple

**Why do you need a licence on top of SSM registration?**

SSM registration says: this business exists.

A licence says: this business is allowed to operate in this place, doing this activity.

They are different things from different bodies.

A restaurant needs:
* SSM registration (one-time, annual renewal)
* Business operating licence from the local council
* Food premise licence from the local council / MOH
* Signboard licence
* Halal certification (optional but often commercially important)

Missing any one of these can result in fines or forced closure.

### 🧑 Detailed

## Overview

Licensing in Malaysia operates at two levels:

* **Local authority level** — the business operating licence and signboard licence issued by the Pihak Berkuasa Tempatan (PBT) where the business premises are located
* **Federal or state agency level** — industry-specific licences issued by the relevant regulator (KKM, MOE, PDRM, BNM, Customs, etc.)

Most businesses need at least the local authority licence. Many need additional industry-specific licences depending on what they do. Some need both federal and state approval.

The key principle: **your licensing obligations are determined by what you do and where you do it** — not by your business structure or size.

---

## Part 1: Business Operating Licence (Lesen Perniagaan)

### What It Is

The business operating licence (also called Lesen Premis Perniagaan or Lesen Perniagaan) is issued by the local authority (PBT) where your business premises are located.

It authorises you to operate a business at a specific physical address.

**Every business with physical premises requires one.** Home-based businesses operating discreetly may sometimes operate without one in practice — but technically, any commercial activity from a premises requires a licence.

### Which PBT Issues Your Licence

Your licence comes from the local authority of the area your premises are in:

| Area | Local Authority (PBT) |
| --- | --- |
| Kuala Lumpur | Dewan Bandaraya Kuala Lumpur (DBKL) |
| Petaling Jaya | Majlis Bandaraya Petaling Jaya (MBPJ) |
| Subang Jaya | Majlis Bandaraya Subang Jaya (MBSJ) |
| Shah Alam | Majlis Bandaraya Shah Alam (MBSA) |
| Klang | Majlis Bandaraya Klang (MBK) |
| Johor Bahru | Majlis Bandaraya Johor Bahru (MBJB) |
| George Town (Penang) | Majlis Bandaraya Pulau Pinang (MBPP) |
| Ipoh | Majlis Bandaraya Ipoh (MBI) |
| Kuching | Dewan Bandaraya Kuching Utara (DBKU) |
| Kota Kinabalu | Dewan Bandaraya Kota Kinabalu (DBKK) |
| Other areas | District or municipal council of that area |

To find your PBT: search "[your area] local council" or check the Ministry of Housing and Local Government directory at **kpkt.gov.my**.

### Application Process

| Step | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Prepare documents | SSM certificate, tenancy agreement, building plan / layout, MyKad |
| 2. Submit application | Via PBT counter or online portal (varies by PBT) |
| 3. Site inspection | PBT officer inspects premises for compliance (building, fire safety, zoning) |
| 4. Pay fee | Varies by PBT, business category, and premises size |
| 5. Receive licence | Typically 2–8 weeks depending on PBT and completeness of application |

### Fees

Fees vary significantly by PBT and business category. Indicative range:

| Business Type | Annual Fee (approx.) |
| --- | --- |
| Small retail / service | RM 100–RM 500 |
| Food and beverage premises | RM 200–RM 1,000 |
| Professional office | RM 150–RM 600 |
| Factory / warehouse | RM 500–RM 3,000+ |

Check your specific PBT's fee schedule — it is published on most PBT websites.

### Renewal

Most business operating licences are valid for **1 year** and must be renewed before expiry. Late renewal attracts penalties. Operating with an expired licence is equivalent to operating without one — enforcement applies.

## Part 2: Signboard Licence (Lesen Papan Tanda)

### What It Is

Any external signage promoting or identifying a business — including LED signs, banners, and window lettering — requires a signboard licence from the local authority.

This is separate from the business operating licence even though it is issued by the same PBT.

### Key Points

| Item | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Who needs it | Any business displaying external signage |
| Issuing body | Same PBT as business operating licence |
| Fee basis | Based on signboard size (per square foot / metre) |
| Renewal | Annual |
| BM requirement | Bahasa Malaysia must appear prominently — regulations specify font size relative to other languages |

### BM Language Requirement

Under the National Language Act and local authority regulations, business signboards must display the business name in **Bahasa Malaysia prominently**. Other languages may appear but must be smaller than the BM text.

This is actively enforced — PBT officers do conduct signboard inspections, and non-compliant signs can result in compounds.

## Part 3: Industry-Specific Licences

### Sector-by-Sector Breakdown

| Sector | Licence / Permit | Issuing Body | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Food and beverage** | Food premise licence | Local authority (PBT) | Required for any premises preparing or selling food |
| **Food and beverage** | Food handling certificate | District Health Office (PKD) | Required for food handlers personally |
| **Healthcare (clinic)** | Private healthcare facility licence | KKM | Under Private Healthcare Facilities and Services Act 1998 |
| **Pharmacy** | Pharmacy licence | Pharmacy Board Malaysia | Pharmacist must be registered |
| **Childcare centre** | Taska licence | Jabatan Kebajikan Masyarakat (JKM) | Under Child Care Centre Act 1984 |
| **Private school / tuition** | Private education institution registration | Ministry of Education (MOE) | SERI registration for tuition centres |
| **Money lending** | Moneylender's licence | Kementerian Pembangunan Usahawan / State | Under Moneylenders Act 1951 |
| **Money services** | Money services licence | Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) | Required for remittance, currency exchange |
| **Security services** | Security agency licence | PDRM | Under Private Agencies Act 1971 |
| **Import / export** | Import / export permit | Royal Malaysian Customs (JKDM) / MITI | Varies by goods category |
| **Alcohol retail** | Liquor licence | State authority / local authority | Separate from business operating licence |
| **Tobacco retail** | Retail tobacco dealer licence | Local authority / MOH | Under Control of Tobacco Products Regulations |
| **Travel agency** | Travel agency licence | Tourism Malaysia / MOTAC | Under Tourism Industry Act 1992 |
| **Real estate** | Real estate agent licence | Board of Valuers, Appraisers, Estate Agents and Property Managers (BOVAEA) | Individual agent registration required |
| **Construction** | CIDB registration | Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) | For contractors bidding on construction projects |
| **Pest control** | Pest control operator licence | Pesticides Board of Malaysia | Under Pesticides Act 1974 |
| **Veterinary / pet shop** | Premises licence | Department of Veterinary Services (DVS) | For pet businesses, livestock handling |
| **Driving school** | Driving school licence | JPJ | Under Road Transport Act 1987 |
| **Logistics / transport** | Goods vehicle permit | Commercial Vehicle Licensing Board (CVLB) / SPAD | For haulage and freight businesses |
| **Financial services** | Various — by product type | Bank Negara Malaysia / Securities Commission | Banks, insurers, fund managers, brokers |

This table is not exhaustive. Highly regulated industries (aviation, telecommunications, broadcasting, oil and gas) have additional sector-specific licensing frameworks not covered here.

**If your business falls into a regulated sector and is not on this list:** contact the relevant ministry or regulatory body directly, or consult a business lawyer with sector experience.

## Part 4: Halal Certification (JAKIM)

### What It Is

Halal certification is not legally mandatory for most businesses — but it is commercially significant for reaching Muslim consumers, supplying to government bodies, hospitals, and schools, and exporting to Muslim-majority markets.

In Malaysia, halal certification is administered by **JAKIM (Jabatan Kemajuan Islam Malaysia)** at the federal level.

### Who Should Consider It

| Business Type | Relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Food manufacturing | High — major markets and retailers require it |
| Food and beverage (restaurants, cafes) | Medium — Muslim consumer confidence |
| Food catering (government, schools) | Required for most government contracts |
| Cosmetics and personal care | Growing requirement for Muslim-majority markets |
| Pharmaceuticals | Required for certain Muslim markets |
| Logistics (food transport / cold chain) | Required if handling halal goods |
| Export to Muslim-majority countries | Often required by import regulations |

### JAKIM vs State Islamic Authorities

| Body | Jurisdiction | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| JAKIM | Federal — Malaysia-wide recognition | Required for export and government supply |
| State Islamic Authorities (MAIN) | State-level — e.g. JAIS (Selangor), JAIPP (Penang) | Valid within the state; some businesses start here |

For export or national distribution, JAKIM certification is the standard. For local operations within a state, state authority certification may suffice.

### Application Process (JAKIM)

1. Register on **halal.gov.my** — Malaysia's integrated halal management system
2. Submit application with supporting documents (premises layout, ingredient lists, supplier information, SOPs)
3. JAKIM conducts audit and inspection
4. Certification valid for **2 years**; renewal required before expiry
5. Certified businesses are listed on the public JAKIM halal directory

### Fees

Fees vary by business category, premises size, and product scope. Check current rates at **halal.gov.my**.

### Beware of Fake Halal Logos

Using a halal logo without JAKIM or recognised state authority certification is an offence under the Trade Descriptions Act. Verify any halal claim by searching the JAKIM directory at **halal.gov.my**.

## Part 5: Import and Export Permits

### Overview

Import and export of most goods in Malaysia requires compliance with customs regulations and, for controlled goods, specific permits.

| Type | What It Covers | Issuing Body |
| --- | --- | --- |
| General customs declaration | All imports and exports | Royal Malaysian Customs (JKDM) |
| Import permit (controlled goods) | Goods on the controlled list (food, chemicals, weapons, vehicles) | MITI / JKDM / relevant ministry |
| Export permit (controlled goods) | Petroleum products, timber, wildlife, certain food items | MITI / relevant ministry |
| ATA Carnet | Temporary import of goods (exhibitions, samples) | Malaysia Chambers of Commerce |
| Halal export certification | Export to Muslim-majority markets | JAKIM |

### Common Import-Controlled Goods

* Firearms and ammunition (strict controls — PDRM)
* Food items (biosecurity inspection — MOA / DVS)
* Pharmaceuticals (NPRA registration required)
* Electrical and electronics (SIRIM approval)
* Motor vehicles (Customs approval)

### How to Get Started

Register with the **MyTrade portal** (mytradelink.gov.my) for import/export facilitation — this is the primary gateway for customs declarations and permit applications in Malaysia.

For businesses new to import/export, engaging a **licensed customs agent (forwarding agent)** significantly reduces the complexity of the first few transactions.

## Part 6: Consequences of Operating Without a Licence

### Enforcement Bodies

| Violation | Enforced By |
| --- | --- |
| No business operating licence | Local authority (PBT) enforcement unit |
| No signboard licence / non-compliant signboard | Local authority |
| No food premise licence | Local authority / MOH district office |
| No industry-specific licence | Relevant regulator (PDRM, KKM, BNM, etc.) |
| Fake halal certification | Ministry of Domestic Trade (KPDNHEP) |
| Unlicensed import / export | Royal Malaysian Customs |

### Typical Consequences

| Violation | Possible Consequence |
| --- | --- |
| No business operating licence | Compound fine; premises closure order |
| Expired licence | Compound fine; same as no licence |
| Non-compliant signboard | Signboard removal order; fine |
| No food handling certificate | Individual fine; premises suspension |
| Operating healthcare facility without licence | Prosecution under PHFSA 1998; significant fines; closure |
| Unlicensed money services | Criminal prosecution under FSA 2013 |
| Fake halal logo | Prosecution under Trade Descriptions Act 2011; fine and imprisonment |

### Compound Fines

Most local authority violations are initially resolved via **compound (kompaun)** — an administrative fine paid in lieu of prosecution. Paying a compound does not legalise the operation — you must still rectify the violation and obtain the proper licence.

Repeat offences or failure to pay compounds escalate to prosecution in the Magistrates' Court.

**Important:** Enforcement raids do happen — particularly for food premises, construction sites, and industries with active regulatory oversight. "Everyone does it" is not a defence.

## Practical Habits for Licence Compliance

Business owners who stay compliant tend to:

* Apply for the business operating licence before opening — not after enforcement finds them
* Keep a licence file with copies of all active licences, renewal dates highlighted
* Set calendar reminders 2 months before each licence renewal date
* Confirm what licences are required before signing a tenancy agreement — some premises are not zoned for your intended use
* Check JAKIM's directory before claiming halal status in marketing materials — even well-intentioned claims without certification are an offence

## Why This Matters

**Scenario 1: Café closed on opening week**

A couple opened a café after months of renovation. They had SSM registration and a tenancy agreement but had not applied for a business operating licence or food premise licence. A PBT enforcement visit in week two issued a closure notice. The café reopened six weeks later after licensing was completed — losing significant early momentum and revenue.

**Scenario 2: Signboard compound despite being licensed**

A beauty salon had a valid business operating licence but displayed a signboard where the English name was larger than the BM name. A PBT enforcement officer issued a compound fine and a rectification order. A simple awareness of the BM signboard rule would have prevented this.

**Scenario 3: Fake halal claim, serious consequences**

A food manufacturer used a halal-style logo on packaging without JAKIM certification, assuming customers would not check. KPDNHEP received a complaint, investigated, and prosecuted under the Trade Descriptions Act. The fine and reputational damage far exceeded the cost of legitimate JAKIM certification.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I operate from home without a business operating licence?**
Technically, any commercial activity from a premises requires a licence. In practice, small home-based businesses (online selling, home baking) often operate without one. However, if a neighbour complains or a PBT officer visits, the absence of a licence is an enforceable violation. Check your local authority's home-based business policy — some PBTs have simplified home business permits.

**Q: My PBT licence application has been pending for months — what can I do?**
Follow up directly with the PBT's licensing department. If there is a compliance issue (zoning, building plans, fire safety), it will be communicated during the inspection. If the delay is administrative, escalate to the department head. eAduan (eaduan.mampu.gov.my) can be used for complaints about prolonged government service delays.

**Q: Does my online-only business need a licence?**
If you operate entirely online with no physical customer-facing premises, a business operating licence may not be required. However, SSM registration is still required, and industry-specific regulations (financial services, healthcare, education content) may still apply regardless of whether you operate online or offline.

**Q: How long does JAKIM halal certification take?**
Typically 3–6 months for a standard food business application. Complex manufacturing or multi-site operations take longer. Plan for this lead time before any halal-related marketing or government supply commitments.

**Q: What is the difference between a food premise licence and a food handling certificate?**
The food premise licence is issued to the **business** — authorising the premises to prepare or sell food. The food handling certificate is issued to **individual food handlers** — confirming they have undergone food safety training. Both are required for food businesses; they come from different authorities and serve different purposes.
